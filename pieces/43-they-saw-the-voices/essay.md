# They Saw the Voices — Lissajous at Sinai

**Exodus 20:15 (Hebrew numbering; 20:18 in many English Bibles)**

> *וְכָל-הָעָם רֹאִים אֶת-הַקּוֹלֹת וְאֶת-הַלַּפִּידִם וְאֵת קוֹל הַשֹּׁפָר וְאֶת-הָהָר עָשֵׁן*
>
> "And all the people saw the thunderings, and the lightnings, and the voice of the shofar,
> and the mountain smoking."

The Hebrew verb is unambiguous: *ro'im*, they saw. The object is *ha-kolot*, the voices, the sounds, the thunderings. They did not hear them — the text uses the verb of sight. The verse appears as 20:18 in many English Bibles because the medieval manuscripts underlying the two traditions (Hebrew *Masoretic* and the Latin *Vulgate* tradition reflected in most Protestant translations) divided certain verses differently; the Hebrew numbering is 20:15. The event it describes is the same: at Sinai, Israel stood before the mountain and saw what should have been heard.

---

## The Mechilta and the Visible Word

The *Mechilta d'Rabbi Ishmael* (Tractate Yitro, Parasha 9) refuses to explain this verse away as poetic hyperbole. It confronts the plain meaning directly: the *kolot* at Sinai were visible. The midrash describes how each of the ten utterances (*dibrot*) issued from the mouth of the Holy One, split into seventy tongues simultaneously — one for every nation of the world — and that Israel could see these utterances as they went out and inscribed themselves upon the tablets. The hearing and the seeing were not two separate events. They were a single act of perception in which the categories of sense collapsed into one another.

*Midrash Tanchuma* (Yitro 13) amplifies this: "they saw what is normally heard and heard what is normally seen." This is not presented as paradox requiring resolution. It is presented as the character of the event itself. Sinai was constitutively synaesthetic.

The Talmud (*Shabbat* 88b) preserves a further image: the voice of God at Sinai was structured in such a way that each individual Israelite received it according to their capacity — the elders heard it one way, the young men another, the women another, the children another. A single utterance reached every person differently, which is why the verse uses the plural *kolot* (voices, thunderings) rather than *kol* (a single voice). The divine speech ramified as it reached the human ear. And yet, the verse says, they *saw* it.

---

## Synesthesia as Theological Claim

Why does this matter? Because the alternative reading — "they saw the lightning that accompanied the thunder" — makes perfectly ordinary sensory sense and strips the verse of its theological weight. The rabbis insist on the stranger reading because it carries a claim that the ordinary reading cannot: that at Sinai, divine speech crossed the boundary between word and world.

To hear a commandment is to receive information: a rule, a prohibition, an obligation, a promise. To *see* it — to see the voice that speaks it — is something categorically different. It means the utterance is not merely informational. It has the status of a physical object. It exists in the world, not merely in the mind of the hearer. The stone tablets are the permanent residue of this literalized speech: words that became stone because the speech that produced them was already real in a way that ordinary speech is not.

The *Yerushalmi Talmud* (Shekalim 6:1) preserves a description of the tablets themselves: the letters were carved through the stone completely, so that they could be read from both sides — and the *mem* and the *samech*, letters whose interiors are fully enclosed, hovered miraculously in space with nothing to support them. This is the material consequence of the Sinai synaesthesia: divine speech that was *seen* at the moment of utterance left words that defied physics when they landed in stone.

## The Lissajous Figure

Jules Antoine Lissajous, demonstrating acoustics for the French *Académie des Sciences* in the 1850s, discovered that if you attach a mirror to a tuning fork and project a light beam off it onto a screen, then attach a second mirror to a second tuning fork perpendicular to the first, the resulting compound oscillation traces a curve on the screen. The curve's shape depends entirely on the ratio of the two frequencies.

A 1:1 ratio traces a circle or ellipse. A 2:1 ratio traces a figure-eight. A 3:2 ratio traces a three-lobed curve. A 5:3 ratio traces an interlocked star. The shapes are pure: they arise from nothing except frequency ratios. No hand draws them. No artist intervenes. Two invisible oscillations — two sounds — compose into a visible geometry.

This is the structure of Exodus 20:15. The *kolot* were invisible oscillations — the frequency of divine speech. Seeing them means they composed into a visible figure. The Lissajous equation and the Sinai verse describe the same phenomenon in two different registers: *sound becoming shape*, *voice becoming visible*.

---

## The Slow Drift Through Ratios

The simple integer ratios — 1:1, 2:1, 3:2, 3:4, 5:3 — are the ratios that produce closed, recognizable figures. When the ratio is exactly an integer fraction, the Lissajous curve closes on itself perfectly and repeats. When the ratio is irrational, or mid-transition between two integer values, the curve never closes — it fills the available space, creating an unresolved, accumulating density.

The animation lives in this tension. It approaches each closed figure slowly, passes through it without stopping, and continues toward the next. The moment of closure — when the ratio is exactly right and the figure forms completely — is brief and quickly left behind. This is theologically apt: the moment of Sinai was the only moment in which the word of God was seen directly, and it could not be held. Moses had to return to the mountain a second time to retrieve what was broken by the golden calf. The figure forms and dissipates. The covenant was given and had to be given again.

---

## Phosphor, Fire, and Memory

The phosphor trail — the slow accumulation and slow fading of the curve's history on the screen — evokes two things simultaneously. First, the fire at Sinai. Deuteronomy 4:11 describes the mountain burning "to the very heart of heaven," its fire still visible in Israel's memory forty years later. The *Yerushalmi* (loc. cit.) describes the letters of Torah as black fire written on white fire. The persisting luminous trace on the screen is fire that does not immediately consume, that lingers past its moment of generation.

Second, the trail evokes the transmission of Torah itself. Each generation adds its readings, interpretations, and practices to the accumulated figure. The earlier strata do not vanish — they fade slowly, persisting beneath the newer strokes as context and ground. The full figure at any moment is a superposition of everything that has been drawn. This is the structure of the Oral Torah: every generation's voice laid over every prior voice, the original utterance still faintly visible beneath centuries of commentary.

The Hebrew verse stands at the bottom of the frame: *וְכָל-הָעָם רֹאִים אֶת-הַקּוֹלֹת*. All the people saw the voices. The Lissajous figure is their testimony, rendered in light.
