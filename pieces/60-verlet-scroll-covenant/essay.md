# HaBrit — The Covenant Unfurls

The ceremony is described in Exodus 24, verses 3 through 8. Moses had ascended Sinai, received the words of God, and descended. What follows is one of the most consequential liturgical acts in Jewish history — the sealing of the covenant between God and Israel through a physical document, read aloud from a scroll.

---

**Exodus 24:3**

וַיָּבֹ֤א מֹשֶׁה֙ וַיְסַפֵּ֣ר לָעָ֔ם אֵ֛ת כָּל-דִּבְרֵ֣י יְהוָ֖ה וְאֵ֣ת כָּל-הַמִּשְׁפָּטִ֑ים וַיַּ֨עַן כָּל-הָעָ֤ם ק֣וֹל אֶחָ֔ד וַיֹּאמְר֗וּ כָּל-הַדְּבָרִ֛ים אֲשֶׁר-דִּבֶּ֥ר יְהוָ֖ה נַעֲשֶֽׂה

*"Moses came and told the people all the words of the LORD and all the rules; and all the people answered with one voice, saying: All the things that the LORD has spoken we will do."*

**Exodus 24:7**

וַיִּקַּ֗ח סֵ֚פֶר הַבְּרִ֔ית וַיִּקְרָ֖א בְּאָזְנֵ֣י הָעָ֑ם וַיֹּ֣אמְר֔וּ כֹּ֠ל אֲשֶׁר-דִּבֶּ֨ר יְהוָ֥ה נַעֲשֶׂ֖ה וְנִשְׁמָֽע

*"Then he took the Book of the Covenant and read it aloud to the people; and they said: All that the LORD has spoken we will do and we will hear."*

**Exodus 24:8**

וַיִּקַּ֤ח מֹשֶׁה֙ אֶת-הַדָּ֔ם וַיִּזְרֹ֖ק עַל-הָעָ֑ם וַיֹּ֗אמֶר הִנֵּ֤ה דַֽם-הַבְּרִית֙ אֲשֶׁ֨ר כָּרַ֤ת יְהוָה֙ עִמָּכֶ֔ם עַ֥ל כָּל-הַדְּבָרִ֖ים הָאֵֽלֶּה

*"Moses then took the blood and dashed it on the people, and said: This is the blood of the covenant that the LORD has now made with you concerning all these commands."*

---

The phrase *נַעֲשֶׂה וְנִשְׁמָע* — *naaseh v'nishma* — appears to reverse the expected sequence. One normally hears before acting: deliberates, understands, then commits. But here Israel pledges action before the full terms are explained. The Talmud (*Shabbat* 88a) reads this as the moment Israel achieved the mode of angels: *"When Israel proclaimed naaseh before nishma, a heavenly voice went forth and said: Who revealed to My children this secret which the ministering angels use?"* Angels act on divine command without prior deliberation; Israel, at this moment, chose the same orientation.

What this ceremony makes visible — and what is often overlooked — is that the covenant was sealed through a physical object. Moses did not simply speak the words. He *wrote* them (verse 4). He rose early, built an altar, and had the sacrifices performed. He took blood in basins. And then, verse 7: he took the *Sefer HaBrit*, the Book of the Covenant, and read it aloud. He held a scroll. The people heard words coming from a physical document.

The material form matters. The covenant is not a mental state or an abstract relationship. It is the kind of thing that has weight, that can be picked up, that hangs from rods and deforms under gravity. This is not incidental. Torah is called *derekh* (a path, a way) as often as it is called *dat* (law) — it is something you walk in, carry, handle. The scroll is not a representation of the covenant. It is its physical form.

This piece gives that form genuine physics. The parchment is modeled as a cloth of mass-spring points under Verlet integration — every fiber interconnected, every ripple propagated through the whole. The scroll unrolls under gravity alone, settles into gentle oscillation, never fully still. The Hebrew inscription deforms with the cloth surface: *נַעֲשֶׂה וְנִשְׁמָע* stretches and ripples as the parchment moves.

Like the covenant itself, the scroll has been accepted — *naaseh* — but continues to move. The understanding — *nishma* — is always still arriving.
