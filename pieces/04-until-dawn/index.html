Until Dawn

The Night We Stayed Awake

Each year on the eve of Shavuot, Jews around the world remain awake through the night in the practice known as Tikkun Leil Shavuot — a vigil of Torah study that extends until dawn. The word tikkun (תִּקּוּן) means repair or preparation: a structured curriculum of texts drawn from across the breadth of Jewish tradition, recited as a vigil before the holiday’s morning prayers.

The custom’s earliest formal articulation appears in the Zohar, the foundational text of Kabbalah (Parashat Emor). The Zohar describes the night before Shavuot as an opportunity for the righteous to adorn the bride — in Kabbalistic language, to prepare the Shekhinah, God’s indwelling presence, for the cosmic marriage of Israel and Torah that occurs at the moment of revelation. The mystics of sixteenth-century Safed — among them Rabbi Joseph Karo, Rabbi Shlomo Alkabetz (author of Lecha Dodi), and Rabbi Moses Cordovero — formalized the all-night study as essential Shavuot practice. Alkabetz recorded in a famous letter that during one such Tikkun night in 1533, a Heavenly Voice spoke through Karo, confirming that the preparation was worthy.

At the center of this piece stands the phrase נַעֲשֶׂה וְנִשְׁמַע — “we will do, and we will hear” — drawn from Exodus 24:7, the moment Israel commits to the covenant at Sinai before hearing all its terms. The Talmud (Shabbat 88a) marveled at this inversion: in ordinary life one hears terms before agreeing to them. Israel reversed that order — commitment before comprehension, action before understanding. A Heavenly Voice then called out: “Who revealed to My children this secret that the ministering angels use?” — as if radical, pre-rational faith were the angels’ own mode of being.

The falling Hebrew letters embody that paradox. Letters precede words, words precede meaning, meaning emerges only in the act of study — and study, in the Tikkun, continues until the sky itself brightens. The canvas shifts from midnight blue to rose-gold not as metaphor but as time: a ninety-second clock whose zero is always nightfall and whose end is always morning. The letters carry no fixed semantic content — they are the raw alphabet, the raw material of Torah — yet their fall enacts the tradition’s central claim: that each act of study, however partial, is already a form of receiving.

The Talmud (Shabbat 88b) adds a final image: at the moment of naaseh v’nishma, each Israelite was crowned with two crowns — one for naaseh, one for nishma. The Tikkun is how we carry those crowns through the dark.

Sources: Zohar, Parashat Emor; Exodus 24:7; Babylonian Talmud, Shabbat 88a–88b; Shlomo Alkabetz, letter on Tikkun Leil Shavuot (Safed, 1533).
