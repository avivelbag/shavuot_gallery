קוֹל אֶחָד לְכָל אֶחָד — שמות רבה ה:ט

The Voice in Seventy Tongues

"The voice went forth and was divided into seventy voices, in seventy languages, so that all the nations might hear." — Shemot Rabbah 5:9

The Midrash recounts what happened when the Voice spoke at Sinai. Shemot Rabbah (5:9) teaches that the divine voice divided itself at the moment of revelation: kol echad l'khol echad — one voice to each and every one. Each nation heard the Torah in its own language simultaneously. The Jerusalem Talmud (Shabbat 1:3) elaborates: the single sound shattered into seventy streams, one for each of the seventy nations the ancient world recognized. Every tongue received what had been spoken before the world's creation. The Torah was not translated — it was original in every language at once.

The Talmud (Shabbat 88b) adds a crucial asymmetry: not all nations received equally. Each people heard precisely what it was willing to receive. The Edomites heard the prohibition against murder and turned away. The Ammonites heard the command against adultery and refused. Only Israel heard — and accepted — without condition. This is the doctrine of naaseh v'nishma: "we will do and we will hear," a prior commitment that preceded understanding, which alone made full reception possible. The Talmud adds that at that moment, six hundred thousand angels bound two crowns to every Israelite — one for naaseh and one for nishma.

Penrose aperiodic tiling captures this paradox with mathematical precision. The P3 rhombic tiling is generated by a single deflation rule applied recursively to just two tile shapes. From one rule, radiating from a single central point, the plane fills with a pattern that never repeats: no finite patch appears twice in exactly the same orientation and extent. Every region is unique. Yet every tile is produced by the same rule from the same origin. Infinite variety; indivisible source. This is the mathematical form of the midrash: the one voice producing seventy tongues, all different, none a copy, each irreducibly itself.

The letter aleph — א — stands at the center. The Zohar and the Kabbalistic tradition teach that the entire revelation at Sinai was contained in the first letter. Some traditions hold that God spoke only aleph and fell silent; the rest was Israel hearing what the aleph contained. From that single letter, all Torah unfolded — as from the central point of a Penrose tiling, all the aperiodic richness radiates outward, never repeating, always extending.

The letters scattered among the tiles are not decoration. The Greek alpha (α), the Aramaic alaph (ܐ), the Arabic alif ancestor, and the Latin A — all descend from the same Phoenician ox-head glyph, the aleph that ancient scribes drew as a bovine head, the first letter because the ox was the first wealth. The seventy tongues of the Midrash are not seventy translations: they are seventy expressions of one indivisible utterance, precisely as the tiles of a Penrose tiling are the singular expression of one aperiodic rule.

The slow pulse moving outward enacts the spreading voice: a shimmer that radiates from center to edge, the light rising and falling as the voice traveled from the mountain to every nation, each receiving what it could. The tiling does not move — only the luminosity shifts, the same way the Torah does not change but each generation finds a different face of it looking back.
