# The Voice in Seventy Tongues

The Midrash recounts what happened when the Voice spoke at Sinai. *Shemot Rabbah* (5:9) teaches that the divine voice divided itself at the moment of revelation: *kol echad l'khol echad* — one voice to each and every one. Each nation and people heard the Torah in its own language simultaneously. The Jerusalem Talmud (*Shabbat* 1:3) elaborates: the single sound shattered into seventy streams, one for each of the seventy nations the ancient world recognized. Every tongue received what had been spoken before the world's creation. The Torah was not translated — it was *original* in every language at once.

The Talmud (*Shabbat* 88b) adds a crucial asymmetry: not all nations received equally. Each people heard precisely what it was *willing* to receive. The Edomites heard the prohibition against murder and turned away. The Ammonites heard the command against adultery and refused. Each nation filtered the infinite voice through the narrow aperture of what it could bear. Only Israel heard — and accepted — without condition. This is the doctrine of *naaseh v'nishma*: "we will do and we will hear," a prior commitment that preceded understanding, which alone made full reception possible. The Talmud adds that at the moment Israel said those words, six hundred thousand angels descended and bound two crowns to every Israelite — one for *naaseh* and one for *nishma* — as if accepting an infinite transmission required the full person on both sides of the covenant.

Penrose aperiodic tiling captures this paradox with mathematical precision. The P3 rhombic tiling is generated by a single deflation rule applied recursively to just two tile shapes — a fat rhombus and a thin rhombus. From one rule, radiating from a single central point, the plane fills with a pattern that never repeats: no finite patch appears twice in exactly the same orientation and extent. Every region is unique. Yet every tile is produced by the same rule from the same origin. Infinite variety; indivisible source. This is the mathematical form of the midrash: the one voice producing seventy tongues, all different, none a copy, each irreducibly itself.

The letter *aleph* — א — stands at the center of this piece. The Zohar and the Kabbalistic tradition teach that the entire revelation at Sinai was contained in the first letter. Moses was told: *I am the LORD your God* — the first word is *anochi*, which begins with aleph. Some traditions hold that God spoke only aleph and fell silent; the rest was Israel hearing what the aleph contained. From that single letter, all Torah unfolded — as from the central point of a Penrose tiling, all the aperiodic richness of the pattern radiates outward, never repeating, always extending, complete at every scale.

The letters scattered among the tiles are not decoration. Each one is an *aleph* by another name. The Greek *alpha* (α), the Arabic *alif* (ب starting from the same Semitic root), the Aramaic *alaph* (ܐ), and the Latin A — all descend from the same Phoenician ox-head glyph, the *aleph* that ancient scribes drew as a bovine head, the first letter because the ox was the first wealth, the first necessity, the first word of practical life before it became the first word of revelation. The seventy tongues of the Midrash are not seventy translations: they are seventy expressions of one indivisible utterance, precisely as the tiles of a Penrose tiling are not varied copies but the singular expression of one aperiodic rule, each unique, all arising from a single center.

The slow pulse that moves outward through the tiles enacts the spreading voice: a shimmer that radiates from center to edge, the light rising and falling as the voice traveled from the mountain to every nation and every ear, each receiving what it could. The tiling does not move — only the luminosity shifts, the same way the Torah does not change but each generation finds a different face of it looking back.
