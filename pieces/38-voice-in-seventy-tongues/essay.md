# The Voice in Seventy Tongues

## The Midrash

When the Torah was given at Sinai, the divine voice did not speak in one language alone.
*Shemot Rabbah* 5:9 records: "The voice went forth and was divided into seventy voices, in
seventy languages, so that all the nations might hear." The Jerusalem Talmud (*Shabbat* 1:3)
echoes this — every people heard the proclamation in its own tongue simultaneously. Not
sequentially, not through translation, but at once, in a single undivided moment that
somehow contained all difference within it.

The source verse is Exodus 20:1, the opening of the Ten Commandments:

**וַיְדַבֵּר אֱלֹהִים אֵת כָּל־הַדְּבָרִים הָאֵלֶּה לֵאמֹר**

*"And God spoke all these words, saying:"*

One voice. Every word. All nations.

## What Each Nation Received

The Babylonian Talmud (*Shabbat* 88b) adds a sobering counterpoint: each nation heard the
voice but understood only what it was prepared to receive. The nations that had built their
civilizations on violence heard a prohibition against killing. Those whose courts were
corrupt heard a word about justice. The people who accepted the Torah fully heard everything.
The word was one; the reception was fractured across the world's moral readiness.

This is not a consolation — it is a diagnosis. Infinite truth enters a finite world and
is refracted by every vessel that receives it. The unity at the source does not guarantee
unity in the hearing.

## Penrose Tiling as Theology

Penrose aperiodic tiling — discovered by Roger Penrose in 1974 — covers the infinite plane
with only two tile shapes, yet the pattern never exactly repeats. From a handful of local
rules, infinite global variety emerges. No patch of tiles can serve as a repeating unit for
the whole; yet every patch obeys the same underlying constraints. The tiling radiates from
a single origin — a "sun" configuration of ten golden triangles — and unfolds outward
through a process called *deflation*: each triangle is subdivided according to fixed
ratios derived from the golden ratio φ = (1+√5)/2.

The paradox of Penrose tiling precisely mirrors the paradox of the midrash. From one
source — the aleph (א) at Sinai, the first letter God spoke — infinitely varied structure
radiates outward. No two regions are identical; yet all are governed by the same originating
proportion. The tiling cannot be periodic because aperiodicity is the nature of genuine
variety: it is not repetition that spreads across the nations but differentiation, each
tile unique in context, none in isolation comprehensible as the whole.

## The Aleph at the Center

The kabbalistic tradition, particularly the *Zohar* and the school of the Baal Shem Tov,
holds that the entire Torah is contained in the first word *Bereishit*, and that word in
the *bet* (ב), and that letter in the silent aleph (א) that precedes it — the breath before
speech. At Sinai, some authorities say, only the aleph was actually heard, and everything
else was heard through interpretation.

In this piece, the aleph sits at the center of the tiling, the point from which all
deflations radiate. The five script glyphs scattered through the field — Hebrew, Greek,
Arabic, Latin, Aramaic — are not decoration but archaeology: the letters α (alpha), A
(Latin), ع (Arabic ayin), א (Aramaic aleph), and the Greek Α are each descended from the
same Phoenician ox-head, *aleph*, meaning the ox that pulls the plow of meaning through
the earth. Every nation's alphabet carries this hidden root outward from the same origin,
like tiles from the center of the sun.

## The Pulse

The slow luminosity wave in this piece is not spectacle. It moves outward at the speed
of the voice described in the midrash — unhurried, unstoppable, one ring of brightness
following another. By the time you notice the edge of the wave, the next has already
begun at the center. The Torah does not stop being spoken because it was spoken once.

The tiles do not move. The voice moves through them.
