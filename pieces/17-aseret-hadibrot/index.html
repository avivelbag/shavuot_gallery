עֲשֶׂרֶת הַדִּבְּרוֹת

"The Ten Words" — Aseret HaDibrot

The climax of the Sinai revelation arrives in Exodus 20:2–14, where God speaks the Aseret HaDibrot — the "Ten Words" or Ten Commandments — directly to all Israel. This is the only moment in the Torah where God addresses the entire assembled nation collectively, not through a prophet or intermediary. Every man, woman, and child present heard the voice of God; the Talmud (Berakhot 22a) even extends this to future generations. The covenant was not mediated — it was spoken into the air of the mountain.

The first words God speaks are:

אָנֹכִי יְהוָה אֱלֹהֶיךָ אֲשֶׁר הוֹצֵאתִיךָ מֵאֶרֶץ מִצְרַיִם מִבֵּית עֲבָדִים

"I am the Lord your God, who brought you out of the land of Egypt, from the house of slavery." — Exodus 20:2

The commandment begins not with an obligation but with an identity declaration: God introduces Himself through historical act, through liberation. Freedom precedes law; deliverance precedes covenant. Only after this introduction do the nine prohibitions and obligations follow — five governing Israel's relationship with God, five governing relations between people, the structure of all ethical life laid out on two tablets of stone.

The physical medium of these words carries its own theology. Exodus 31:18 records that when Moses descended, God gave him:

לֻחֹת הָאֶבֶן כְּתֻבִים בְּאֶצְבַּע אֱלֹהִים

"tablets of stone written by the finger of God" — Exodus 31:18

Deuteronomy 9:10 adds that the writing was charut (חָרוּת), "engraved," upon the stone — not painted, not inscribed with ink, but driven into the rock itself. The rabbis of the Mishnah heard a pun in this word that transformed its theology:

אַל תִּקְרָא חָרוּת אֶלָּא חֵרוּת, שֶׁאֵין לְךָ בֶן חוֹרִין אֶלָּא מִי שֶׁעוֹסֵק בְּתַלְמוּד תּוֹרָה

"Do not read charut ('engraved') but cherut ('freedom'): for no one is truly free except one who occupies himself with the study of Torah." — Pirkei Avot 6:2

The play on חָרוּת / חֵרוּת — engraved / free — encodes a paradox at the heart of Sinai: the very law that constrains is the path to liberation. Constraint and freedom are not opposites; the letters cut into stone carry both meanings simultaneously, and the pun depends on nothing more than a vowel.

On Shavuot morning, the festival that commemorates the giving of the Torah at Sinai, it is customary in most congregations to rise for the public chanting of the Aseret HaDibrot. The Torah reader chants the passage with the special ta'am elyon ("upper cantillation"), a melody reserved for this passage, because the words were not merely heard but, as Exodus 20:15 records, also seen: "All the people saw the sounds." Sound, vision, law, and fire — all converge in these ten words, still alive in the stone.

In this piece the words breathe. The amber glow pulsing through each engraved letter recalls the midrashic tradition (Talmud Bavli Megillah 21a) that the original script was written in fire — black fire on white fire — the divine inscription still burning within the rock, waiting to be read.

Sources: Exodus 20:2–14; Exodus 31:18; Deuteronomy 9:10; Pirkei Avot 6:2; Talmud Bavli Berakhot 22a; Talmud Bavli Megillah 21a; Talmud Yerushalmi Shekalim 6:1.
