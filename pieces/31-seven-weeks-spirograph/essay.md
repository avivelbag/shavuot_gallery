# Seven Weeks of Seven — Sefirat HaOmer Spirograph

*The commandment to count the Omer is given in Leviticus 23:15–16: "You shall count for yourselves from the day after the Sabbath, from the day when you bring the wave-offering sheaf — seven complete weeks they shall be; until the day after the seventh Sabbath you shall count fifty days." Shavuot literally means "weeks" — the festival is named for the seven weeks of counting that precede it.*

---

The structure of the Omer count is deceptively simple: seven times seven. Forty-nine days bridge Passover (the liberation from Egypt) and Shavuot (the giving of Torah at Sinai). But the kabbalists, particularly the Safed school of the sixteenth century, heard in these numbers a precise map of the soul's refinement.

The Zohar and later the Ari (R. Isaac Luria) mapped each of the forty-nine days onto a grid of the seven *sefirot* — divine emanations or qualities — repeated seven times. The first week belongs to *chesed* (lovingkindness); the second to *gevurah* (strength and discipline); the third to *tiferet* (beauty and harmony); the fourth to *netzach* (eternity and perseverance); the fifth to *hod* (splendor and gratitude); the sixth to *yesod* (foundation and connection); the seventh to *malkhut* (sovereignty and presence). Within each week, the seven days subdivide by the same seven qualities. Day 1 is *chesed within chesed* — the lovingkindness of lovingkindness; Day 2 is *gevurah within chesed* — the discipline within lovingkindness. Each of the forty-nine days is a unique spiritual coordinate, a specific combination of two qualities, one week-level and one day-level.

This piece encodes that seven-fold symmetry in the geometry of a hypotrochoid — a spirograph curve traced by a point on a small circle rolling inside a large one. With outer radius R = 7, inner radius r = 1, and tracing distance d = 3, the curve produces exactly seven petals. The mathematics reveals the theology: the same ratio 7:1 that governs the weeks governs the flower's symmetry. The formula for the curve's distance from center simplifies to the square root of 45 + 36·cos(7t), which achieves its maximum at seven equally-spaced angles — seven petal tips, one for each week of the count.

The Zohar (Tikunei Zohar 18b) describes the counting as "the purification of the bride before her wedding." Israel is the bride; Torah is the groom; and the forty-nine days are the preparation — the inner work of *teshuvah* (return) and *tikun* (repair) that makes the receiving of Torah possible. The kabbalists speak of the forty-nine gates of impurity that Israel had absorbed during slavery in Egypt, one gate being shed each day of the Omer. By the fiftieth day, Shavuot, all forty-nine gates stand open — and Israel stands at the foot of Sinai, ready.

The Talmud (Rosh HaShanah 21b) notes that there are fifty gates of understanding, forty-nine of which were given to Moses; the fiftieth belongs to God alone. Shavuot arrives as that fiftieth gate — the moment beyond the grid, the arrival at what the structure was always pointing toward. The seven-petaled rose captures both the mathematical structure (7 × 7) and the organic, flowering quality of that preparation: counting is not merely arithmetic but cultivation, each day a petal unfurling.

The curve is drawn progressively, stroke by stroke, over eight seconds — one second per week of counting. The color journey mirrors the agricultural movement of the Omer season: deep field green at the start (*barley* harvest, Passover's offering) shifts gradually to harvest gold by the close (*wheat* harvest, Shavuot's Shtei HaLechem offering). The completed rose then displays the name *שָׁבוּעוֹת* at its heart, where the seven petals converge — the destination that the entire seven-fold structure encircles and points toward, but cannot contain.

The Sefat Emet (R. Yehudah Leib Alter, 1847–1905) observed that the word *sefirah* (counting) shares its root with *sapphire* — the gemstone that represents divine clarity and revelation. The forty-nine days are not mere waiting; they are facets being polished. The spirograph, which requires patience to complete and reveals its full geometry only when the tracing returns to its starting point, enacts this same arc: the curve begins in one place and must trace the full seven-fold path before the flower's shape becomes visible. So too with the counting of the Omer: the complete design is seen only at the end.
