Seven Weeks of Seven — Spirograph Omer Rose

The commandment to count the Omer is given in Leviticus 23:15–16: "You shall count for yourselves from the day after the Sabbath, from the day when you bring the wave-offering sheaf — seven complete weeks they shall be; until the day after the seventh Sabbath you shall count fifty days." Shavuot literally means "weeks" — the festival is named for the seven weeks of counting that precede it.

The structure of the Omer count is deceptively simple: seven times seven. Forty-nine days bridge Passover (the liberation from Egypt) and Shavuot (the giving of Torah at Sinai). But the kabbalists, particularly the Safed school of the sixteenth century, heard in these numbers a precise map of the soul's refinement.

The Zohar and later the Ari (R. Isaac Luria) mapped each of the forty-nine days onto a grid of the seven sefirot — divine emanations or qualities — repeated seven times. The first week belongs to chesed (lovingkindness); the second to gevurah (strength and discipline); the third to tiferet (beauty); the fourth to netzach (perseverance); the fifth to hod (gratitude); the sixth to yesod (connection); the seventh to malkhut (presence). Within each week, the seven days subdivide by the same qualities. Day 1 is chesed within chesed; Day 2 is gevurah within chesed. Each of the forty-nine days is a unique spiritual coordinate.

This piece encodes that seven-fold symmetry in the geometry of a hypotrochoid — a spirograph curve traced by a point on a small circle rolling inside a large one. With outer radius R = 7, inner radius r = 1, and tracing distance d = 3, the curve produces exactly seven petals. The mathematics reveals the theology: the same ratio 7:1 that governs the weeks governs the flower's symmetry.

The Zohar (Tikunei Zohar 18b) describes the counting as "the purification of the bride before her wedding." Israel is the bride; Torah is the groom; and the forty-nine days are the preparation — the inner work of teshuvah and tikun that makes the receiving of Torah possible. The kabbalists speak of the forty-nine gates of impurity absorbed during slavery, one gate being shed each day of the Omer. By the fiftieth day, Shavuot, all gates stand open.

The curve is drawn progressively over eight seconds — one second per week of counting. The color journey mirrors the agricultural Omer season: deep field green at the start (barley harvest, Passover's grain) shifts to harvest gold by the close (wheat harvest, Shavuot's Shtei HaLechem offering). The completed rose displays שָׁבוּעוֹת at its heart, where the seven petals converge — the destination that the seven-fold structure encircles and points toward, but cannot contain.

The Sefat Emet observed that the word sefirah (counting) shares its root with sapphire — the gemstone representing divine clarity. The forty-nine days are not mere waiting; they are facets being polished. The spirograph enacts this same arc: the curve must trace the full seven-fold path before the flower's shape becomes visible. So too with the Omer: the complete design is seen only at the end.
