She Went and Gleaned in the Field

Kruskal's minimum spanning tree — the optimal path through a barley field

"Let me go to the field and glean among the ears of grain after him in whose sight I shall find favor." And she went and came and gleaned in the field after the reapers; and her chance — מִקְרֶה — brought her to the portion of the field belonging to Boaz, who was of the family of Elimelech. — Ruth 2:2–3

Ruth asks Naomi's permission to go glean in the barley fields of Bethlehem. She does not choose Boaz's field deliberately. The text says va-yiker mikreha — literally, "her chance happened to her." She wanders and she lands where she lands.

Yet the Talmud will not let this randomness stand unchallenged. In Megillah 13a, the rabbis read mikreh — "chance" — as providential. What looks like Ruth stumbling into Boaz's field is, in the Talmud's reading, the concealed hand of Providence arranging what appears to be accident into inevitability.

The minimum spanning tree makes precisely the same argument from the opposite direction.

The law of peah. Leviticus 19:9–10 commands every farmer: do not harvest the corners of your field (peah), do not gather the grain that falls (leket). These portions belong to the poor, the stranger, the widow, the orphan. The field is not entirely yours to harvest. Its corners are consecrated to those who have nothing.

Ruth, the Moabite widow, is exactly this person — the stranger, the widow, the one the law names as the rightful gleaner of what the owner leaves behind.

What the algorithm shows. Kruskal's algorithm builds a spanning tree by making purely local decisions: at each step, take the shortest available edge that does not form a cycle. It never plans. It never sees the whole field. Yet when it is done, it has found the global optimum — the unique arrangement of edges with the smallest possible total cost, connecting every point.

The algorithm wanders and gathers, one edge at a time, just as Ruth wanders and gathers, one stalk at a time. And both arrive, through local choices, at an arrangement that turns out to be optimal.

Notice the four corner clusters. They are deferred until last — left unharvested while the rest of the field connects. These are the peah points, held back by design. They complete the tree only at the end, when the main harvest is done. Ruth's point — the stranger, the gleaner — sits near the center of its star of edges, connected in all directions, the hub through which the field finds its structure.

What looks like chance is the field assembling its optimal shape around the one who chose to be there.
