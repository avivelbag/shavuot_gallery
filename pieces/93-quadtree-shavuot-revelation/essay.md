# From Undivided Light, the Word

On the morning of the third day there was thunder and lightning, with a thick cloud over the mountain, and a very loud trumpet blast. Everyone in the camp trembled. — *Exodus 19:16*

Moses led the people out of the camp to meet with God, and they stood at the foot of the mountain. — *Exodus 19:17*

The Torah describes the moment of revelation at Sinai as simultaneously one of overwhelming unity and explosive articulation. The whole people stood at the foot of the mountain — a single mass, a single breath held — and yet the Ten Commandments were spoken one by one, letter by letter, each a discrete act of creation. The undivided became articulated. The formless was given form.

Midrash Tanchuma (*Yitro* 11) makes this explicit: before the Torah was given, the world existed in a state of *tohu vavohu* — without form, an undifferentiated mass. The divine speech at Sinai was the event that imposed structure on formlessness, as a sculptor does not add material to stone but reveals a figure already hidden within the undivided block. Revelation was not the arrival of something new from outside; it was the articulation of what was always latent in the primordial light.

**The quadtree is a formal model of this process.** A single undivided region carries maximum uncertainty — it could be any image; its average color tells you nothing about its internal structure. Each subdivision resolves ambiguity: splitting a region into four smaller regions, each carrying its own average, its own local truth. The information content of the image — in Claude Shannon's precise sense — is exactly the sum of the variances eliminated at each split. Subdivision is literally the act of information creation. The algorithm begins with the regions of highest variance, the most uncertain, the most in need of articulation, and recursively resolves them first.

This is exactly the structure of the divine speech at Sinai. The first word — *Anochi* — is the region of maximum variance, the divine self-declaration that contains everything. It is subdivided by the commandments, and the commandments by their clauses, and the clauses by their letters, until the full image of the covenant is articulated into its smallest legible units. The Midrash says Moses received Torah letter by letter; the quadtree works the same way.

The piece begins with a single white square: one undivided region of light, carrying within it the hidden word **שְׁבוּעוֹת** (Shavuot). Frame by frame the algorithm subdivides the regions of highest variance, early blocks slightly brighter and more washed out than their final values — light condensing as the tree deepens, as undifferentiated brightness resolves into the particular. The Hebrew word emerges not by addition but by differentiation: each region discovers what it actually is.

When every part of the image has been fully articulated — when all regions have reached their minimum size — the word stands complete: harvest gold on warm parchment, revealed from the undivided by recursive precision. Then the tree collapses in reverse. The letters dissolve back into regions, regions into larger regions, until the canvas is once again a single undivided square of light. Pause. And begin again.

Revelation is not a one-time event but a structure: the same hidden content, the same undivided source, the same word — waiting inside the light, every time.
