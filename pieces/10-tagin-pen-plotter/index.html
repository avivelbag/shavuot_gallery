The Crowns

Source: Talmud Bavli, Menachot 29b; Maharsha (Rabbi Shmuel Eliezer Eidels), ad loc.

Look closely at a handwritten Torah scroll and you will notice something unexpected above certain letters: three tiny strokes, each curving upward like a miniature crown. These are tagin (singular: tag, from the Aramaic for crown). They are not decorative flourishes added by the scribe on a whim. They are mandatory, their positions specified by tradition, their origin the subject of one of the most haunting stories in the Talmud.

Tractate Menachot 29b: Moses ascends to heaven at Sinai and finds the Holy One seated, tying crowns onto the letters of the Torah. Moses asks: why the crowns? God explains that a man named Akiva, who will live many generations hence, will interpret each tag and from it derive mountains of law. Moses asks to see this man. He is transported forward in time and sits in Rabbi Akiva's academy, listening. He cannot follow the discussion. He is distressed — this wisdom transcends him. Then a student asks Rabbi Akiva: from where do you derive this? And Rabbi Akiva answers: it is a law given to Moses at Sinai.

This passage is one of the most theologically compressed in rabbinic literature. The Maharsha reads it as a statement about the nature of revelation itself. What was given at Sinai was not only the text that Moses received but the entire future of its interpretation — the crowns contained, in latent form, everything that scholars would draw out across millennia. Moses could not understand what Akiva taught, yet Akiva's teaching was already present at Sinai, sealed inside the ornamental strokes that Moses himself watched God add to the letters.

For Shavuot, which commemorates the giving of Torah at Sinai, the tagin are a potent symbol. The holiday is not merely a commemoration of a past event but a celebration of Torah's inexhaustibility — the fact that every generation finds something new within it. The crowns on the letters are the visual emblem of that surplus. Each flourish says: there is more here than meets the eye.

This artwork draws a single shin — the first letter of Shema, the central Jewish affirmation — in the style of a pen plotter: thin bezier strokes with the slight tremor of a hand working in lampblack ink on parchment. The three arms of the shin are drawn one by one, as if by a scribe composing a Torah scroll. When the letter is complete, tagin blossom at each arm tip in triple upward flourishes, re-enacting the scene from Menachot 29b: the crowns added after the letter, carrying meanings not yet known.

The palette is bone-white parchment and deep sepia ink. No color, no ornament except the marks themselves — and the crowns above them.
