# And Sinai Was Covered in Flowers

*Sources: Exodus 19:18; Shir HaShirim Rabbah 4:12; Pirkei DeRabbi Eliezer ch. 41.*

---

Exodus 19:18 records the moment of revelation with overwhelming imagery: *"And Mount Sinai smoked altogether, because the LORD descended upon it in fire; and the smoke thereof ascended as the smoke of a furnace, and the whole mount quaked greatly."* Fire, smoke, thunder, earthquake — the mountain is a furnace, the divine descent an act of consuming power. The verse gives us a theophany of terror. And yet the rabbinic tradition looks at the same mountain and sees something else entirely.

The Midrash in Shir HaShirim Rabbah (4:12) records that at the moment the Torah was given, Mount Sinai burst into bloom. The barren desert mountain — part of the same arid wilderness that surrounded it — was suddenly covered with grasses and flowers. Pirkei DeRabbi Eliezer (ch. 41) elaborates the same tradition: the mountain blossomed with all manner of vegetation, as if Eden itself had come down upon Sinai for the day of the giving.

This is not mere decoration. The Midrash is making a theological claim. The Torah is *etz chaim*, a tree of life. When the concentrated source of life entered the world in verbal form — when the infinite contracted into letters, commandments, and story — the earth itself responded. The rock and dust of Sinai did not remain passive while revelation passed through them. They flowered.

The pattern is consistent across the tradition. The Song of Songs — read by the Sages as an allegory of the Sinai encounter — speaks of spring arriving, of the beloved's voice calling from the mountains: *"arise, come away; for lo, the winter is past, the rain is over and gone; the flowers appear on the earth."* Shir HaShirim Rabbah reads these images against the backdrop of the desert revelation: the flowers of this verse are the voice at Sinai, the winter was the slavery of Egypt. Even the natural world is reshaped by the entry of Torah into history.

The Eden growth model used here mirrors this precisely. A single seed cell at the canvas center — the mountain peak — expands stochastically outward. At each step, a cell selected at random from the current frontier is absorbed into the cluster. The result is not the sparse, fractal tendrils of diffusion-limited aggregation, which is starved by long diffusion paths, but a dense, lush, petal-shaped bloom that fills the entire canvas. From a single point, wholeness radiates outward.

The color follows distance from the seed: deep violet at the mountain's heart, warming through rose to wheat gold, and finally pale cream at the outermost growth front — the fresh edge of the bloom still reaching. The center is fire; the edge is the petal. The animation begins with the verse from Exodus — *וְהַר סִינַי עָשַׁן כֻּלּוֹ*, "And Mount Sinai smoked altogether" — visible at the canvas center. As the bloom grows outward from the mountain peak, the hex cells cover the text. The smoke and fire are not erased: they are transformed. The bloom does not replace the revelation; it fulfills it. The mountain that smoked is the same mountain that flowered. Both are the same event, seen from different angles of the tradition.

*See: Exodus 19:18 (the theophany at Sinai); Shir HaShirim Rabbah 4:12 (the flowering of Sinai at Matan Torah); Pirkei DeRabbi Eliezer ch. 41 (the mountain covered in vegetation at the giving of Torah); Song of Songs 2:11–12 (the flowers appear on the earth — read by the Sages as referring to the Torah given at Sinai).*
