# Chag HaKatzir — The Harvest Festival

*Sources: Exodus 23:16; Exodus 34:22; Leviticus 23:9–22; Ruth 2:1–23.*

---

Shavuot is Chag HaKatzir — the Festival of the Harvest — a name that appears in Exodus 23:16 and 34:22, tying the day to the physical reality of standing grain, sickles in hand, fields gold under summer sun. The Torah names the day at the moment of reaping: not a historical anniversary, but an agricultural fact.

The wheat harvest in the Land of Israel completes itself in late spring, as the barley harvest that began at Passover draws to a close. The agricultural calendar was framed by these two reaping festivals: the cutting of the first barley sheaf — the omer — on the second day of Passover (Leviticus 23:10–11), and the offering of two loaves of leavened wheat bread on Shavuot itself (Leviticus 23:17). In between lay the seven-week count, the Sefirat HaOmer, bridging one grain and another.

The omer offering was simple: a single sheaf of new barley, cut before dawn. The Shavuot offering — the Shtei HaLechem, the Two Loaves — required baked bread, leavened (unlike any other Temple offering), brought by the entire community at once. Together they stood at the altar: farmer and poor represented in the same gesture.

This tension is built into the harvest laws themselves. Leviticus 23:22 — appearing immediately after the laws of Shavuot — commands the farmer to leave the corner of the field (pe'ah) unreaped, and not to return for dropped or forgotten sheaves (shikchah). These portions belong, by Torah law, to the stranger, the orphan, and the widow.

The most famous gleaner in the Hebrew Bible is Ruth. Newly arrived in Bethlehem from Moab with her mother-in-law Naomi, she goes to glean in the fields of Boaz during the barley and wheat harvests (Ruth 2:3). The Torah's agricultural laws are not theoretical for her; they are survival. Boaz instructs his workers to leave extra stalks for her deliberately — a quiet act of chesed embedded in a system designed for it.

The harvest is not incidental to Sinai; it is its season. The Torah was given to a people who had just been freed from forced agricultural labor, who understood what it meant to reap what they had not sown, and who were now told that even what they sowed and reaped had corners that belonged to someone else.

*See: Exodus 23:16 (Chag HaKatzir); Exodus 34:22; Leviticus 23:9–14 (omer offering); Leviticus 23:15–17 (two wheat loaves); Leviticus 23:22 (pe'ah and shikchah); Ruth 2:1–23 (Ruth gleaning in Boaz's fields).*
