# Chag HaKatzir — The Harvest Festival

*Sources: Exodus 23:16; Exodus 34:22; Leviticus 23:9–22; Ruth 2:1–23.*

---

Shavuot carries three names in the Torah, and each name is a different window into the same day. It is Chag HaShavuot — the Festival of Weeks, marking seven weeks from Passover. It is Chag HaBikkurim — the Festival of First Fruits, when the choicest of the new harvest was brought to the Temple as an offering of gratitude. And it is Chag HaKatzir — the Festival of the Harvest, the name that appears in Exodus 23:16 and 34:22, tying the day to the physical reality of standing grain, sickles in hand, fields gold under summer sun.

The wheat harvest in the Land of Israel completes itself in late spring, just as the barley harvest that began at Passover draws to a close. The agricultural calendar of ancient Israel was framed by these two reaping festivals: the cutting of the first barley sheaf — the omer — on the second day of Passover (Leviticus 23:10–11), and the offering of two loaves of leavened wheat bread on Shavuot itself (Leviticus 23:17). In between lay the seven-week count, the Sefirat HaOmer, bridging one grain and another, one festival and the next.

The omer offering was simple and raw: a single sheaf of new barley, cut in the night before dawn. But the Shavuot offering — the Shtei HaLechem, the Two Loaves — was something far more elaborate. It required not sheaves but baked bread, made from the finest new wheat, leavened (unlike any other Temple offering), and brought by the entire community at once. One loaf represented the farmer; one represented the poor. Together they stood at the altar.

This tension between farmer and poor is built into the harvest laws themselves. Leviticus 23:22 — appearing immediately after the laws of Shavuot — commands the farmer not to complete the harvest of his field: he must leave the corner (pe'ah) unreaped, and must not return to collect whatever sheaves he dropped or forgot (shikchah). These portions belong, by Torah law, to the stranger, the orphan, and the widow.

The most famous gleaner in the Hebrew Bible is Ruth. Newly arrived in Bethlehem from Moab with her mother-in-law Naomi, she goes to glean in the fields of Boaz during the barley and wheat harvests (Ruth 2:3). She works behind the reapers, gathering what they leave. The Torah's agricultural laws are not theoretical for her; they are survival. Boaz instructs his workers to leave extra stalks for her deliberately — a quiet act of chesed embedded in a system designed for it.

The image at the center of this piece is the sheaf itself: three bound stalks of wheat leaning together, the form the Talmud calls an omer. This is not a romantic abstraction of harvest; it is the actual unit of measurement, the physical quantity that gave the counting its name. The wheat is rendered as stipple — ten thousand dots, denser where the light falls shallow, sparse where it glances off the golden straw — because the stipple technique mirrors the act of gleaning itself: one dot at a time, one grain at a time, the image assembling slowly from what accumulates.

Shavuot is, in all three of its names, the same day: the day the grain reached its fullness, the first fruits were carried to Jerusalem, the count of the weeks completed. The harvest is not incidental to Sinai; it is its season. The Torah was given to a people who had just been freed from forced agricultural labor, who understood what it meant to reap what you had not sown, and who were now told that even what they sowed and reaped had corners that belonged to someone else.

*See: Exodus 23:16 (Chag HaKatzir); Exodus 34:22 (Chag HaShavuot); Leviticus 23:9–14 (omer offering); Leviticus 23:15–17 (two wheat loaves); Leviticus 23:22 (pe'ah and shikchah); Ruth 2:1–23 (Ruth gleaning in Boaz's fields).*
