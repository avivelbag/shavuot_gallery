שָׁבוּעוֹת

The Fiftieth Day — שָׁבוּעוֹת

"You shall count fifty days to the morrow of the seventh week." — Leviticus 23:16

Shavuot arrives on the fiftieth day. Leviticus 23:15–16 specifies the count precisely: seven complete weeks, and then the fiftieth day. The number arrives at the end of an act of consecrated counting — forty-nine days, seven sevens — and the festival falls on the threshold that opens beyond the count.

The name שָׁבוּעוֹת derives from שֶׁבַע (sheva, seven) and means "weeks." But the word is grammatically plural — weeks, not one week — and the festival is a festival of multiples: seven weeks of seven, forty-nine completed, the fiftieth the gift that cannot be counted toward itself. A festival of sevens that arrives beyond the reach of sevens.

There is a second resonance buried in the same word. שְׁבוּעָה (shvuah) means oath, vow, covenant-promise. שָׁבוּעוֹת and שְׁבוּעָה share their root: shin-vet-ayin. The festival of weeks and the festival of oaths wear the same name. The Talmud (BT Shevuot 2b) notes the shared root — the book on vows begins on the holiday whose name carries a vow inside it. Shavuot is the day when Israel stood at Sinai and answered: Naaseh v'nishma — "We will do, and we will hear." An oath sealed in fire and sound.

The number fifty is its own world of meaning. The Talmud (BT Rosh Hashanah 21b) records that fifty gates of understanding — sha'arei binah — were created in the world, and all but one were given to Moses: he received forty-nine, the last withheld. Forty-nine is the maximum of human comprehension; fifty is where the divine alone stands. Shavuot, the fiftieth day, arrives precisely at that threshold.

The same fifty echoes across Jewish time. The Jubilee year — Yovel (Leviticus 25:10) — is the fiftieth year, when the land rests, debts release, and slaves go free. Fifty days from the Exodus to Sinai: from freedom to covenant, from departure to arrival.
