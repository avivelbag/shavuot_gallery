As One Person, with One Heart

Matan Torah / unity at Sinai

"Va'yichan sham Yisrael neged hahar" — Israel encamped there, opposite the mountain. (Exodus 19:2)

The verb is singular. Not va'yachanu, they encamped, but va'yichan, he encamped — as if all of Israel were one person arriving at one destination with one motion. Rashi notices this immediately. He writes that Israel encamped ke'ish echad be'lev echad — as one person, with one heart. It is, he notes, a unity without parallel in the biblical narrative: not commanded, not coerced, simply there. And the Mechilta draws a sharp inference from the verse order: Israel encamped before God spoke. The unity was a precondition, not a result.

The Model

Tamás Vicsek published his model in 1995 to explain a puzzle in the physics of living systems: how do thousands of starlings, with no leader and no blueprint, wheel together as a single body? His answer was minimal to the point of provocation. Give each particle a position, a speed, and a heading. At each step, let it align its heading with the average heading of its neighbors within radius r, then add a small random noise η. That is all.

No cohesion force pulling particles together. No separation force pushing them apart. No memory of where the flock has been. Just: look at your neighbors, point where they point, add a little randomness, move. The surprising result — and what makes Vicsek a genuine discovery rather than a simulation trick — is that this minimal rule produces a genuine phase transition. Above a critical noise level, the swarm is disordered: particles scatter, no coherent motion emerges. Below it, the swarm spontaneously orders into a single flowing body, all pointing the same direction, moving together. The transition is sharp. It is not gradual persuasion. It is physics.

This Piece

The animation begins at high noise — the swarm is scattered, chaotic, each particle going its own way. As the noise slowly drops, nothing seems to change — until suddenly it does. The disorder snaps into coherent flow. Hundreds of particles, each aware only of its nearest neighbors, become one river.

This is the moment Rashi describes. Not a miracle imposed from outside, but a phase transition arising from within. Each person looked at the neighbors closest to them and aligned. No one directed the whole. No center held it. And yet, below the threshold of noise, they became ke'ish echad — as one person.

Color encodes local alignment: particles that move with their neighbors glow wheat gold (#D4A017); those partly aligned fade through twilight violet; those disordered burn deep indigo. At the moment of the phase transition, the canvas shifts from mostly indigo to mostly gold — the visual signal that coherence has arrived.

The mountain does not move. The particles flow above it.
