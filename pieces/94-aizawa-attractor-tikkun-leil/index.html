The Night Turns on Its Axis

Aizawa Strange Attractor — Tikkun Leil Shavuot

The practice of Tikkun Leil Shavuot — staying awake all night on the eve of Shavuot to study Torah until dawn — first appears in the Zohar (Introduction) and was codified as widespread custom by the Magen Avraham (Orach Chaim 494). The word tikkun means "repair" or "correction." The night is not merely observed; it is repaired.

The Legend Behind the Vigil

According to the Midrash (Shir HaShirim Rabbah 1:12), the Israelites at Sinai overslept on the night before the revelation. Moses had to rouse them when the thunder began. The dawn of the third day was the moment of Matan Torah. The Tikkun is a correction of that missed vigil: each year, by staying awake through the night, the community prepares to meet dawn as Israel, that first time, did not.

The revelation arrived not in the depth of night but at its turning — with the morning:

וַיְהִי בַיּוֹם הַשְּׁלִישִׁי בִּהְיֹת הַבֹּקֶר וַיְהִי קֹלֹת וּבְרָקִים וְעָנָן כָּבֵד עַל־הָהָר וְקֹל שֹׁפָר חָזָק מְאֹד וַיֶּחֱרַד כָּל־הָעָם אֲשֶׁר בַּמַּחֲנֶה׃ וַיּוֹצֵא מֹשֶׁה אֶת־הָעָם לִקְרַאת הָאֱלֹהִים מִן־הַמַּחֲנֶה וַיִּתְיַצְּבוּ בְּתַחְתִּית הָהָר׃

"And it came to pass on the third day, when it was morning, that there were thunders and lightnings, and a thick cloud upon the mountain, and the voice of the horn exceeding loud; and all the people that were in the camp trembled. And Moses brought forth the people out of the camp to meet God; and they stood at the lower end of the mountain."

— Exodus 19:16–17

Dawn, not midnight, is the appointment. The Tikkun is the practice of being already awake — already standing — when that moment returns.

The Aizawa Attractor

The Aizawa attractor is a strange attractor in three-dimensional space, defined by six parameters (a=0.95, b=0.7, c=0.6, d=3.5, e=0.25, f=0.1) and three differential equations:

dx/dt = (z − b)·x − d·y
dy/dt = d·x + (z − b)·y
dz/dt = c + a·z − z³/3 − (x²+y²)(1+e·z) + f·z·x³

Unlike the Lorenz attractor's butterfly or the Rössler band, the Aizawa traces a toroidal orbit: each revolution circles the tube of the torus once while also advancing around its central axis. The orbit is bounded and deterministic — obeying exact rules at every moment — yet it never repeats in any finite time. Just as the night sky wheels without exactly repeating from one Shavuot to the next.

Structured Time — the Non-Obvious Insight

The Tikkun is not merely sleeplessness. The traditional order of all-night study — Tanach, Mishnah, Talmud, Kabbalah, and back — traces a spiral like the attractor. Each pass through the same textual territory reaches a different depth. You cover the same ground; it is not the same ground. The orbit appears closed from a distance. Up close it never retraces a single point.

Five trajectories — started from nearly identical initial conditions, slowly diverging as all study partners eventually do — spiral through the attractor as the viewpoint rotates slowly (one full revolution every two minutes). Each point is colored by height: midnight blue (#0a0a2e) at the bottom of the torus, where the orbit descends into the depth of the night, rising through deep violet, cobalt, and silver-blue to pale gold (#f0e8b0) at the top — the color of pre-dawn sky, עלות השחר. When the trajectories climb to the upper reaches of the torus, the word שַׁחֲרִית (Shacharit — the dawn prayer) fades into view at the top of the canvas: the prayer that ends the vigil.

The star trails rotate. The night turns on its axis. Each position is new. The orbit is the same orbit. And it is carrying you toward morning.
