Seven Waves Across the Field

On Shavuot, the kohen in the Temple would receive a basket of the seven species — shivat ha-minim — and the pilgrim would recite a declaration that compressed all of Israelite history into a dozen verses. That declaration begins with a geographical confession: this Land, given to our ancestors, yields these fruits. The fruits are themselves a map of the Land.

Deuteronomy 8:8 — The Seven Species

אֶרֶץ חִטָּה וּשְׂעֹרָה, וְגֶפֶן וּתְאֵנָה וְרִמּוֹן; אֶרֶץ-זֵית שֶׁמֶן, וּדְבָשׁ.
"A land of wheat and barley, and vines and fig trees and pomegranates; a land of olive oil and honey."
Deuteronomy 8:8

The verse is a list, but it is not a random inventory. Each species corresponds to a different agricultural season — and therefore to a different part of the Land. Barley ripens first, in the Jordan Valley and the coastal plain, during the first weeks of the Omer. Wheat follows, ascending the hill country toward Jerusalem, arriving precisely at Shavuot. The grapes, figs, pomegranates, olives, and dates come later in summer, higher in elevation, longer in cycle. The seven species span the full year of the Land's agricultural rhythm.

Deuteronomy 26:5–10 — The Bikkurim Declaration

וְעָנִיתָ וְאָמַרְתָּ לִפְנֵי יְהוָה אֱלֹהֶיךָ: אֲרַמִּי אֹבֵד אָבִי, וַיֵּרֶד מִצְרַיְמָה וַיָּגָר שָׁם בִּמְתֵי מְעָט; וַיְהִי שָׁם לְגוֹי גָּדוֹל עָצוּם וָרָב… וַיּוֹצִאֵנוּ יְהוָה מִמִּצְרַיִם בְּיָד חֲזָקָה וּבִזְרֹעַ נְטוּיָה… וַיְבִאֵנוּ אֶל-הַמָּקוֹם הַזֶּה, וַיִּתֶּן-לָנוּ אֶת-הָאָרֶץ הַזֹּאת, אֶרֶץ זָבַת חָלָב וּדְבָשׁ. וְעַתָּה, הִנֵּה הֵבֵאתִי אֶת-רֵאשִׁית פְּרִי הָאֲדָמָה אֲשֶׁר-נָתַתָּה לִּי, יְהוָה.
"You shall then recite before the LORD your God: 'My father was a wandering Aramean; he went down to Egypt and sojourned there, few in number; and there he became a great, mighty, and populous nation… The LORD freed us from Egypt with a mighty hand and an outstretched arm… He brought us to this place and gave us this land, a land flowing with milk and honey. Wherefore I now bring the first fruits of the soil which You, O LORD, have given me.'"
Deuteronomy 26:5–10

The non-obvious insight: the declaration does not begin with gratitude for the fruit in the basket. It begins with humiliation — arami oved avi, "my father was a perishing Aramean." Before celebrating the harvest, the speaker recounts servitude, exile, and redemption. The fruit arrives at the end of history, not at its beginning. The Bikkurim ritual insists that the Land's fertility is inseparable from the story of how the people came to stand in it.

The Harvest Front as Wave

In the weeks of the Omer — the seven-week count from Pesach to Shavuot — the harvest front moved northward and upward across the Land. Barley was cut in the warm Jordan Valley while Jerusalem's wheat was still green. By the time the Omer count reached forty-nine, the highland wheat was ready, the first fruits prepared, and Shavuot arrived as the transition from the first species to the second.

The Mishnah (Bikkurim 1:3) specifies which regions sent which species to Jerusalem: lowland cities brought barley and olives; hill towns sent wheat, grapes, and pomegranates; the Jordan Valley sent dates. The entire Land spoke through its geography, the pilgrims arriving at the Temple like a slow wave breaking from the south and the east toward the center.

The Cyclic CA and the Seven Species

The artwork enacts this wave logic through a cyclic cellular automaton. Each of the 200×160 cells holds one of seven states, each state the color of a species: wheat gold, barley tan, grape purple, fig brown, pomegranate red, olive green, date amber. The update rule is local and uniform: a cell advances to its next species whenever at least three of its eight Moore neighbors already hold that next state. From random noise, coherent spiral wave fronts self-organize within a few hundred frames — not designed but emergent, each front the leading edge of one species converting its neighbors to the next, cycling endlessly through all seven.

This is the harvest cycle as physics: wheat becomes barley becomes grape becomes fig becomes pomegranate becomes olive becomes date becomes wheat again — not a metaphor but a structural identity between the biological succession of species and the mathematical propagation of excitable wave fronts. Shavuot falls precisely where the barley wave finishes and the wheat wave begins. The cellular automaton does not represent the harvest; it is the harvest's underlying logic, made visible.
