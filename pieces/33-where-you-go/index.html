Where You Go — String Art Portrait of Ruth

כִּי אֶל-אֲשֶׁר תֵּלְכִי אֵלֵךְ וּבַאֲשֶׁר תָּלִינִי אָלִין Ruth 1:16 — "Where you go, I will go; where you lodge, I will lodge. Your people shall be my people and your God my God."

The Book of Ruth opens in grief. Elimelech, a man of Bethlehem, takes his wife Naomi and their two sons to the fields of Moab to escape a famine. There his sons marry Moabite women — Orpah and Ruth. Then the three men die. Naomi, twice widowed by proxy, is left with two daughters-in-law in a foreign land, bound to them only through sorrow and the habit of shared meals.

The moment that shapes everything comes on the road back to Bethlehem. Naomi urges her daughters-in-law to go home — back to their mothers' houses, back to the gods of Moab. Shuvna, she says: return. The logic is sound. Naomi has nothing to offer them. No more sons in her womb. No economic prospects. No standing in Bethlehem for a widow with Moabite companions in tow.

Orpah weeps and turns back. The narrative does not condemn her. She makes the reasonable choice. But Ruth cleaves. The Hebrew verb — vattidbok — is the same word used in Genesis 2:24 for the cleaving of husband and wife in marriage. What Ruth does in this moment is not politely declining advice; it is an act of covenant-making, as deliberate and binding as any formal vow.

"Where you go, I will go. Where you lodge, I will lodge. Your people shall be my people and your God my God. Where you die, I will die, and there will I be buried." (Ruth 1:16–17) The declaration is absolute, sevenfold, irrevocable. Ruth transfers her entire identity — national, religious, familial — from Moab to Israel. She converts, entirely voluntarily, at the worst possible moment, with no promise of reward.

The rabbis recognized this scene as the paradigm of sincere conversion. The Talmud (Yevamot 47b) records that a prospective convert must be informed of the hardships of Jewish life so that their acceptance is genuinely free. The wording echoes Ruth's declaration precisely: your people will be my people, your God my God. Ruth, encountering no court and no formal process, says these words spontaneously — to a woman who has explicitly warned her not to come.

This is precisely why the Book of Ruth is read on Shavuot. The festival commemorates Israel's acceptance of Torah at Sinai — the moment the people declared naaseh v'nishma, "we will do and we will hear" (Exodus 24:7). Ruth's declaration on the road has the same structure: she commits before she understands what she is accepting. The acceptance is precisely the point.

The Talmud (Yevamot 47b) draws the parallel explicitly: just as Israel accepted Torah at Sinai as a people, so the ger — the one who joins — re-enacts that acceptance individually. Ruth standing on the road to Bethlehem is Israel standing at the foot of Sinai. Her "where you go, I will go" is their naaseh v'nishma — loyalty declared before arrival, covenant sworn in the open field of unknowing.

The visual language of string art makes this geometry legible. Two figures, each built from hundreds of interlocking straight lines, lean toward each other across a shared space. No single line creates the form; the figures emerge only through density and accumulation — the way loyalty becomes visible not in one dramatic declaration but in the textured weight of daily presence: the gleaning in the fields of Boaz, the return each evening to Naomi's side, the quiet persistence of a woman who chose a life she had no claim to and then worked to deserve it.

Ruth's story does not end with declaration. She marries Boaz. She bears Obed, who becomes the father of Jesse, who becomes the father of David. Ruth the Moabite, the voluntary convert, becomes the great-grandmother of the king whose line the tradition holds will produce the Messiah. The Book of Ruth is not an addendum to Israelite history. It is a hinge. And the hinge swings on six words spoken on a dusty road: ki el asher telchi elech — where you go, I will go.
