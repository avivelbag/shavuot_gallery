# A Land Flowing

## Eretz Zavat Chalav U'Dvash

"And I have come down to deliver them out of the hand of the Egyptians, and to bring them
up out of that land unto a good land and a large, unto a land flowing with milk and honey."
(Exodus 3:8)

The Hebrew phrase *eretz zavat chalav u'dvash* — a land flowing with milk and honey
— appears more than twenty times across the Torah, from Exodus through Leviticus, Numbers,
Deuteronomy, and Joshua. It is one of the defining images of the covenantal promise: not
merely a place to settle, but a living land, generous and overflowing.

God first speaks it to Moses at the burning bush (Exodus 3:8), before the plagues, before
the Exodus, when redemption is still only a word. It recurs as the Israelites stand at
the edge of the wilderness (Numbers 13–14), when the spies return carrying grapes too
heavy for one man and the people nearly turn back. It is spoken again in Deuteronomy as
Moses summarizes the whole forty-year journey — the phrase is the destination that
justified everything.

## Shavuot and the First Fruits

Shavuot is *Chag HaBikkurim* — the festival of first fruits (Numbers 28:26) —
and *Chag HaKatzir* — the harvest festival (Exodus 23:16). It falls seven weeks
after Passover, at the height of the wheat harvest. The first fruits that pilgrims brought
to Jerusalem (Deuteronomy 26) were the seven species: wheat, barley, grapes, figs,
pomegranates, olives, and dates. Wheat was the most abundant, the centerpiece of early
summer's abundance.

Before handing over his basket the pilgrim recited the *mikra bikkurim* declaration
(Deuteronomy 26:5–10), beginning: *"Arami oved avi"* — "My father was a wandering
Aramean." The whole history of the people — slavery, exodus, redemption — was compressed
into this moment of offering. The first fruits were sweet precisely because they arrived
after bitterness. The land could not be taken for granted.

## Why Dairy on Shavuot?

The Ashkenazic and Sephardic custom of eating dairy foods on Shavuot has its earliest
recorded sources in the medieval period. The Talmud (Pesachim 68b) mentions that Shavuot
calls for both physical and spiritual rejoicing, but the dairy custom appears later, and
several explanations have accumulated around it.

One account: the Torah was given on Shabbat, and the Israelites at Sinai had no time to
prepare kosher meat in accordance with the newly received laws, so they ate dairy instead.
Another, from the Song of Songs (4:11): *"Honey and milk are under your tongue"*
— the words of Torah are sweet as milk, nourishing as honey, and the foods enact the
metaphor.

A third, less commonly cited: milk and honey are the only two foods that come from living
creatures without killing them. Both flow from the animal or the comb without harm to the
source. The rabbis see in this an image of the covenant at its most generous: a relationship
that sustains without violence, that takes without destroying. On Shavuot, when we receive
the Torah — itself described as "a tree of life to those who grasp it" (Proverbs 3:18) —
we eat foods that embody this gentleness.

## This Piece

This simulation implements Jos Stam's 1999 stable fluids algorithm entirely on the GPU:
semi-Lagrangian advection, implicit viscosity diffusion, Helmholtz-Hodge pressure
projection to enforce incompressibility. Two fluid streams — warm cream from the left,
amber honey from the right — are injected along a slow figure-eight Lissajous path,
their velocity impulses alternating direction every few seconds.

The two fluids swirl together but never fully merge. Cream and honey each hold their
character — one pale and light, one deep and amber — even as they interpenetrate. Like
the Written Torah and the Oral Torah, or like the land and its people: each distinct,
each incomplete without the other, each shaped by the encounter.

*Eretz zavat chalav u'dvash.* A land that flows. Not still, not settled —
always in motion, always overflowing, always arriving.
