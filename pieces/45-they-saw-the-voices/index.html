They Saw the Voices

Lissajous at Sinai — Exodus 20:15

וְכָל-הָעָם רֹאִים אֶת-הַקּוֹלֹת

"And all the people saw the thunderings, and the lightnings, and the voice of the shofar, and the mountain smoking." (Exodus 20:15, Hebrew numbering; the verse appears as 20:18 in many English Bibles due to differing verse divisions in the medieval manuscripts underlying the two traditions.) The Hebrew verb is unambiguous: ro'im, they saw. The object is ha-kolot, the voices, the sounds, the thunderings. They did not hear them — the text uses the verb of sight. At Sinai, Israel stood before the mountain and saw what should have been heard.

The Mechilta and the Visible Word

The Mechilta d'Rabbi Ishmael (Tractate Yitro, Parasha 9) refuses to explain this verse away as poetic hyperbole. It confronts the plain meaning directly: the kolot at Sinai were visible. The midrash describes how each of the ten utterances (dibrot) issued from the mouth of the Holy One, split into seventy tongues simultaneously — one for every nation of the world — and that Israel could see these utterances as they went out and inscribed themselves upon the tablets. The hearing and the seeing were not two separate events. They were a single act of perception in which the categories of sense collapsed into one another.

Midrash Tanchuma (Yitro 13) amplifies this: "they saw what is normally heard and heard what is normally seen." This is not presented as paradox requiring resolution. It is the character of the event itself. Sinai was constitutively synaesthetic.

The Talmud (Shabbat 88b) preserves a further image: the voice of God at Sinai was structured so that each individual Israelite received it according to their capacity — the elders heard it one way, the young men another, the women another, the children another. A single utterance reached every person differently, which is why the verse uses the plural kolot (voices) rather than kol (a single voice). The divine speech ramified as it reached the human ear. And yet, the verse says, they saw it.

Synesthesia as Theological Claim

Why does this matter? Because the alternative reading — "they saw the lightning that accompanied the thunder" — makes perfectly ordinary sensory sense and strips the verse of its theological weight. The rabbis insist on the stranger reading because it carries a claim the ordinary reading cannot: that at Sinai, divine speech crossed the boundary between word and world.

To hear a commandment is to receive information: a rule, a prohibition, an obligation, a promise. To see it — to see the voice that speaks it — is something categorically different. It means the utterance is not merely informational. It has the status of a physical object. It exists in the world, not merely in the mind of the hearer. The stone tablets are the permanent residue of this literalized speech: words that became stone because the speech that produced them was already real in a way that ordinary speech is not.

The Yerushalmi Talmud (Shekalim 6:1) describes the letters of Torah as black fire written on white fire. The tablets were the solidification of a fire-word. And if the word was fire, and fire can be seen, then they saw the word — they saw the voice — because at Sinai the categories of word and thing had not yet separated.

The Lissajous Figure

Jules Antoine Lissajous, demonstrating acoustics for the French Académie des Sciences in the 1850s, discovered that if you compose two perpendicular oscillations — two pure frequencies — the resulting curve traces a shape that depends entirely on the ratio of the frequencies. A 1:1 ratio traces a circle or ellipse. A 2:1 ratio traces a figure-eight. A 3:2 ratio traces a three-lobed curve. A 5:3 ratio traces an interlocked star. The shapes are pure: they arise from nothing except the frequency ratio, from no physical material, from no artist's hand. Two invisible oscillations — two sounds — compose into a visible figure.

This is the precise structure of what happened at Sinai. Something heard — a frequency, a voice, a kol — became something seen. The Lissajous figure is that crossing made geometric: pure oscillation becoming pure shape. The voice acquires form. Exodus 20:15 and the Lissajous equation describe the same phenomenon in two different registers: sound becoming visible.

The Slowly Shifting Figure

The animation passes slowly through the simple integer frequency ratios — 1:1, 2:1, 3:2, 3:4, 5:3 — that produce closed, recognizable figures. When the ratio is exactly an integer fraction, the Lissajous curve closes on itself and repeats. When the ratio is mid-transition between two integer values, the curve never closes — it fills the available space, creating an unresolved, accumulating density.

The animation lives in this tension. It approaches each closed figure slowly, passes through it without stopping, and continues toward the next. The moment of closure is brief and quickly left behind. This is theologically apt: the moment of Sinai was the only moment in which the word of God was seen directly, and it could not be held. Moses had to return to the mountain a second time to retrieve what was broken by the golden calf. The figure forms and dissipates. The covenant was given and had to be given again.

Phosphor, Fire, and Memory

The phosphor trail — the slow accumulation and slow fading of the curve's history — evokes the fire at Sinai. Deuteronomy 4:11 describes the mountain burning "to the very heart of heaven," its fire still vivid in Israel's memory forty years later. The Talmud (Shabbat 88b) and Yerushalmi Talmud (Shekalim 6:1) preserve the image of Torah's letters as black fire on white fire. The persisting luminous trace on the screen is fire that does not immediately consume, that lingers past its moment of generation.

The trail also evokes the transmission of Torah. Each generation adds its readings and interpretations to the accumulated figure. The earlier strata do not vanish — they fade slowly, persisting beneath the newer strokes as context and ground. The full figure at any moment is a superposition of everything that has been drawn. This is the structure of the Oral Torah: every generation's voice laid over every prior voice, the original utterance still faintly visible beneath centuries of commentary.

The colors cycle from electric blue — the color of lightning — through white to amber and gold, evoking the lightning and fire of Exodus 20:15. At the bottom of the frame, the Hebrew text stands quietly: וְכָל-הָעָם רֹאִים אֶת-הַקּוֹלֹת. All the people saw the voices. The Lissajous figure is their testimony, rendered in light.
