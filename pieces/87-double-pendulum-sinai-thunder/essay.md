# And All the People Saw the Thunder

**וְכָל-הָעָם רֹאִים אֶת-הַקּוֹלֹת**

"And all the people *saw* the thunders" (Exodus 20:15).

The Hebrew is grammatically impossible. Thunder — *ha-kolot*, the voices — is something you hear. Sight requires light; sound requires nothing but air and distance. Yet the verse insists: *ro'im*, they *saw*. The Septuagint translates it conventionally, flattening the strangeness. But the rabbis take the impossibility literally, and what they find there is one of the most extraordinary claims in classical Jewish theology.

## The Mekhilta

The Mekhilta deRabbi Yishmael (Parashat Bachodesh, chapter 9) asks directly: how did they *see* what should only be *heard*? Its answer: God's voice subdivided at Sinai. Each Israelite received the revelation individually, calibrated to their capacity — the elders heard what elders could bear, the young heard what youth could absorb, and so on across the entire nation. The single divine voice was not broadcast uniformly but arrived at each soul as a personal transmission.

This is not a modern pluralist reading projected backward. It is the classical rabbinic interpretation, preserved in the oldest Tannaitic midrash. The strangeness of the verse — seeing thunder — is the textual trigger for a claim about individuation: revelation does not flatten its recipients into a uniform audience. Six hundred thousand people stood at the same mountain and heard different things.

## Chaos

Here is the physical analogue. A double pendulum is a deterministic system — its equations are perfectly known, its motion is fully caused. But two pendulums that begin from states indistinguishably close will, within seconds, trace wildly different paths. This is not randomness. It is sensitive dependence on initial conditions: the microscopic differences that no measurement can eliminate compound exponentially until the trajectories bear no resemblance to each other.

Seven pendulums begin from nearly the same angle — differing by just 0.002 radians each. Within the first cycle they are already diverging; by the third they trace entirely distinct paths — seven separate calligraphies of light written by the same initial motion. The same initial conditions, the same equations of motion, the same gravity: and yet seven utterly different histories.

## The Piece

What you see is seven revelations. They begin as one — the same starting angle, the same equations, the same moment of release — and become seven distinct signatures accumulating on the canvas until the image is a dense, luminous tangle. The same divine voice, arriving at seven hundred thousand receivers, each shaped by their own infinitesimally different initial conditions, each tracing their own unrepeatable path through the space of possible understanding.

The verse says *saw*. The Mekhilta says: each one according to their capacity. The mathematics says: divergence was inevitable from the first instant.
