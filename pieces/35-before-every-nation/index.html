Before Every Nation

The Torah Offered — and Refused — at the Gates of Each Kingdom

Long before Israel stood at the foot of Sinai, the Torah was offered elsewhere. This is not a marginal tradition. It is recorded in two major midrashic collections — Sifre Devarim, Piska 343 and Mechilta d'Rabbi Ishmael, Yitro, chapter 5 — and cited across rabbinic literature as the essential context for understanding what Israel's acceptance at Sinai truly meant.

The midrash unfolds in a precise sequence. God went first to the children of Esau, who asked: What is written in it? God replied: You shall not murder. They refused: their founding ancestor Esau was characterised by the sword. Their whole identity was structured around violence, and the Torah's prohibition of murder struck at the core of who they were.

Next God went to the children of Ammon and Moab. They too asked what was written. God said: You shall not commit adultery. They refused: their very origins were bound up with transgression. Then God went to the children of Ishmael. They asked their question and received the answer: You shall not steal. They refused: their ancestor was described as pera adam — a wild man whose hand would be against everyone. One by one, every nation was approached, and every nation found a commandment that cut too close.

Then God came to Israel. And Israel's answer was entirely different: נַעֲשֶׂה וְנִשְׁמָע — we will do and we will hear (Exodus 24:7). They did not ask what is written in it? They committed before knowing the terms.

The Talmud (Shabbat 88a) is thunderstruck by this response. When Israel said naaseh v'nishma — we will do and then we will understand — 600,000 angels descended and placed two crowns on each person's head, one for each word. A philosopher at court told Rav Ashi: you are a rash people, who put your mouths before your ears. Rav Ashi replied: we walked in perfect trust.

There is a deep tension here that the midrash does not resolve. The nations were not simply evil — they were honest. They saw what the Torah would require and knew they could not accept. Israel, by contrast, agreed without conditions: either extraordinary faith or reckless impulsiveness, depending on how you read it. But the midrash implies something heavier: Israel accepted the Torah on behalf of all the nations who refused. The obligation does not shrink; it expands. To be chosen is not to be privileged but to be held to a standard that every other people declined.

The rectangles that fill this canvas are those kingdoms. Each is sealed in its own colour — ochre, slate, terracotta, olive. One by one they dim, withdrawn into darkness, not destroyed but sealed off. What remains is a single glowing rectangle, which blooms into warm gold and holds the words naaseh v'nishma. Then the canvas resets and the question asks itself again.

Sources: Sifre Devarim, Piska 343; Mechilta d'Rabbi Ishmael, Yitro 5; Talmud Bavli, Shabbat 88a–88b; Exodus 24:7.
