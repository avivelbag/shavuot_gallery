# Before Every Nation

Long before Israel stood at the foot of Sinai, the Torah was offered elsewhere.
This is not a marginal tradition. It is recorded in two major midrashic
collections — **Sifre Devarim, Piska 343** and **Mechilta d'Rabbi Ishmael,
Yitro, chapter 5** — and cited across rabbinic literature as the essential
context for understanding what Israel's acceptance at Sinai truly meant.

The midrash unfolds in a precise sequence. God went first to the children of
Esau, who asked: *What is written in it?* God replied: *You shall not murder.*
They refused: their founding ancestor Esau was characterised by the sword. Their
whole identity was structured around violence, and the Torah's prohibition of
murder struck at the core of who they were.

Next God went to the children of Ammon and Moab. They too asked what was
written. God said: *You shall not commit adultery.* They refused: their very
origins (according to Genesis 19) were bound up with sexual transgression. The
commandment they could not accept was the one that named the fault line at their
foundations.

Then God went to the children of Ishmael. They asked their question and received
the answer: *You shall not steal.* They refused: their ancestor was described in
Genesis 16 as *pera adam* — a wild man whose hand would be against everyone.
Trade and raiding were woven into their story; the prohibition of theft was not a
commandment they could take on.

One by one, every nation was approached, and every nation found a commandment
that cut too close. The nations did not reject the Torah because they were evil
in some abstract sense. They rejected it because each nation had built its
character, its history, its self-understanding around a particular practice or
value that the Torah would have required it to surrender. They knew themselves
well enough to know they could not accept.

Then God came to Israel. And Israel's answer was entirely different: **נַעֲשֶׂה
וְנִשְׁמָע** — *we will do and we will hear* (Exodus 24:7). They did not ask
*what is written in it?* They committed before knowing the terms.

The Talmud (**Shabbat 88a**) is thunderstruck by this response. The text says
that when Israel said *naaseh v'nishma* — *we will do and then we will
understand* — 600,000 angels descended and placed two crowns on each person's
head, one for each word. The Talmud cites Rav Simai: how did Israel earn this
merit? Because they said *we will do* before *we will hear.* A philosopher at
court, hearing this later, told Rav Ashi: *you are a rash people, who put your
mouths before your ears.* Rav Ashi replied: we walked in perfect trust.

There is a deep tension here that the midrash does not resolve, because it
cannot be resolved. On one reading, the other nations were merely honest: they
looked at the Torah and saw that it would require them to change in ways they
were unwilling to change. Israel, by contrast, agreed without conditions —
which could be read as either extraordinary faith or reckless impulsiveness. On
another reading, Israel's unconditional acceptance is precisely what made the
covenant possible: you cannot enter a covenant if you are still negotiating the
terms. The marriage is only a marriage if you say yes before you have read every
clause of the contract.

But the midrash also generates an unsettling implication. If God offered the
Torah to every nation and every nation refused on the grounds that they could not
keep it, then Israel's acceptance carried the full weight of that offer. Israel
did not just receive the Torah — Israel received it on behalf of all the nations
who could not. The obligation does not shrink; it expands. To be chosen is not
to be privileged but to be held to a standard that every other people declined.

The visual structure of this piece encodes that refusal and acceptance. The
canvas is divided recursively into rectangular kingdoms, each sealed off in its
own colour. One by one, those kingdoms dim — not destroyed, but withdrawn,
sealed into their own darkness. What remains is a single rectangle, luminous and
central, that blooms into warm gold. The Hebrew words appear: *naaseh v'nishma*.
The animation loops endlessly, because the question the midrash poses does not
admit a permanent answer. Every generation stands again at Sinai, and every
generation must decide whether it will ask what is written in it, or whether it
will say *we will do* before it has finished reading.
