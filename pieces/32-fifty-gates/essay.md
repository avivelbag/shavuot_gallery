# The Fifty Gates — Bayer-Dithered Omer Calendar

*"There are fifty gates of Binah, and all of them were given to Moses except one." (Talmud Bavli, Rosh Hashanah 21b)*

---

The Omer count is a pilgrimage in time. Leviticus 23:15–16 commands:

> וּסְפַרְתֶּם לָכֶם מִמָּחֳרַת הַשַּׁבָּת מִיּוֹם הֲבִיאֲכֶם אֶת עֹמֶר הַתְּנוּפָה שֶׁבַע שַׁבָּתוֹת תְּמִימֹת תִּהְיֶינָה׃ עַד מִמָּחֳרַת הַשַּׁבָּת הַשְּׁבִיעִת תִּסְפְּרוּ חֲמִשִּׁים יוֹם וְהִקְרַבְתֶּם מִנְחָה חֲדָשָׁה לַיהוָה׃
>
> "You shall count for yourselves from the day after the Sabbath, from the day when you bring the wave-offering sheaf — seven complete weeks they shall be; until the day after the seventh Sabbath you shall count fifty days." (Leviticus 23:15–16)

The destination is Shavuot, the fiftieth day, the giving of Torah at Sinai.

Shavuot is unique among the major festivals in one striking way: it has no fixed date in the Torah. Passover falls on the fifteenth of Nisan. Sukkot on the fifteenth of Tishrei. Rosh HaShanah on the first of Tishrei. But Shavuot is identified only as "the day after seven weeks" — it is defined entirely by counting, not by the calendar. The rabbis noticed this and drew a theological conclusion: the fiftieth day cannot be fixed in advance. It can only be arrived at by counting. The festival is not a point on a map; it is the end of a journey. You cannot mark it on the calendar and simply arrive. You must count your way to it, day by day.

The Talmud (Rosh Hashanah 21b) speaks of fifty gates of *Binah* — divine understanding, the second sefirah, associated with the womb of creation, the generative intelligence from which all lower forms of wisdom flow. Moses, the greatest of prophets, received forty-nine of these gates. The fiftieth gate — the threshold of God's own understanding — remained beyond any human's reach. Tractate Nedarim (38a) returns to this theme, noting that Moses was distinguished above all other human beings, the receiver of Torah itself — yet even he attained only forty-nine of the fifty gates. The structure of the Omer count encodes this limit. We count forty-nine days; on the fiftieth, we arrive. But we do not cross. We receive Torah; we do not receive God. The gate opens toward us, but the threshold is not ours to step over.

This is the non-obvious theological move: the rabbis read Shavuot's undated status not as an oversight but as a statement. The festival that commemorates the giving of the Torah — the moment of maximum divine-human encounter — is also the moment defined only as arrival. It has no independent calendar position; it is entirely constituted by the counting. You cannot know when you will arrive at Shavuot; you can only count until you are there.

This piece renders the forty-nine-day count as Bayer ordered dithering, a technique in which a threshold matrix determines which pixels light up at a given luminance level. The standard 4×4 Bayer matrix — sixteen threshold values arranged in a precise dispersed pattern — produces mathematically optimal distribution of lit and unlit pixels at any luminance between 0 and 1. At luminance 1/50 (day 1), only the single lowest threshold is exceeded: one pixel of sixteen lights up, a lone golden point in a field of indigo. At luminance 2/50, two pixels light. The pattern grows in steps, each new threshold crossed illuminating one more pixel per 4×4 tile, building a progressively brighter grid of gold.

The dithering technique is a literal enactment of threshold-crossing. At each pixel position, the question is binary: does the luminance value exceed the local threshold? This mirrors exactly how the Talmud describes the Omer experience — each day is a gate, a threshold. The threshold is either crossed or it is not. Forty-nine thresholds are crossed, forty-nine gates opened, forty-nine days counted. On the fiftieth, the threshold is not crossed by us — it opens itself.

The Bayer matrix is also the most *ordered* arrangement of dithering thresholds possible. Ordered dithering — as opposed to error-diffusion dithering or random stippling — places each new lit pixel at the position of maximum visual distance from all already-lit pixels. The pattern is not chaotic; it expands in a mathematically defined sequence toward uniform coverage. This too mirrors the Omer: the preparation for Sinai was not random. The kabbalists mapped each of the forty-nine days onto a precise coordinate in the 7×7 grid of the *sefirot* — seven qualities of soul, each refined seven times. The spiritual expansion toward the fiftieth gate follows an inner logic, crossing thresholds in an ordered sequence.

The animation materializes each cell column by column, left to right, over a quarter second — a single day of counting rendered as a sweep of light across one square in the grid. The full pattern emerging in each cell is not random; it is the Bayer matrix itself, the most ordered arrangement of dithering thresholds possible: clarity emerging from structure, light from law, fifty times.
