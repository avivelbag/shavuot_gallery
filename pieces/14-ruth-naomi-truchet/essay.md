# Wherever You Go — Ruth and Naomi

*"Wherever you go, I will go; wherever you lodge, I will lodge; your people shall be my people, and your God my God." — Ruth 1:16*

---

The Book of Ruth is one of the shortest books in the Hebrew Bible, but its reach is immense. It is read on Shavuot — the feast of weeks, the festival of harvest and covenant — for reasons that illuminate both the agricultural season and the theological depth of the holiday.

The story begins in grief. Naomi, an Israelite woman, has lost her husband and both her sons in the land of Moab. Bereaved, she prepares to return to Bethlehem in Judah, where the barley harvest has begun, and she urges her two daughters-in-law to return to their own people. One does. Ruth does not.

Ruth's declaration in Ruth 1:16 stands as one of the most celebrated expressions of covenantal loyalty in all of scripture: *Wherever you go, I will go. Wherever you lodge, I will lodge. Your people shall be my people, and your God my God.* She is not merely accompanying a mother-in-law on a difficult journey. She is making a declaration of belonging — to a people, to a land, to a God. The Moabite foreigner claims Israel as her own.

The rabbinic tradition heard in these words a paradigm of sincere conversion. The Talmud (Yevamot 47b) teaches that when accepting a convert, one should describe the hardships of Jewish life — yet if the candidate persists in spite of this, one should welcome them. Ruth's insistence in the face of Naomi's explicit discouragement became the classic case. She persists three times. Her declaration at Ruth 1:16 is the moment of spiritual transformation made visible in language.

The harvest setting is not incidental. The two women arrive in Bethlehem "at the beginning of the barley harvest" (Ruth 1:22), which is precisely when the Omer count begins — the forty-nine days between Passover and Shavuot. The story of Ruth unfolds during those weeks of counting. When she gleans in the fields of Boaz, she enters the agricultural cycle that Shavuot consecrates. The holiday of the first fruits of the wheat harvest is the culmination of the narrative's time-frame. The story ends at harvest; Shavuot is the harvest feast.

The connection to Sinai runs deeper still. The rabbis notice a resonance between Ruth's declaration — "your people shall be my people, and your God my God" — and Israel's own declaration at Sinai: *Na'aseh v'nishma*, "we will do and we will hear" (Exodus 24:7). Both are covenantal acceptances made freely, without coercion. Israel accepted the Torah at Sinai; Ruth accepted Israel's covenant on the road from Moab to Bethlehem. Shavuot celebrates both acceptances simultaneously. The Midrash (Ruth Rabbah 2:14) presses this connection further: Ruth's arrival is understood as completing, in the individual, what the nation accepted collectively at the mountain.

This piece renders that journey as a truchet tiling — a grid of square cells, each filled with one of two arc variants, producing an organic flowing pattern of continuous curves across the canvas. The left side carries the muted earth tones of Moab: ochre, sienna, dust. The right side opens into the warmer, greener palette of Bethlehem: barley gold, leaf green, pale straw. The two palettes are not divided by a hard line but by a gradient, because the road between them is not a wall but a web — an interlocking network of curves that joins wherever you are to wherever you are going.

Within the tile pattern, two silhouette figures walk toward the right. The taller is Naomi, returning home. The smaller is Ruth, choosing home. Between them and behind them: Moab. Before them: Bethlehem, and harvest.

*See: Ruth 1:16, 1:22; Exodus 24:7; Talmud Bavli Yevamot 47b; Midrash Ruth Rabbah 2:14.*
