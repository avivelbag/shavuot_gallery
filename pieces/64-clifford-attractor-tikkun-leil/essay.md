# Until the Morning Light — Clifford Attractor

Every year on the eve of Shavuot, communities across the Jewish world stay awake through the entire night, studying Torah from nightfall until dawn. This practice — Tikkun Leil Shavuot, "the repair of the night of Shavuot" — is first codified in the Zohar, Parashat Emor (III, 98a), where it declares that the pious ones of old would not sleep on this night, occupying themselves instead with the words of Torah, Prophets, and Writings.

The specific reason given by the Magen Avraham (Orach Chaim 494) is rooted in a Midrashic tradition: on the morning when God descended upon Sinai to give the Torah, the Israelites were asleep. Moses was forced to wake them for the appointed moment of revelation. The Song of Songs preserves an echo of this failure in a verse the rabbis read as Israel's voice on that morning:

> **אֲנִי יְשֵׁנָה וְלִבִּי עֵר קוֹל דּוֹדִי דוֹפֵק פִּתְחִי-לִי אֲחֹתִי רַעְיָתִי יוֹנָתִי תַמָּתִי**
>
> "I sleep, but my heart is awake: it is the voice of my beloved who knocks: Open to me, my sister, my love, my dove, my undefiled." (Song of Songs 5:2)

The "beloved" is the divine call; the sleeper is Israel, not ready at the threshold. Tikkun Leil Shavuot is the annual repair of that unreadiness — a vow never to sleep through the arrival of dawn again.

But a deeper kabbalistic reason underlies the practice. In the Pri Etz Chaim (Shaar Chag HaShavuot, ch. 1), Rabbi Chayyim Vital records the teaching of the Ari (Rabbi Isaac Luria of Safed, 16th century) that the night of Shavuot is the wedding night between Israel and the Torah — and between the Holy One, blessed be He, and the Shekhinah. The Tikkun anthology — a fixed selection of passages spanning every book of the Torah and Tanakh — is recited to "adorn the bride," to prepare the divine union with the full garment of Israel's sacred texts. Those who complete the Tikkun are said to become the groomsmen of the bride.

## This Piece

The Clifford attractor plots millions of points, each step determined by four parameters and two sinusoidal equations: *x' = sin(a·y) + c·cos(a·x), y' = sin(b·x) + d·cos(b·y)*. No single point predicts where the cloud will form. But over eight million iterations — the number plotted here, the approximate number of letters in a complete Torah scroll — the attractor reveals a luminous figure-of-eight: two lobes of concentrated light suggesting the two tablets, emerging from the deep navy darkness like a long-exposure photograph of a turning night sky.

The piece builds the way a Tikkun Leil Shavuot unfolds: one step after another through the night, each iteration invisible in isolation, until the full form appears with the light of dawn. The Hebrew label at the bottom — עַד אוֹר הַבֹּקֶר, "until the morning light" — fades in only when all eight million points have been placed.
