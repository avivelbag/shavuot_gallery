We Will Do and We Will Hear

נַעֲשֶׂה וְנִשְׁמַע · Naaseh V'Nishma / Matan Torah

The Unconditional Acceptance

"And Moses took the book of the covenant and read it in the hearing of the people; and they said: All that the Lord has spoken we will do and we will hear." (Exodus 24:7)

The sequence is strange. In ordinary human reasoning, one hears before one obeys. You listen to the terms; you understand them; only then do you agree. But at the moment of the covenant's sealing, Israel reversed the order. They said naaseh — we will do — before v'nishma — and we will hear. They committed to obedience before they knew what it would require.

The Talmud (Shabbat 88a) takes this reversal with full seriousness. When Israel pronounced those six syllables, sixty thousand ministering angels descended and placed two crowns — tagin — on each Israelite: one crown for each of the two words, naaseh and nishma. Acting before understanding. Commitment before comprehension. The Sages read this not as recklessness but as the highest form of trust — a relationship so foundational that Israel could receive its obligations without first auditing them.

The Tagin

The word tag (plural: tagin) refers to the small flourishes — decorative "crowns" — that scribes traditionally add to certain letters in a Torah scroll. They appear as tiny upward strokes above the letterforms. In kabbalistic tradition these markings are not decorative: each tag carries layers of meaning that the basic consonant alone cannot hold. The letters of Torah already contain infinite depth; the tagin encode depths beyond that.

When the Talmud imagines Israel wearing these crowns as a result of saying naaseh v'nishma, the imagery is dense. The people become like the letters themselves, bearing meaning they have not yet fully deciphered — ornamented with knowledge not yet understood, resonating with dimensions not yet heard.

The sin of the golden calf took those crowns away. But the declaration that earned them was not erased.

The Shatter of Meaning

This piece renders the two words as they appear in a Torah: with their full nikud — the vowel points and cantillation markers that scribes have carried across millennia — נַעֲשֶׂה וְנִשְׁמַע. But instead of displaying them as intact, bounded letterforms, the piece shatters each word into a Voronoi diagram: a partition of the plane into cells, each cell belonging to the seed point nearest to it. Every cell is a facet. Every facet catches the light at a slightly different angle.

The seeds inside the letter-shapes glow with warm gold and parchment — the tones of illuminated manuscripts, the color of fire on the mountain. The seeds outside recede into deep indigo, the color of the night sky above Sinai. At the boundary between inside and outside, a thin white line marks where the letter ends and the world begins — or where the word breaks open into its meaning.

A slow pulse of brightness moves outward from the center, as if the utterance is still resonating through the plane. The rabbis suggest that the moment of naaseh v'nishma has never quite stopped. The crowns were taken, but the act of placing commitment before comprehension remains embedded in Jewish memory as the defining gesture of the covenant. Something broke open at that moment. The Voronoi cells show the pieces — each one still warm.
