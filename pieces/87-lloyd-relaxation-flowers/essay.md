# Adorned with Roses: A Shavuot Garden

## The Rama's Ruling

The Rama — Rabbi Moshe Isserles — records in *Orach Chaim* 494:3: "The custom is to strew fragrant grasses in the synagogue and the home in honor of the festival." On Shavuot, synagogues across the Jewish world fill with cut flowers, fragrant herbs, and greenery. The custom is widespread enough that it has come to define the holiday's sensory texture alongside the dairy foods and the long night of learning. But why flowers? The Rama gestures to a reason — "in memory of Mount Sinai" — and the deeper source runs through the midrashim.

## The Mountain in Bloom

Both *Shir HaShirim Rabbah* (1:2) and *Pirkei DeRabbi Eliezer* (ch. 41) record the same tradition: when the Holy One descended upon Har Sinai to give the Torah, the mountain burst into bloom. Flowers appeared at its foot at the very moment of revelation. The barren wilderness was transformed, however briefly, into a garden. The world itself blossomed in recognition of what was being transmitted.

The Talmud in *Shabbat* 88b adds another layer: at the moment Israel pronounced *naaseh v'nishma* — "we will do and we will hear" — the very angels descended to crown each Israelite with roses and myrtles. The people became a garden; the covenant was sealed in flowers.

## Re-Enactment, Not Decoration

Here is the non-obvious insight: filling the synagogue with flowers on Shavuot is not decoration. It is a re-enactment. Each year, by filling the space of prayer with blooms, the community recreates the miraculous landscape of Sinai — making the moment of Torah-giving spatially present, generation by generation. The flowers do not merely adorn the room. They transform it into the mountain.

The custom thus carries the same logic as the Passover Seder, where one is commanded to see oneself as if personally leaving Egypt. On Shavuot, the synagogue becomes Sinai. The flowers prove it.

## The Algorithm as Analogy

Lloyd's algorithm enacts the same principle. From scattered seed points — placed randomly like wildflowers across an unplanted field — Voronoi cells form and centroids are computed, iteration by iteration, until the entire plane self-organizes into a perfectly balanced floral tessellation. What begins as chaos converges to perfect order: each seed at the heart of its own territory, the wilderness become a garden.

The wilderness before Sinai was formless — a people without structure, without covenant. Revelation fell, and order emerged not by imposition but by convergence: each soul finding its place in the architecture of the divine word. The flowers do not merely fill the space. They organize it.
