# And the Mountain Burned — de Jong Attractor

The theophany at Sinai begins with weather and becomes something else entirely:

> **וַיְהִי בַיּוֹם הַשְּׁלִישִׁי בִּהְיֹת הַבֹּקֶר וַיְהִי קֹלֹת וּבְרָקִים וְעָנָן כָּבֵד עַל-הָהָר וְקֹל שֹׁפָר חָזָק מְאֹד וַיֶּחֱרַד כָּל-הָעָם אֲשֶׁר בַּמַּחֲנֶה**
>
> "And it came to pass on the third day, when it was morning, that there were thunders and lightnings and a thick cloud upon the mountain, and the voice of the shofar exceedingly loud; so that all the people who were in the camp trembled." (Exodus 19:16)

By verse 18, the mountain is entirely consumed:

> **וְהַר סִינַי עָשַׁן כֻּלּוֹ מִפְּנֵי אֲשֶׁר יָרַד עָלָיו יְהֹוָה בָּאֵשׁ וַיַּעַל עֲשָׁנוֹ כְּעֶשֶׁן הַכִּבְשָׁן וַיֶּחֱרַד כָּל-הָהָר מְאֹד**
>
> "And Mount Sinai was all in smoke, because the LORD descended upon it in fire; and the smoke thereof ascended as the smoke of a furnace, and the whole mountain quaked greatly." (Exodus 19:18)

The phrase **כְּעֶשֶׁן הַכִּבְשָׁן** — "like the smoke of a furnace" — is not a simile deployed for rhetorical effect. The Talmud (Shabbat 88b) records that the mountain was literally uprooted and held suspended above the heads of the people as a cosmic ultimatum: receive the Torah, or be buried beneath it. The fire was real; the simile understates it.

But this scene was already anticipated when God led Israel out of Egypt. There, the Torah establishes a duality that runs through the entire wilderness narrative:

> **וַיהֹוָה הֹלֵךְ לִפְנֵיהֶם יוֹמָם בְּעַמּוּד עָנָן לַנְחֹתָם הַדֶּרֶךְ וְלַיְלָה בְּעַמּוּד אֵשׁ לְהָאִיר לָהֶם לָלֶכֶת יוֹמָם וָלָיְלָה**
>
> "And the LORD went before them by day in a pillar of cloud to lead them in the way; and by night in a pillar of fire to give them light; that they might go by day and by night." (Exodus 13:21)

Cloud by day, fire by night — the same divine presence in two distinct registers. Rashi explains that the cloud served specifically so Israel could approach: the opacity that appears to conceal is precisely what makes encounter possible. Without the cloud, the fire alone would be unapproachable. The form of hiddenness is the form of accessibility.

## This Piece

The de Jong strange attractor is defined by four numbers and a two-line rule:
*x' = sin(a·y) − cos(b·x), y' = sin(c·x) − cos(d·y)*. No single point reveals where the next will land. The iteration is deterministic — the same starting position always produces the same sequence — yet the cloud that emerges over a million iterations could not have been read from the rule. Local obedience produces global irreducibility. This is what makes the attractor *strange*: determinism without predictability, law without foreknowledge.

The revelation at Sinai has this quality. Israel was told to prepare for three days. They knew something was coming. Yet nothing in their prior experience — not the ten plagues, not the crossing of the sea — constituted a framework for what actually occurred. The forty-nine gates of understanding that Moses possessed gave no advance picture of the moment. Revelation arrived as the attractor settles: by lawful iteration, producing something genuinely unprecedented.

Three parameter presets cycle here over roughly thirty seconds, each generating a different cloud shape — a tall flame column, a wide billowing mass, a narrow pillar — before dissolving into the next. They are the fire by night and the cloud by day: the same four-parameter grammar, different aspects, one underlying fire.
