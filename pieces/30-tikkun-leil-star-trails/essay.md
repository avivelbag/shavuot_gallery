# The Night Turns Toward Morning

The custom of staying awake all night on the eve of Shavuot, immersed in Torah
study until dawn, is known as *Tikkun Leil Shavuot* — the repair, or
preparation, of Shavuot night. It is among the most distinctive practices the
kabbalists bequeathed to Jewish life, and its authority rests on a single
luminous passage in the Zohar.

The Zohar, that foundational text of Jewish mysticism, speaks in **Parashat
Emor** of the night before the giving of the Torah. The ancient *chasidim* —
the pious ones — it says, would not sleep on that night, staying awake to
"adorn the bride." In Kabbalistic language, the Shekhinah (God's indwelling
presence, identified with the sefirah of Malkhut) is the Bride; Israel and the
Torah constitute the groom; and the night of Shavuot is the wedding night in
which the celestial marriage is consummated at Sinai. To stay awake and study
is to act as an usher preparing the bridal canopy of the divine union.

The kabbalists of sixteenth-century Safed formalized this midnight vigil into a
structured curriculum — the *Tikkun* — drawing passages from every book of the
Hebrew Bible, every tractate of the Mishnah, and selections from the Zohar
itself. Among those who observed it most famously was **Rabbi Joseph Karo**, the
great legal codifier and author of the *Shulchan Aruch*. Karo kept a private
mystical diary, the *Maggid Meisharim*, in which he recorded that during one
Tikkun Leil vigil a heavenly voice — attributed to the Mishnah personified as
the Shekhinah — spoke through him, praising the circle's all-night study and
confirming the practice's cosmic efficacy. His companion that night, Rabbi
Shlomo Alkabetz, wrote a famous letter describing the experience, which
circulated among Safed's scholars and helped establish the Tikkun as essential
Shavuot practice across the Jewish world.

Tikkun Leil turns time itself into a spiritual act. The forty-nine days of the
Omer count — the weeks of refinement and preparation that begin at Passover —
reach their culmination not at dawn on the fiftieth day, but in the night that
precedes it. All those days collapse into a single dark span between sunset and
sunrise, and study becomes the labor of a night-worker transforming darkness
into the light it is waiting for.

There is, embedded in the tradition, a note of comic self-reproach. The Talmud
records, and the **Magen Avraham** in **Orach Chayyim 494** cites this as the
direct reason for the all-night vigil, that when the Israelites camped at Sinai
on the night before the giving of the Torah, they overslept. Moses had to rouse
them in the morning. This is the original sin of Shavuot: the nation for whom
the entire cosmos waited — for whom, the Talmud says, creation itself was
conditional on their receiving the Torah — slept through the alarm. Tikkun Leil
is the annual corrective, the communal determination never again to be asleep
when dawn comes.

In this piece the sky performs exactly that arc. From deep midnight blue through
the violet of astronomical twilight, the rose of nautical dawn, and finally to
the amber and gold of sunrise — the sixty-second loop traces one Tikkun night in
compressed time. The stars rotate slowly around the pole star, each leaving a
short luminous trail, marking not just space but duration: these arcs are time
made visible, the night sky's quiet turning as the students below keep watch.
The phrase inscribed at the lower edge — **עַד-עֲלוֹת הַשַּׁחַר**, *until the
rise of the dawn* — is taken from the Zohar's own description of the Tikkun
vigil. It is the night's destination and its promise: that if you can keep your
eyes open long enough, morning will eventually come.
