The Night Turns Toward Morning

Tikkun Leil Shavuot

The custom of staying awake all night on the eve of Shavuot, immersed in Torah study until dawn, is known as Tikkun Leil Shavuot — the repair, or preparation, of Shavuot night. It is among the most distinctive practices the kabbalists bequeathed to Jewish life, and its authority rests on a single luminous passage in the Zohar.

The Zohar, that foundational text of Jewish mysticism, speaks in Parashat Emor of the night before the giving of the Torah. The ancient chasidim — the pious ones — it says, would not sleep on that night, staying awake to "adorn the bride." In Kabbalistic language, the Shekhinah is the Bride; Israel and the Torah constitute the groom; and the night of Shavuot is the wedding night in which the celestial marriage is consummated at Sinai. To stay awake and study is to act as an usher preparing the bridal canopy of the divine union.

The kabbalists of sixteenth-century Safed formalized this midnight vigil into a structured curriculum — the Tikkun — drawing passages from every book of the Hebrew Bible, every tractate of the Mishnah, and selections from the Zohar itself. Among those who observed it most famously was Rabbi Joseph Karo, the great legal codifier and author of the Shulchan Aruch. Karo's private mystical diary, the Maggid Meisharim, records that during one Tikkun Leil vigil a heavenly voice — attributed to the Mishnah personified as the Shekhinah — spoke through him, praising the circle's all-night study and confirming the practice's cosmic efficacy.

Tikkun Leil turns time itself into a spiritual act. The forty-nine days of the Omer count reach their culmination not at dawn on the fiftieth day, but in the night that precedes it. All those days collapse into a single dark span between sunset and sunrise, and study becomes the labor of a night-worker transforming darkness into the light it is waiting for.

There is, embedded in the tradition, a note of self-reproach. The Magen Avraham in Orach Chayyim 494 records that when the Israelites camped at Sinai on the night before the giving of the Torah, they overslept. Moses had to rouse them in the morning. This is the original failing of Shavuot: the nation for whom creation itself waited slept through the alarm. Tikkun Leil is the annual corrective — the communal determination never again to be asleep when dawn comes.

In this piece the sky performs that arc. From deep midnight blue through the violet of astronomical twilight, the rose of nautical dawn, and finally to amber and gold sunrise — the sixty-second loop traces one Tikkun night in compressed time. The stars rotate around the pole star, each leaving a short luminous trail: these arcs are time made visible, the quiet turning of the night sky as the students below keep watch. The phrase inscribed at the lower edge — עַד-עֲלוֹת הַשַּׁחַר, until the rise of the dawn — is taken from the Zohar's own description of the vigil.
