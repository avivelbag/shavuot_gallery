613 Paths

Force-Directed Network of the Mitzvot

דָּרַשׁ רַבִּי שִׂמְלַאי: שֵׁשׁ מֵאוֹת וּשְׁלֹשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה מִצְווֹת נֶאֶמְרוּ לְמֹשֶׁה בְּסִינַי

"Rabbi Simlai expounded: Six hundred and thirteen commandments were declared to Moses at Sinai."

— Talmud Bavli, Makkot 23b

The Number That the Torah Itself Never States

The figure 613 is among the most-quoted facts about the Torah. Every bar mitzvah speech repeats it; the opening letters of "TaRYaG" (ת״רי״ג) are stamped on rabbinic memory. And yet the Torah never once says "613." The number is entirely a rabbinic construction — a retrospective enumeration, first explicitly stated by Rabbi Simlai in a homiletical context in the Babylonian Talmud, probably no earlier than the third century CE.

This is not a scandal but a feature. The tradition is here reading itself back to its own source, counting up what it has received and finding a number charged with meaning. Rabbi Simlai immediately continues: "365 negative commandments, corresponding to the days of the solar year; and 248 positive commandments, corresponding to the limbs of the human body." The number 613 is therefore not merely a count — it is a cosmological claim. The law fits the world exactly. The human body is shaped by what it is commanded to do; the calendar is structured by what it is commanded to avoid. Torah and creation are commensurate.

The Talmud credits the 248 evarim (limbs) to a received anatomical tradition — Oholot 1:8 lists them in detail — and the 365 days to the solar calendar which the Jewish year does not formally use but which represents the rhythms of the created world. The midrashic effect is profound: even if you count neither the limbs correctly nor the commandments correctly, the structural rhyme insists that the moral law and the physical world share a grammar.

The Debate Over Which 613

Once Rabbi Simlai's number was accepted, a new problem arose: which 613? The Torah contains many hundreds of verses with imperative or prohibitive force, and the question of which verses constitute discrete commandments — and which are specifications, repetitions, or applications of broader principles — is itself a major legal-philosophical enterprise.

Maimonides (Rabbi Moshe ben Maimon, 1138–1204) dedicated his Sefer HaMitzvot specifically to this question, establishing fourteen root-principles (shorashim) for what counts as a commandment. He excluded rabbinic ordinances, excluded commandments whose observance was only possible while the Temple stood (unless they were generative of ongoing obligations), and excluded narrative passages even when they carried normative force. The result was a list organized by polarity (positive / negative) and then, in his great legal code the Mishneh Torah, by subject-matter, across fourteen books.

Rabbi Moshe of Coucy (the Semag, 13th c.) disagreed on several items. Nachmanides (Rabbi Moshe ben Nachman, 1194–1270) wrote an extensive critique of Maimonides' root-principles. The anonymous 13th-century Sefer HaChinuch — attributed to a rabbi of Barcelona — gave each of Maimonides' 613 a systematic treatment: definition, rationale, relevant laws, who is obligated, and when it applies. The debate never closed. The 613 is a commitment of the tradition to a particular way of reading — one that insists on totality, on system, on the coextension of law with life and world.

Force-Directed Layout as Midrash

The artwork renders all 613 commandments as glowing nodes on a dark field — 248 warm harvest-gold points for the mitzvot aseh (positive commandments), 365 deep violet points for the mitzvot lo ta'aseh (negative commandments). Within each group, nodes are connected by thin edges whenever they share the same of Maimonides' fourteen books in the Mishneh Torah.

The force-directed layout runs a spring simulation: intra-book edges attract, global repulsion pushes nodes apart until forces balance. No external authority assigns the final positions — the clustering emerges from the structure of the relationships alone. This is, deliberately, a way of reading the tradition back at itself. The grouping of commandments into books, into categories, into families — the project of Maimonides, of the Chinuch, of all systematic halakhic literature — was itself an organic process. The force-directed layout shows what happens when the commandments are given only each other to push and pull against: they cohere into clusters without a single hand imposing the grouping. Law as a self-organizing field.

Shavuot as the Moment of Receiving

Shavuot commemorates the Matan Torah, the Giving of the Torah at Sinai. But what exactly was given? The two tablets of the Decalogue, certainly. According to the Talmud (Berakhot 5a), the entire Written Torah was given, and the Oral Torah as well — every rabbinic interpretation that would ever be offered, already latent in the transmission. The 613, then, were given at Sinai in the sense that they were implicit in what was given, waiting to be articulated by Rabbi Simlai, elaborated by Maimonides, arranged by the Chinuch, debated by every posek since.

The force-directed network is an image of that latency: 613 points of light, their final arrangement not set in advance, emergent from the bonds between them. At frame 0 they scatter at random. By frame 120 the clusters have formed. Then the ambient drift sets in — each node receiving a tiny random nudge each frame, a slow breathing quality, the network resting but never fully still. The law lives.
