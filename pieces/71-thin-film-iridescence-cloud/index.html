The Cloud That Would Not Lift

Exodus 19:16–19, 24:15–18 · thin-film interference / WebGL fragment shader

וַיְהִי בַיּוֹם הַשְּׁלִישִׁי בִּהְיֹת הַבֹּקֶר וַיְהִי קֹלֹת וּבְרָקִים וְעָנָן כָּבֵד עַל-הָהָר
On the third day, as morning dawned, there was thunder and lightning and a heavy cloud upon the mountain — Exodus 19:16

On the third day of Israel's preparation at Sinai — the day God had appointed for revelation — a thick cloud descended on the mountain. Exodus 19:16–19 records what happened at dawn: thunder, lightning, and a dense cloud over the mountain; then the sound of a shofar, loud and growing louder, so that all the people in the camp trembled. God descended onto Sinai in fire, and the smoke of the mountain rose like the smoke of a furnace. The shofar blast continued to intensify. The whole mountain quaked violently.

Then Moses ascended. And the cloud received him.

Exodus 24:15–18 gives the second account, slower and stranger. The cloud covered the mountain for six days. Six days of waiting. On the seventh day, God called to Moses from within the cloud — and Moses entered it and went up the mountain. He remained there forty days and forty nights. To the Israelites watching from below, the cloud appeared as esh ochelet, consuming fire, at the summit of the mountain.

Concealing and Revealing

The theological paradox of the cloud is this: it conceals and reveals at the same moment. Moses cannot see God's face and live (Exodus 33:20), so the cloud functions as a screen. But it is precisely this screen that Moses must enter to receive the Torah. The cloud is not an obstacle to the revelation — it is the medium of the revelation. The Presence dwells within it (Exodus 40:34: the cloud fills the Tabernacle and the glory of God fills it entirely). God tells Moses explicitly in Exodus 19:9: "I will come to you in a thick cloud so that the people will hear when I speak with you." The cloud is chosen — not as a substitute for direct encounter but as the form that direct encounter takes under the conditions of mortal existence.

Color Without Pigment

The iridescence of thin-film interference is the correct physical metaphor for this. An iridescent surface — a soap bubble, an oil slick, the wing of a morpho butterfly — produces its colors not from any pigment or dye. There is no substance in the film that is violet, or gold, or green. The color emerges entirely from the interference of light with itself: waves reflecting from the top and bottom surfaces of the film, arriving slightly out of phase, canceling some wavelengths and reinforcing others. The color is in the light, not in any material thing, exactly as the Presence was in the cloud without the cloud being divine.

The shifting colors are driven by the film's thickness — a spatial variation invisible to the naked eye. Thicker regions cancel different wavelengths than thinner ones, producing a continuously flowing spectrum across the surface. The beauty comes from a structure you cannot see directly. This is precisely how the cloud worked: the revelation was real, the Presence was genuine, but the structure behind it was hidden — accessible only to the one who entered, and even then only partially, in smoke and fire and the growing sound of the shofar.

The Physics of the Artwork

The artwork renders this: a thickness field slowly undulating in time, each pixel's color determined by which wavelengths of visible light constructively interfere at that film depth. Constructive interference occurs when 2·n·d·cos(θ) = m·λ, where d is the film thickness, n is the refractive index (1.45, that of water and soap), and λ is the wavelength. A fragment shader evaluates this across eight wavelength samples spanning the visible spectrum, converts the resulting intensities to CIE XYZ color space using precomputed observer weights, and then to sRGB for display. The thickness field is driven by two octaves of sinusoidal harmonics — a Perlin-like noise computed entirely in the shader, no library required.

Violet pools thicken into blue, green edges warm to gold, orange drifts toward red and back again. The cloud would not lift. The fire at the summit was consuming and illuminating at once. The colors shift because the structure shifts — and the structure you cannot see.

Sources: Exodus 19:9, 19:16–19, 24:15–18, 33:20, 40:34; CIE 1931 standard colorimetric observer (2°).
