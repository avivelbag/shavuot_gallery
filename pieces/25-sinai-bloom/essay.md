# The Mountain in Bloom — Sinai's Flowering at Revelation

*Sources: Exodus 19:18–19; Shir HaShirim Rabbah 1:12; Pirkei DeRabbi Eliezer ch. 41; Shulchan Aruch Orach Chaim 494:3.*

---

Exodus 19:18–19 gives us the canonical theophany: the mountain wrapped in smoke, the Lord descending in fire, the whole mountain trembling greatly. The shofar blast grew louder and louder without ceasing. Thunder, lightning, thick cloud pressing down. This is the Sinai of collective memory — overwhelming, terrifying, magnificent. This is the encounter that Israel was barely able to survive.

But there is another Sinai preserved in Midrash that most readers never encounter.

Midrash Tehillim on Psalm 68:17 preserves a remarkable tradition: the mountains competed to host the revelation. Tabor came from the west. Carmel arrived from the coast. Each one presented its height, its grandeur, its historical credentials. The Holy One, blessed be He, rejected them all. Sinai was chosen — and Pirkei DeRabbi Eliezer (ch. 41) explains why: precisely because Sinai did not trumpet its own greatness. Every other mountain raised itself up. Sinai alone did not. Its humility made it worthy. The smallest mountain received the greatest gift.

And then there is *Shir HaShirim Rabbah* 1:12. In this midrash, at the very moment of revelation — not before, not after, but *at* the moment the covenant was given — Mount Sinai burst into flower. The mountain was covered in grass and blooming plants. The fire came down not onto bare desert rock but onto a living, flowering field.

This juxtaposition is not decorative. The rabbis were making a deliberate theological claim: Torah is both terrible and life-giving. The moment that contains fire, smoke, and unendurable sound also contains wild plants reaching toward the sky. These two realities are not contradictory. They are two aspects of the same encounter. Torah is overwhelming in power, but it is also the source of flourishing. To receive it is to be changed in the way soil is changed by rain.

The Shulchan Aruch (Orach Chaim 494:3, codified by the Rama) records the Ashkenazic custom of decorating homes and synagogues with greenery and flowers on Shavuot. Some later authorities discouraged the practice as resembling non-Jewish seasonal festivals, but the midrashic grounding is clear: the decoration enacts the mountain's flowering. Every Shavuot, we bring the bloom indoors so we do not forget that Torah came to a blooming mountain, not a barren one.

This animation renders both truths simultaneously. L-system branching rules — the same recursive logic that governs how real plants undergo morphogenesis — unfold from a single axiom into full botanical form, plants rising incrementally from the slopes of the dark mountain against an indigo sky. The growth takes time; the viewer watches it happen. Then, as the blooms crown the mountain's peak, the fire comes: lightning jagged and electric at the summit, flickering above all that green life.

The two images exist together on the canvas, as the Midrash says they existed together on the mountain.

הַר סִינַי. The mountain that bloomed.
