The Mountain in Bloom

Sources: Exodus 19:18–19; Shir HaShirim Rabbah 1:12; Pirkei DeRabbi Eliezer ch. 41; Shulchan Aruch Orach Chaim 494:3.

Exodus 19:18–19 gives us the canonical theophany: the mountain wrapped in smoke, the Lord descending in fire, the whole mountain trembling greatly. The shofar blast grew louder and louder without ceasing. Thunder, lightning, thick cloud pressing down. This is the Sinai of collective memory — overwhelming, terrifying, magnificent.

But there is another Sinai preserved in Midrash that most readers never encounter.

Midrash Tehillim on Psalm 68:17 preserves a remarkable tradition: the mountains competed to host the revelation. Tabor came from the west. Carmel arrived from the coast. The Holy One, blessed be He, rejected them all. Sinai was chosen — Pirkei DeRabbi Eliezer (ch. 41) explains why: precisely because Sinai did not trumpet its own greatness. Its humility made it worthy.

And then there is Shir HaShirim Rabbah 1:12. At the very moment of revelation — not before, not after, but at the moment the covenant was given — Mount Sinai burst into flower. The fire came down not onto bare desert rock but onto a living, flowering field.

This juxtaposition is deliberate. Torah is both terrible and life-giving. The moment that contains fire and unendurable sound also contains wild plants reaching toward the sky. To receive Torah is to be changed the way soil is changed by rain.

The Shulchan Aruch (Orach Chaim 494:3, Rama) codifies the custom of decorating homes and synagogues with greenery on Shavuot. The midrashic grounding is clear: the decoration enacts the mountain's flowering. We bring the bloom indoors so we do not forget that Torah came to a blooming mountain, not a barren one.

This animation renders both truths simultaneously. L-system branching rules — the same recursive logic governing plant morphogenesis — unfold from a single axiom into full botanical form, rising incrementally from the dark mountain slopes. Then, as the blooms crown the peak, the fire comes: lightning jagged and electric above all that green life.

הַר סִינַי. The mountain that bloomed.
