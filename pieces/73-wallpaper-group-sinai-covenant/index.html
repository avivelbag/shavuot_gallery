The Pattern That Fills the Earth

"All that God has spoken, we will do and we will hear." — Exodus 24:7

In Exodus 24:3–7, Moses descends from Sinai and recounts all of God's words to the assembled people. "And all the people answered with one voice: all that God has spoken, we will do — naaseh." Moses writes down the words, builds an altar at the foot of the mountain, and erects twelve stone pillars, one for each of the twelve tribes. He reads aloud from the Book of the Covenant. The people answer a second time — and this second answer is the one the Talmud cannot stop discussing: "naaseh v'nishma — we will do, and we will hear."

The order is the miracle. In Tractate Shabbat (88a), the Rabbis marvel: how did Israel dare to promise observance before understanding? To commit without first asking what they were committing to? They see in this reversal a moment of extraordinary fidelity. When the words were spoken, six hundred thousand ministering angels descended and placed two crowns upon each Israelite — one for naaseh, one for nishma. A crown of action and a crown of understanding, inseparably bound, in exactly that order.

The tiling before you belongs to wallpaper group p6m — the most symmetric of the seventeen crystallographic groups that classify all possible repeating planar patterns. Evgraf Fedorov proved in 1891 that exactly seventeen such groups exist, no more. George Pólya confirmed the complete enumeration in 1924. This is a finite, closed universe: every repeating symmetry of the infinite plane falls into one of these seventeen families. The classification is total. All possibilities are covered, without remainder.

P6m is the crown of this classification. It possesses the maximum number of symmetry operations of any wallpaper group: six rotational axes and six lines of reflection, generating twelve distinct transformations in total. Twelve, as in the twelve pillars Moses raised at the foot of Sinai. Each fundamental domain — a 30-60-90 triangle — is stamped twelve times to fill one hexagonal tile; that tile repeats to cover the plane. In this piece, each tiny triangle holds a stylized wheat ear: the grain of the harvest season, the symbol of Shavuot.

Each triangular domain is a naaseh: a single act of commitment, placed where the pattern demands. Its mirror image, generated by the reflection axis, is the nishma: the same act, heard from the other side. Together, action and understanding tile the plane completely. As the covenant at Sinai was meant to cover all of Israel — all generations, all possibilities — the p6m tiling covers all of the plane. Leviticus 26:3 and Deuteronomy 28:1 frame this same covenant in terms of doing and hearing: if you walk in my statutes and keep my commandments and do them — naaseh — you will hear abundance in the land. The pattern that fills the earth.

Sources: Exodus 24:3–7; Babylonian Talmud, Shabbat 88a; Leviticus 26:3; Deuteronomy 28:1; Fedorov, E. S. (1891); Pólya, G. (1924).
