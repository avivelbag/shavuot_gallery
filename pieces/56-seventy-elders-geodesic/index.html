"And Moses went up, and Aaron, Nadab and Abihu, and seventy of the elders of Israel. And upon the nobles of the children of Israel He laid not His hand; and they beheld God, and they ate and drank."

Exodus 24:9–11

The Most Startling Sentence in the Torah

This passage does not announce itself as remarkable. It is tucked between the blood-rite of verse 8 (Moses dashing sacrificial blood on the people: "Behold the blood of the covenant") and Moses's subsequent forty-day ascent into the cloud. Yet Exodus 24:11 contains what may be the most theologically compressed sentence in the entire Hebrew Bible: vayechezu et haElohim vayochlu vayishtu — "and they beheld God and they ate and drank."

Seventy-four people ascended Sinai. They saw the God of Israel. Under the divine feet was something like a pavement of lapis lazuli, as clear as the sky itself — an aesthetic description of surpassing strangeness, as though the narrator wants us to understand that what they encountered was not a voice or a presence but a place, a specific visual scene. And then they sat down to a meal. The eating has troubled every reader since. It looks either like scandalous casualness in the face of the absolute, or like the most natural thing in the world.

Three Readings

Rashi (Rabbi Shlomo Yitzchaki, 11th century, France) reads the eating as incrimination. In his comment on verse 11, he explains that "He laid not His hand upon the nobles" implies they should have been struck down — that their gaze upon the divine was impudent and careless. Rashi cites the later fate of Nadab and Abihu (Leviticus 10), consumed by fire for bringing "strange fire" before God, as delayed punishment for their disrespect on Sinai. They ate and drank when they should have prostrated themselves in fear. God's restraint was a mercy extended only because the joy of Sinai's covenant was too great to shatter with immediate punishment.

Nachmanides (Rabbi Moshe ben Nachman, 13th century, Catalonia) offers a different escape: the entire encounter was prophetic vision, not physical proximity. Following his reading of vayiru and vayechezu as terms of prophetic apprehension rather than literal sight, he argues that the elders entered a state of elevated consciousness — a machazeh, a prophetic trance — in which they apprehended something about the divine nature. The sapphire pavement is clearly symbolic, a vision of cosmic clarity. When the vision ended, they ate as ordinary bodies recovering from prophetic suspension. Nobody literally saw what cannot be seen.

The plain-text reading refuses both escapes. The syntax is relentlessly physical: they went up, they saw, the pavement is described with the eye of someone who noticed its color and texture. Modern biblical scholarship, following Umberto Cassuto and others who read Exodus in the context of ancient Near Eastern literature, notes the precise parallel to suzerain-vassal treaty ratification ceremonies documented throughout the ancient Levant. A covenant was not concluded until it was sealed at table with the suzerain. After the blood rite, after the recitation of terms, after the people's acceptance, the covenant must be ratified by a shared meal. The elders eat because the form requires it. The meal is the signature.

The Communal Meal as Sacred Form

If the plain-text reading is correct, it transforms everything. The eating is not a scandal or an anticlimax; it is the culminating liturgical act. The 70 elders — who represent the full community of Israel, seventy being the traditional number of the nations and of Israel's foundational families — sit before God at the top of the mountain and share a meal that closes the covenant. The physical act of eating together is itself holy, itself revelatory, itself part of what happened at Sinai.

This reading carries an implication for Shavuot. The institution of Tikkun Leil Shavuot — the all-night vigil of Torah study — reenacts in its texture the ancient communal gathering: a community assembled through the night, sustained by learning and by food, keeping faith with the covenant by sitting together under one canopy. The dairy meal of Shavuot is not incidental. The dome of students bent over the Talmud is the dome of elders ascending Sinai. The meal has never ended.
