# They Saw God and Ate — Geodesic Crown of the 70 Elders

## The Source

> וַיַּעַל מֹשֶׁה וְאַהֲרֹן נָדָב וַאֲבִיהוּא וְשִׁבְעִים מִזִּקְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל׃
> וַיִּרְאוּ אֵת אֱלֹהֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל וְתַחַת רַגְלָיו כְּמַעֲשֵׂה לִבְנַת הַסַּפִּיר
> וּכְעֶצֶם הַשָּׁמַיִם לָטֹהַר׃ וְאֶל אֲצִילֵי בְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל לֹא שָׁלַח יָדוֹ
> וַיֶּחֱזוּ אֶת הָאֱלֹהִים וַיֹּאכְלוּ וַיִּשְׁתּוּ׃
>
> *Exodus 24:9–11*

"And Moses went up, and Aaron, Nadab and Abihu, and seventy of the elders of Israel. And they saw the God of Israel; and under His feet was like a paved work of sapphire stone, and like the very heaven in its clearness. And upon the nobles of the children of Israel He laid not His hand; and they beheld God, and they ate and drank."

## The Most Startling Sentence in the Torah

This passage does not announce itself as remarkable. It is tucked between the blood-rite of verse 8 (Moses dashing sacrificial blood on the people: "Behold the blood of the covenant") and Moses's subsequent forty-day ascent into the cloud. Yet Exodus 24:11 contains what may be the most theologically compressed sentence in the entire Hebrew Bible: *vayechezu et haElohim vayochlu vayishtu* — "and they beheld God and they ate and drank."

Seventy-four people ascended Sinai. They saw the God of Israel. Under the divine feet was something like a pavement of lapis lazuli, as clear as the sky itself — an aesthetic description of surpassing strangeness, as though the narrator wants us to understand that what they encountered was not a voice or a presence but a *place*, a specific visual scene. And then they sat down to a meal.

The eating has troubled every reader since. It looks either like scandalous casualness in the face of the absolute, or like the most natural thing in the world. The commentators cannot agree which.

## Three Readings

**Rashi** (Rabbi Shlomo Yitzchaki, 11th century, France) reads the eating as incrimination. In his comment on verse 11, he explains that the phrase "He laid not His hand upon the nobles" implies that they *should* have been struck down — that their gaze upon the divine was impudent and careless. Rashi cites the later fate of Nadab and Abihu (Leviticus 10), consumed by fire for bringing "strange fire" before God, as the delayed punishment for their disrespect on Sinai. They ate and drank, Rashi implies, when they should have prostrated themselves in fear. The meal is evidence of hubris. God's restraint was a mercy extended only because the joy of Sinai's covenant was too great to shatter with immediate punishment.

**Nachmanides** (Rabbi Moshe ben Nachman, 13th century, Catalonia) offers a different rescue: the entire encounter was prophetic vision, not physical proximity. Following his reading of *vayiru* and *vayechezu* as terms of prophetic apprehension rather than literal sight, he argues that the elders entered a state of elevated consciousness — a *machazeh*, prophetic trance — in which they apprehended something about the divine nature. The sapphire pavement is clearly symbolic, a vision of cosmic clarity. When the vision ended, they ate and drank as ordinary bodies recovering from prophetic suspension. Nobody literally saw what cannot be seen; nobody literally dined in the divine court. The text's physical language is the necessary idiom of prophetic literature.

**The plain-text reading** will not accept either escape. The syntax is relentlessly physical: they *went up* (vayaal), they *saw* (vayiru), the pavement is described with the eye of someone who noticed its color and texture, and the meal follows without any transition into a dreamlike register. Modern biblical scholarship, following Umberto Cassuto and others who read Exodus in the context of ancient Near Eastern literature, has noted the precise parallel to suzerain-vassal treaty meals documented throughout the ancient Levant. A covenant was not concluded until it was ratified at table. After the blood rite, after the recitation of terms, after the people's threefold acceptance (*naaseh*, *nishma*, and the blood sealed it), the covenant must be sealed at a shared meal with the suzerain. The elders eat because that is what the form requires. The meal *is* the signature.

## The Communal Meal as Sacred Form

If the plain-text reading is correct, it transforms everything. The eating is not a scandal or an anticlimax; it is the culminating liturgical act. The 70 elders — who represent the full community of Israel, seventy being the traditional number of the nations and of Israel's foundational families — sit *before* God at the top of the mountain and share a meal that closes the covenant. The physical act of eating together is itself holy, itself revelatory, itself part of what happened at Sinai.

This reading carries an implication for Shavuot that is rarely made explicit. The rabbinic institution of *Tikkun Leil Shavuot* — the all-night vigil of Torah study on the eve of the holiday — grew partly from a tradition that Israel slept through the morning at Sinai and had to be roused for revelation. But the vigil is also, in its texture, a reenactment of that ancient communal gathering: a community assembled through the night, sustained by learning and by food, keeping faith with the covenant by sitting together under one roof. The dairy meal of Shavuot, the cheesecake and blintzes, is not incidental. It echoes the meal on the mountain. The dome of students bent over the Talmud *is* the dome of elders ascending Sinai. The meal has never ended.

## This Artwork

The animation renders the mountain as a geodesic icosphere — eighty triangular faces formed by one subdivision of the icosahedron, all vertices projected outward to a unit sphere, slowly rotating as the night sky rotates. Seventy amber nodes pulse at face-centers like cooking fires: the 70 elders, each visible as a warm glowing disc, arranged across the upper hemisphere of the dome. Ten brighter white nodes at the apex mark Moses, Aaron, Nadab, and Abihu — those named in the text, those who went first. The wireframe edges grow dimmer toward the far side of the sphere and brighter toward the viewer; the depth suggests the mountain receding into darkness, the nearer faces catching the light of the communal fire.

The Hebrew phrase in the corner — *וַיֶּחֱזוּ אֶת הָאֱלֹהִים* — is the exact pivot of the passage, the moment before the meal: "and they beheld God." The dome turns. The fires hold. The meal continues.
