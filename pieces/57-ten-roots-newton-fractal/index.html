Ten Roots

Newton's Fractal of the Commandments — Exodus 20:1–14

The Ten Commandments — Aseret HaDibrot — were not given as a numbered list. The text of Exodus 20:1–14 records a speech, and the rabbis have counted its clauses differently for two thousand years. Rambam (Maimonides), following the Talmudic reading in Makkot 24a, enumerates "I am the LORD your God" as a positive commandment — the first of the 248 positive mitzvot he catalogs in the Sefer HaMitzvot. The Sefer HaChinuch, by contrast, follows the enumeration in the Mekhilta and treats "You shall have no other gods" as the true first prohibition. Neither tradition is wrong. They are measuring different things. The Ten Commandments are not ten items in a list. They are a grammar — a generative structure from which 613 distinct obligations unfold.

This is the starting point for the fractal you are looking at.

In mathematics, Newton's method is an algorithm for finding the roots of a polynomial equation. Given a function f(z) and a starting point z₀ in the complex plane, the method takes successive steps: z₁ = z₀ − f(z₀)/f′(z₀). Each step moves z closer to a root of f — a value where f(z) = 0. The polynomial f(z) = z¹⁰ − 1 has exactly ten roots, the tenth roots of unity, equally spaced around the unit circle at 36° from one another. Starting from any point in the complex plane, Newton's method will converge to one of these ten roots. The fractal is the map of that convergence: each pixel is colored by which of the ten roots its starting coordinate eventually reaches, under repeated iteration of the Newton step.

The ten basins of attraction are colored with a palette evocative of fire and carved stone: white flame for the first commandment (אָנֹכִי), warming through wheat gold, amber, and harvest orange into deep rose, then deepening through dark violet and midnight indigo, cooling into deep teal and forest green, returning to olive gold. The near-black tracery between the colored regions — where convergence fails within the iteration limit — is the fractal boundary itself.

The fractal boundary is where ethical complexity lives.

Most points in the complex plane converge quickly and unambiguously. A point near the root of unity at 0° is clearly in the first basin; a point near 180° is clearly in the sixth. But near the boundary between basins, behavior becomes chaotic. Infinitely fine detail accumulates at these lines — fractal structure all the way down, self-similar at every scale, never fully resolving into a clear answer.

The halakha — Jewish law — is a centuries-long project of mapping exactly this kind of boundary. Exodus 20 gives ten structural principles. The Mishnah translates them into tractates. The Talmud examines every edge case, every ambiguous pixel at the basin edge. The Rishonim codify; the Aḥaronim extend. At the center of each basin, the answer to most moral questions is unambiguous: most of life is solid color. A murder is a murder. The hard cases are the boundary points. The termination of a life to end suffering — which basin does that occupy? The borrowing that edges toward exploitation. The Sabbath labor required to save a life. The tradition does not pretend these are simple. It maps them, generation by generation, as you are seeing mathematics map its own boundaries here: with precision that never exhausts the detail, and with patient iteration that always approaches, without quite reaching, the line.

The Sinai theophany inaugurated not a fixed set of answers but a structure of inquiry. The ten attractors are not destinations but orientations: toward the divine, away from idolatry, toward Sabbath rest, toward parents, away from violence, away from adultery, away from theft, toward truth, away from covetousness. Every soul begins somewhere in the complex plane of human circumstance. Newton's method — or the tradition — carries it, step by step, toward the nearest attractor. Most of the journey is smooth. The difficulty, and the beauty, lies at the cracks.

The slow zoom drifting toward the origin over sixty seconds reveals that finer and finer fractal detail accumulates near the center — near the point where all ten basins meet. That meeting point is Sinai itself: the source from which all ten orientations radiate, the place from which the tradition has been receding, and returning to, ever since.
