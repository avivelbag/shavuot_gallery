We Will Do and We Will Hear

Exodus 24:7 · Gosper curve / L-system / animated path tracing

נַעֲשֶׂה וְנִשְׁמָע
"We will do and we will hear" — Exodus 24:7

At the foot of Mount Sinai, Moses read aloud the Book of the Covenant. When he finished, the people answered with a single voice: naaseh v'nishma — נַעֲשֶׂה וְנִשְׁמָע — "we will do and we will hear." The declaration is strikingly out of order. Normally one hears first — gathers information, weighs consequences, deliberates — and only then decides to act. Israel reversed this sequence entirely: we commit first; we understand later.

The Talmud (Shabbat 88a) names this reversal as a moment of extraordinary spiritual courage. The Sages say that six hundred thousand ministering angels descended and placed two crowns (katarim) on the head of each Israelite — one for naaseh and one for nishma. The angels, who do not face this choice because they perceive clearly before acting, envied Israel for making a commitment they themselves could never make. The double crown is not merely a reward; it marks the structure of the act — two distinct moments, doing and hearing, each deserving its own recognition.

The Crown of Nishma

But the Talmud's account points toward something deeper than simple obedience preceding comprehension. The word nishma shares its root with shema — the paradigmatic act of Jewish listening that encompasses comprehension, response, and inner transformation. Nishma is not passive hearing; it carries the connotation of ongoing interpretation and study. Lishmoa is to hear in a way that changes you. When Israel said v'nishma, they were not saying "we will hear the rationale afterward." They were saying: "we will study this forever — the practice itself will be our understanding."

This inverts the usual model of learning. To know first, then do, is the way of philosophical inquiry. To do first, and find understanding inside the doing, is the way of Torah. The Rabbis call the crown of nishma the higher one: sustained study that never finishes, always discovering the covenant's deeper dimensions from within the practice itself. Each commandment is an entry into the text; every performance is also an interpretation.

The Flowsnake

The Gosper curve — known as the flowsnake — is a single unbroken path that covers every cell of its hexagonal island without repetition or gap. You cannot see its shape from inside the path: at any given moment you are surrounded by turns you cannot anticipate, curves you have just left, and segments yet to come that are indistinguishable from the space around you. The whole shape is only visible in retrospect, from outside, when the path has been completed and the island stands fully traced.

This is the precise structure of naaseh v'nishma. Israel committed to the whole before seeing the whole. The path — the practice of Torah — is itself the act of understanding. To follow it is to know it. The curve does not lead somewhere else; it covers its own ground completely, and in covering it, reveals itself.
