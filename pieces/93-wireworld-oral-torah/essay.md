# Moses Received and Transmitted

## Pirkei Avot 1:1

מֹשֶׁה קִבֵּל תּוֹרָה מִסִּינַי וּמְסָרָהּ לִיהוֹשֻׁעַ, וִיהוֹשֻׁעַ לִזְקֵנִים, וּזְקֵנִים לִנְבִיאִים, וּנְבִיאִים מְסָרוּהָ לְאַנְשֵׁי כְנֶסֶת הַגְּדוֹלָה.

*Moshe kibel Torah miSinai u'mesarah l'Yehoshua; v'Yehoshua la'Zekenim; u'Zekenim la'Nevi'im; v'Nevi'im mesaruha l'Anshei Knesset HaGedolah.*

"Moses received the Torah from Sinai and transmitted it to Joshua; Joshua to the Elders; the Elders to the Prophets; and the Prophets transmitted it to the Men of the Great Assembly."

This is the opening verse of Pirkei Avot — Ethics of the Fathers — a tractate unlike any other in the Mishnah. Where the rest of rabbinic literature addresses legal questions, Pirkei Avot collects the ethical maxims and wisdom teachings of every major rabbinic figure from the Second Temple period onward. It is read on Shabbat afternoons between Pesach and Shavuot, one chapter each Shabbat during the weeks of the Omer count. This makes it the literary companion of the counting: as the seven weeks of the Omer measure the distance from Egypt to Sinai, Pirkei Avot traces the chain of transmission that carries what was received at Sinai into every subsequent generation.

## Receive and Transmit

The Hebrew is precise in a way that translations often flatten. The text does not say Moses *taught* (limmad) Torah. It says he *received* (kibel) it and *transmitted* (mesarah) it. These are relay verbs. Moses is not the originator — he is a link. Sinai is the source. Moses received something intact and passed it on intact. The chain is the point.

Each generation is named by a collective, not an individual: Joshua, the Elders, the Prophets, the Men of the Great Assembly. The tradition belongs to no single mind. It is held by a community and handed forward as a living object, not a document. A document can be copied; this tradition requires a person to carry it.

## The Fragility of the Chain

Wireworld — a cellular automaton devised by Brian Silverman in 1987 — enacts this chain with uncanny literalness. Each cell exists in one of four states: Empty, Conductor (amber wire), Electron Head (white-gold leading edge), or Electron Tail (ember-red trailing edge). A single transition rule governs everything: a Conductor cell becomes an Electron Head if and only if exactly one or two of its neighboring cells are currently Electron Heads. Not zero. Not three or more. Exactly one or two.

This condition is the non-obvious heart of the piece. Too few neighboring heads — the signal dies. Too many — the conductor stays dark, overwhelmed, the signal absorbed. Only the precise range of one or two triggers propagation. The tradition's transmission conditions are as exact as a logic gate: the right number of living teachers, handing on to the right number of students. Fewer, and the signal fades. More, and the competing authorities cancel each other.

The Talmud records near-extinctions of the chain: the Roman execution of the Ten Martyrs, the destruction of the academies, the repeated exile of the Jewish people across continents. At each rupture the chain nearly broke. That it did not is because in each generation the exact transmission conditions were met — not by miracle alone, but by people who found exactly one or two teachers and transmitted to exactly one or two students.

## The Branching Tree

What you see is a single pulse injected at Sinai — the top of the wire tree. It propagates down the trunk and at each generation splits at a Y-junction, duplicating into both child branches. One tradition at Sinai becomes two at Moses, four at the Elders, eight at the Prophets, expanding into the 120 members of the Great Assembly. From there the Tannaim, the Amoraim, the Geonim, and onward — the branching continues beyond the canvas, beyond history, into every beit midrash where the electron still flows.

The viewer watches a single pulse from Sinai multiply through the generations indefinitely. This is the Oral Torah as circuit: not stored, but propagating; not contained, but transmitted; not taught once but relayed without end.
