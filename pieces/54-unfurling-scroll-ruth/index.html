The Unfurling Scroll

Megillat Ruth · Shavuot · Ruth 1:16

עַמֵּךְ עַמִּי וֵאלֹהַיִךְ אֱלֹהָי

"Your people shall be my people, and your God my God."

Ruth 1:16 — The Declaration

These words are spoken by Ruth the Moabite to her mother-in-law Naomi on the road between Moab and Bethlehem. Naomi has urged her daughters-in-law twice to return to their own families after the death of their husbands. Orpah weeps, kisses Naomi, and goes back. Ruth cleaves. Ruth 1:16–17 is the whole of the declaration, the most complete statement of voluntary covenantal belonging in the Hebrew Bible: wherever you go, I will go; wherever you die, I will die; there will I be buried.

The verse is not a prayer. It is not a ritual formula. It is a declaration made on a dusty road to a grief-stricken woman who has nothing to offer and who is explicitly telling Ruth to leave. The full-heartedness of the choice is its content.

The Custom of Reading Megillat Ruth on Shavuot

The Book of Ruth is read publicly on Shavuot, the festival that commemorates the giving of the Torah at Sinai. The Talmud Yerushalmi (Megillah 3:5) and later authorities establish this practice. Two reasons are given most often. The first is agricultural: Ruth's story unfolds during the barley and wheat harvest, and Shavuot is Chag HaKatzir, the festival of the grain harvest. The second reason is theological: Ruth's conversion mirrors the giving of the Torah. Just as Israel stood at Sinai and voluntarily accepted the Torah, Ruth voluntarily accepts the God and people of Israel.

Her declaration is a private Sinai. She accepts not just a people but a covenant, not just a land but its laws — including the very laws that will shortly feed her in Boaz's field.

Soloveitchik and the Covenant of Acceptance

Rabbi Joseph B. Soloveitchik draws a sustained parallel between Ruth's declaration and the covenant Israel made at Sinai. Both, he argues, are defined not by circumstance or birth but by a full-hearted, unconditional acceptance. Ruth is told explicitly: there is nothing here for you. She cleaves anyway.

The Rav notes that Ruth's declaration contains no conditions. She does not say: I will go with you unless the journey is too hard. Death is the only limit she names — and she names it to exclude it. This is the structure of Sinai: naaseh v'nishma, first we will do, then we will understand. The commitment precedes the comprehension.

The Scroll as Form

The megillah — scroll — is the physical form in which Ruth is read on Shavuot. A sofer (scribe) writes the text by hand on parchment, and the scroll is unrolled and read aloud, column by column. The physical act of unrolling is part of the experience: the text is revealed progressively, as the reader's hands move apart, as the words come into view stroke by stroke.

This animation makes the form visible as form. The parchment unfurls between two wooden rollers. The Hebrew letters of Ruth 1:16 appear not as rendered font glyphs but as individual bezier strokes, each drawn at its own pace, as if a sofer's pen were moving across the parchment in real time. The letters are right-to-left, as Hebrew is read. The scroll does not close. It breathes — a slow glow pulse in the gold of the ink's shadow, like a living text. Because the text of Ruth, once spoken, remains.
