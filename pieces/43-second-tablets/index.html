The Second Tablets — A Celtic Knot of Renewal

כִּי עַל-פִּי הַדְּבָרִים הָאֵלֶּה כָּרַתִּי אִתְּךָ בְּרִית
"For upon the terms of these words I have made a covenant with you."
— Exodus 34:27

Shavuot and the First Tablets

Every year on Shavuot we celebrate the giving of the Torah at Sinai — the moment when Moshe ascended the mountain and received the first tablets. The date is the sixth of Sivan. The covenant was given in innocence, in the first flush of the relationship between Israel and the Holy One.

Forty days later, on the seventeenth of Tammuz, Moshe descended and found Israel dancing around a golden calf. He shattered the tablets at the foot of the mountain.

The Timeline of Repair

Here is what the sources record: Moshe ascended again on the first of Elul, spent another forty days on the mountain, and came back down on the tenth of Tishrei — Yom Kippur — carrying the second tablets. Ta'anit 26b and Pirkei DeRabbi Eliezer 46 are clear: the second tablets arrived on Yom Kippur, the day of atonement and repair. Shavuot is the festival of the first tablets, the covenant given in innocence. Yom Kippur is the festival of the second tablets, the covenant given after rupture.

The Covenant of Repair

The Sages did not regard the second tablets as inferior. The first tablets were given before an undivided people, in pristine innocence. The second were given after betrayal, after grief, after an incomplete people made their way back. The covenant of the second tablets was sealed not with pristine innocence but with the harder, more earned knowledge that repair is possible.

Eruvin 54a: "The tablets were the work of God, and the writing was the writing of God" — this covers both sets, the first and the second alike. The Ark held both: the intact second tablets and the fragments of the first. Broken law is still law, and the covenant does not erase its own history.

The Celtic Knot

Celtic knotwork is built from a single strand that interweaves with itself, over and under, without beginning and without end. The strand is continuous. It cannot be cut at any crossing, because at every crossing it passes both over and under.

This is the image for the covenant of the second tablets. The strand was not severed on the seventeenth of Tammuz. The golden calf created a crossing — a moment of descent, of crisis. But the strand kept going. Moshe went back up. The covenant was rewoven, the over and under switching roles, the same continuous thread now running through the knot in a different configuration.

Shavuot gives us the opportunity to re-enter the weave — to find our place in the continuous strand, to recognize that wherever we are in it, the knot holds.
