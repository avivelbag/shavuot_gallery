# לוחות שניות — The Second Tablets: A Covenant of Repair

## Shavuot and the First Tablets

Every year on Shavuot we celebrate the giving of the Torah at Sinai — the moment when Moshe ascended the mountain and received the first tablets. The date is the sixth of Sivan. The tablets were the work of God; the writing was the writing of God. The covenant was given in innocence, in the first flush of the relationship between Israel and the Holy One.

Forty days later, on the seventeenth of Tammuz, Moshe descended and found Israel dancing around a golden calf. He shattered the tablets at the foot of the mountain.

## The Timeline of Repair

Here is what the sources record: Moshe ascended again on the first of Elul, spent another forty days on the mountain, and came back down on the tenth of Tishrei — Yom Kippur — carrying the second tablets. This is the account in Ta'anit 26b and in Pirkei DeRabbi Eliezer 46: the second tablets arrived on Yom Kippur, the day of atonement and repair.

Shavuot is the festival of the first tablets, the covenant given in innocence. Yom Kippur is the festival of the second tablets, the covenant given after rupture.

## Why the Second Tablets Are the Deeper Revelation

The Sages did not regard the second tablets as inferior. In some sense they saw them as greater. The first tablets were given in the presence of the whole world, with thunder and lightning, before an undivided people. The second tablets were given after Israel had broken trust — after betrayal, after grief, after an incomplete people made their way back. The covenant of the second tablets was sealed not with pristine innocence but with the harder, more earned knowledge that repair is possible.

Exodus 34 records the text of the second tablets and the renewed covenant in the words: *כִּי עַל-פִּי הַדְּבָרִים הָאֵלֶּה כָּרַתִּי אִתְּךָ בְּרִית* — "For upon the terms of these words I have made a covenant with you" (Exodus 34:27). The covenant is renewed. It is not replaced. It carries the memory of what broke it.

## What Eruvin 54a Teaches

The Talmud in Eruvin 54a observes: "The tablets were the work of God, and the writing was the writing of God" — and this phrase covers both sets of tablets, the first and the second alike. Both were carved by divine hands. Both carried the same words. The shattering of the first did not diminish the sanctity of the second; if anything, the second tablets carry a weight the first did not. They are the testimony that the relationship survived its worst moment.

The Ark held both: the intact second tablets and the fragments of the first. The broken pieces were not discarded. They were preserved alongside the whole, because broken law is still law, and the covenant does not erase its own history.

## The Celtic Knot as Covenant Metaphor

Celtic knotwork is built from a single strand — or a small number of strands — that interweave with themselves, over and under, without beginning and without end. The strand is continuous. It cannot be cut at any crossing, because at every crossing it passes through, it passes both over and under: it is always and already woven back into itself.

This is the image for the covenant of the second tablets. The strand was not severed on the seventeenth of Tammuz. The golden calf created a crossing — a moment of under, of descent, of crisis. But the strand kept going. Moshe went back up the mountain. The covenant was rewoven, the over and under switching roles, the same continuous thread now running through the knot in a different configuration.

The covenant between Israel and God has no beginning that precedes the strand and no end that will outlast it. What Shavuot gives us each year is the opportunity to re-enter the weave — to find our place in the continuous strand, to recognize that wherever we are in it, the knot holds.

## This Piece

The artwork renders a grid-based Celtic knotwork in the shape of the two tablets (luchot): two portrait-orientation rectangles with rounded arched tops, side by side. Within each tablet, a five-column by nine-row grid of crossings generates the interlace; at each crossing, the over/under alternates by position parity, creating the woven effect.

The strand is rendered as a ribbon: a thick base stroke in deep lapis lazuli (#1A3A6B), a gold highlight (#C8A000) along the upper edge, and a near-black shadow (#0A0A18) along the lower edge — the palette of illuminated manuscripts, of Torah text written in ink on parchment.

The background is warm off-white parchment (#F2E8D0). The strand breathes: its width pulses very slightly on a five-second sinusoidal cycle, ±4%, as if the cord is alive.

Below the two tablets, the Hebrew text reads: *כָּרַתִּי אִתְּךָ בְּרִית* — "I have made a covenant with you" (Exodus 34:27). Right-to-left. The words of the second giving.

The piece connects to Shavuot not because the second tablets were given on Shavuot — they were not — but because every Shavuot we re-enter a covenant that has already survived its own shattering. We celebrate not just the first giving but the capacity to be given again.
