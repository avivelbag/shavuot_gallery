All Night They Learned: The Beit Midrash at Dawn

The Zohar's Prescription

The practice of staying awake all night on Shavuot — known as Tikkun Leil Shavuot, the "repair of Shavuot night" — traces its earliest textual source to the Zohar. In Parashat Emor (III:98a), the mystical text declares: "The pious ones of old did not sleep that night but engaged in Torah, saying: let us acquire holy inheritance for ourselves and our sons in both worlds." This is the beit midrash before dawn — a room where candles burn through the night, and every learner's voice adds its domain of light to the whole.

The Tzfat Circle

The custom was crystallized in the sixteenth century by the kabbalists of Tzfat. R. Joseph Karo, R. Shlomo Alkabetz, and their circle composed the Tikkun anthology — a structured program of passages from Torah, Prophets, Writings, and Mishnah, arranged to carry the learner from darkness to dawn. The Magen Avraham (Orach Chaim 494:1) later codified what Tzfat had practiced: "It is the custom to stay awake all night on Shavuot engaged in Torah." From Tzfat the practice spread across the Jewish world; today it is universal.

The Repair of a Drowsy Moment

Here is the non-obvious insight: the word tikkun means "repair." What exactly is being repaired? The Midrash in Shir HaShirim Rabbah (1:12) records a startling detail — Israel fell asleep the night before Matan Torah, and Moses had to wake them when the Divine Presence descended on Sinai. The nation slept through the opening moment of the greatest revelation in history. Every generation's Tikkun Leil Shavuot is a retroactive correction of that original failure. By staying awake — each learner in their own domain of candle-light, each voice adding its illumination — we repair the drowsiness at Sinai. The annual vigil is not merely piety; it is the annual undoing of a collective lapse.

The Algorithm as Vigil

The Jump Flooding Algorithm computes Voronoi diagrams in O(log N) passes over the canvas: in each pass, every pixel samples its eight neighbors at an exponentially shrinking step distance and adopts the nearest seed it finds. After log₂(max(W,H)) passes the full Voronoi diagram is complete. The canvas begins in near-total darkness. One by one, over eight seconds representing the night's eight hours, each of the forty-nine seed-learners activates and its Voronoi cell blooms outward — a wave of light spreading from the individual to fill every corner of their domain. Seeds on the left entered study at the depth of night: their cells glow midnight blue and violet. Seeds toward the right arrived as dawn approached: their cells burn deep orange and warm gold. At full illumination, no pixel of the canvas remains in darkness. That, precisely, is the point of the tikkun.
