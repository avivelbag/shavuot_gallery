The Greening of the House — Decorating for Shavuot

Source: The custom is codified by the Rama (Rabbi Moshe Isserles) in Shulchan Aruch, Orach Chaim 494:3. The Mishnah Berurah (ad loc., §10–11) explains the theological reason.

Walk into any synagogue on the eve of Shavuot and you will find the bimah wreathed in palm fronds, roses tucked between the pews, ferns cascading from the windowsills. The smell of cut greenery mingles with the scent of candle wax. Children carry branches. This is the custom of decorating with greenery — and it is ancient.

The Rama records the practice as normative law, not merely custom: spread grass and flowers in the home and synagogue on Shavuot. The Mishnah Berurah preserves the reason in two registers. The first is pragmatic and joyful: greenery and flowers honor the holiday. The second is theological and surprising.

The folk explanation, cited in the name of the Geonim, runs as follows. When the Torah was given at Sinai, the mountain was green. Exodus 34:3 forbids the flocks from grazing near Sinai — not because the mountain was barren, but because it was lush. God prepared a blooming mountain for the revelation. We bring the bloom indoors so we do not forget.

There is a subtle inversion at work here. The verse that most readers read as prohibition becomes, in this reading, evidence of extraordinary fertility. The mountain God chose for the covenant was not the austere desert peak of popular imagination but something vivid and growing. Torah was given in a garden.

This is theologically apt for Shavuot in a way that goes beyond decoration. Shavuot marks the giving of Torah, but the rabbis understand Torah as the blueprint of creation, pre-existing the world, the medium through which abundance becomes possible. Greenery is not mere ornament: it is the argument. Look at what grows.

The painting responds to this idea through the mechanics of the image itself. Each plant begins as a single point — a seed, an axiom — and unfolds through recursive branching rules into a full form. The L-system grammar that drives the growth is an algorithm of becoming: apply the rule, apply the rule again, apply it again. What starts as F expands into the whole elaborate branching structure.

The two plant types — the tree-form with its wide branching crown, and the vine with its coiling tendrils and clustered nodes — represent the two registers of Sinai's greenery: the anchored trees that give shade and the spreading vines that overgrow whatever supports them.

Five-petal flowers bloom at the branch tips in white and soft rose. Their color is the pale pink of early morning — the moment when the people of Israel gathered at the foot of the mountain and waited for what would come down.
