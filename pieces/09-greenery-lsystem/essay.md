# The Greening of the House — Decorating for Shavuot

*Source: The custom is codified by the Rama (Rabbi Moshe Isserles) in Shulchan Aruch, Orach Chaim 494:3: "It is customary to spread grasses and flowers in the synagogue and in the home on Shavuot, in honor of the holiday." The Mishnah Berurah (ad loc., §10–11) elaborates and explains the reason behind the practice.*

---

Walk into any synagogue on the eve of Shavuot and you will find the bimah wreathed in palm fronds, roses tucked between the pews, ferns cascading from the windowsills. The smell of cut greenery mingles with the scent of candle wax. Children carry branches. The ark is garlanded. This is the custom of *bakashat ha-yarok* — seeking the green — and it is ancient.

The Rama records the practice as normative law, not merely custom: spread grass and flowers in the home and synagogue on Shavuot. The Mishnah Berurah preserves the reason in two registers. The first is pragmatic and joyful: greenery and flowers honor the holiday, heighten the mood of celebration. The second is theological and surprising.

The folk explanation, cited in the name of the Geonim and sharpened by later authorities, runs as follows. When the Torah was given at Sinai, the mountain was green. Exodus 34:3 forbids the flocks from grazing near Sinai — not because the mountain was barren, but because it was *lush*. The prohibition on the animals was a protective fence around abundance: the flocks would have stripped it bare. God prepared a blooming mountain for the revelation. We bring the bloom indoors so we do not forget.

There is a subtle inversion at work here. The verse that most readers read as prohibition — "let no flock or herd graze in front of that mountain" — becomes, in this reading, evidence of extraordinary fertility. The mountain God chose for the covenant was not the austere desert peak of popular imagination but something vivid and growing. Torah was given in a garden.

This is theologically apt for Shavuot in a way that goes beyond decoration. Shavuot marks the giving of Torah, but the rabbis understand Torah as the blueprint of creation, pre-existing the world, the medium through which abundance becomes possible. Greenery is not mere ornament: it is the argument. Look at what grows. Look at what the world can be when tended.
