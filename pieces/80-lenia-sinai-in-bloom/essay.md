# And the Mountain Blossomed

*Sources: Pirkei de-Rabbi Eliezer ch. 41; Shir HaShirim Rabbah 1:2; Shabbat 88b; Shulchan Aruch Orach Chayyim 494.*

---

Pirkei de-Rabbi Eliezer, chapter forty-one, records what most tellings omit: when God descended upon Sinai to give the Torah, the mountain did not merely burn and tremble. It blossomed. The text is precise — at the moment of the giving, the mountain filled with blooms and fragrance, as it is written in the Song of Songs (2:6): "His left hand is under my head." Shir HaShirim Rabbah (1:2) draws the same connection, reading the lover's fragrant garden as the mountain of revelation. Sinai, the most humble of mountains — rejected by Tabor and Carmel as too small — received the Torah and erupted into flower.

This Midrash is the direct source of the Ashkenazi custom of decorating the synagogue with cut flowers and green boughs on Shavuot eve. The Magen Avraham (Orach Chayyim 494) codifies it. The Vilna Gaon later discouraged the practice, fearing it imitated non-Jewish harvest celebrations; centuries of custom disagreed. On Shavuot eve, across a hundred communities, the sanctuary still smells of lilacs and roses — a landscape encoded in scent, a re-enactment of the mountain's flowering.

The non-obvious reading: the blooming is not decoration. A desert mountain erupting into flower at the moment of revelation signals transformation — the sterile made alive, the indifferent made welcoming. The Talmud (Shabbat 88b) records that the mountain was held over the Israelites like a barrel, כְּגִיגִית — a threat. The flowers contradict the threat. They signal not coercion but hospitality: a host that dressed itself in welcome. Sinai prepared for its guest the way a bride prepares for a wedding. The Torah came not to a wasteland but to a garden.

Lenia, the continuous cellular automaton developed by Bert Chan in 2019, enacts this dynamic precisely. Unlike Conway's Game of Life with its binary on/off cells, Lenia evolves real-valued states — a field of values between zero and one — through a smooth, rotationally symmetric ring kernel. With the Orbium parameters (μ=0.15, σ=0.017), the simulation behaves like living soil: seeded with a few circular patches of activity, the grid either collapses to silence or crystallizes into gliders — stable, rotating, flower-like structures that drift across the field, self-sustaining, unrepeating, alive.

Each glider arose from nothing but the initial conditions and the growth function. No designer placed the flowers. The rules, applied uniformly to an indifferent field, produced them. That is precisely what Pirkei de-Rabbi Eliezer describes: a mountain that, for one moment, grew flowers no one planted, because the conditions were right and the occasion demanded it.

The green field in this simulation is the mountain before the Torah came. The golden blooms that crystallize from random seeds are what the mountain became. Watch the simulation long enough and a structure will form, drift, and persist — alive in the way the Midrash says the mountain was alive: spontaneously, without explanation, at the moment it was needed.

הַר סִינַי פָּרַח. The mountain of Sinai bloomed.
