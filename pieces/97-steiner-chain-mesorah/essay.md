# Links That Never Break

*"Moses received the Torah from Sinai and transmitted it to Joshua; Joshua to the Elders; the Elders to the Prophets; the Prophets transmitted it to the Men of the Great Assembly." — Pirkei Avot 1:1*

---

This sentence is read on the Shabbatot before Shavuot — the weeks when the world tilts toward Sinai — and it is, on its face, a simple list of names. But the Sages of the Mishnah chose to open Pirkei Avot with it because it is also a geometric claim: the transmission of Torah is a closed chain. Not a chain that might break, not a chain that happened to survive — a chain that is closed by design, like a mathematical theorem.

Steiner chains are exactly this. A Steiner chain is a finite ring of circles, each tangent to two fixed non-intersecting circles and to both its neighbors. The surprising theorem — Steiner's porism, proved by Jakob Steiner in 1826 — says: if such a chain closes after k steps from one starting circle, it closes after k steps from every starting circle. Closure is not an accident; it is a property of the configuration, independent of where you begin. You cannot start wrong. The chain will always close.

Most things in life require exactly the right conditions to succeed. A domino chain needs each piece set precisely. A relay race is lost the moment a runner stumbles. The Steiner chain breaks this rule: change the starting position and the chain still closes, rotated but intact. This is the mathematical image of an indestructible tradition: the mesorah has survived exile, dispersion, persecution, and the fracturing of every external institution — and it closes. Always. Not because the conditions were always perfect, but because the structure guarantees closure regardless of where in the ring you begin.

The construction exploits a beautiful technique: inversion. By applying a carefully chosen conformal map — a circle inversion — the two complicated non-concentric bounding circles transform into simple concentric rings. In that inverted world, constructing the chain is trivial: place k equal circles, evenly spaced around the annular gap. Then invert back. The circles warp into different sizes and positions, but they remain mutually tangent, still closing the ring. Conformal maps preserve angle; they preserve the kiss of tangency. The inversion is the same move the mesorah makes: the Torah adapts to each generation's language, context, and crisis — yet the structure of transmission, the tangency between teacher and student, is preserved.

In this piece, seven circles orbit endlessly in the annular gap between two golden bounding circles. They are the seven generations of Pirkei Avot 1:1: Moses, Joshua, the Elders, the Prophets, the Men of the Great Assembly, Shimon the Righteous, and Antigonus of Socho. Each circle carries its bearer's Hebrew name. They never break contact. The inner circle — the living core of tradition — pulses gently, a heartbeat that does not stop. As the angle of rotation increases each frame, the chain's position changes but its closure does not. This is Steiner's porism made visible, and Shavuot made mathematical.

*Sources: Pirkei Avot 1:1; Jakob Steiner, Gesammelte Werke (1826); H.S.M. Coxeter, "The Problem of Apollonius," American Mathematical Monthly 75 (1968).*
