# The Highways of the Night

## The Night Before Sinai

The custom of Tikkun Leil Shavuot — staying awake through the entire night of Shavuot in Torah study — originates in the Zohar's commentary on Parashat Emor, where the righteous adorn the Shekhinah throughout the night, weaving a bridal garment from the words they study. As a communal institution, the practice was formalized in sixteenth-century Safed by the kabbalists around Rabbi Joseph Karo and Rabbi Shlomo Alkabetz, who composed a prescribed reading sequence — a *Tikkun* — cycling through selections from every book of Torah, Prophets, and Writings, then through passages of Mishnah and Zohar.

## The Tikkun as Repair

The word *tikkun* carries a dual charge. In kabbalistic usage it means preparation — a readying of vessels to receive divine light. But it also means repair. A midrash in *Shir HaShirim Rabbah* records that the Israelites slept the night before the revelation at Sinai and had to be awakened by Moses. They arrived late to their own theophany. The all-night vigil of Tikkun Leil Shavuot re-enacts that approach to Sinai, this time without the sleeping. The non-obvious insight is that the tikkun is corrective: the Jewish people re-enact the approach to Sinai that their ancestors fumbled, proving with their wakefulness that this time they are ready. The debt is not legal or financial; it is existential — a second chance at the night that mattered most.

## Emergence From Rule-Following

Each learner on Tikkun Leil Shavuot traces their own path through the prescribed texts — one pauses on a legal dispute in the Talmud, another follows a strand of midrash to its end, a third copies the kabbalistic sequence from beginning to finish. The paths diverge and intersect; no one plans the shape of the night in advance. And yet, like the emergent highways forming on screen, the whole eventually crystallizes into something ordered — not random, not designed, but the irreducible product of simple rules followed faithfully by many agents simultaneously.

The ants on this canvas each follow a deterministic rule table, as spare as "if the cell is white, paint it black and turn right." After thousands of steps of apparent chaos, long diagonal highways emerge unbidden from the noise — ordered structures that no designer specified. The Torah night is this: individual learners following their own rules, the collective enterprise converging into highways of meaning from what looked, in the small hours, like wandering.

## This Piece

Five turmites — two-dimensional Turing machines generalizing Langton's Ant — run simultaneously on an 800×800 canvas. Each follows a distinct deterministic rule table: the classic Langton's RL rule, its mirror LR, and three four-state highway-forming rules from the known catalog (LLRR, LRRL, RLLR). Each ant carries its own independent color-state grid; their trails overwrite each other visually but never interfere algorithmically. The result is layered, crossing highways in five warm tones — gold, amber, copper, wheat, and cream — materializing from dark navy, exactly as the Tikkun Leil materializes from the darkness of a night that should have been slept through but was not.
