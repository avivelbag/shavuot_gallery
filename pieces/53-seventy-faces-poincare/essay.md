# Seventy Faces — The Poincaré Disk as Living Torah

*"The Torah has seventy faces."*
— BaMidbar Rabbah 13:15 (שבעים פנים לתורה)

---

## The Teaching

There is a principle so central to rabbinic thought that it appears almost as an axiom: every text of Torah simultaneously holds multiple valid meanings. The Sages counted seventy. The Zohar teaches that the Torah was given in fire — black fire written on white fire — and that each letter contains worlds within worlds. Rashi reads a word one way; Ibn Ezra reads it another; both are correct, and neither cancels the other.

This is not a failure of precision. It is the designed nature of divine speech.

The medieval commentators systematized this plurality into four levels known by the acronym **PaRDeS** — an orchard, a garden, a paradise:

- **Peshat** — the plain, contextual meaning of the text
- **Remez** — the allegorical or philosophical dimension hinted below the surface
- **Derash** — the homiletical layer, the rabbinic imagination spinning out narrative
- **Sod** — the secret, mystical reading that dissolves language back into mystery

But the Rabbis were careful: these four are a floor, not a ceiling. The seventy faces remain open. A word that was read one way in Alexandria is read differently in Babylon, differently again in Troyes, differently again in Bnei Brak today — and all those readings are simultaneously alive in the text, waiting to be drawn out by the right question at the right moment.

Shavuot is the festival of receiving this text. Not merely a book. An inexhaustible tradition.

## The Geometry

The hyperbolic plane is a mathematical space where the usual rules of Euclidean geometry fail in a beautiful way. On a flat Euclidean surface, you can tile a plane with regular hexagons and every tile is identical in size. In the hyperbolic plane, you can do something Euclidean space forbids: tile a bounded disk with infinitely many congruent tiles that pack more and more densely as they approach the boundary, shrinking toward zero while remaining — in the internal geometry of the disk — exactly the same size as the tiles at the center.

The Poincaré disk model represents this strange space. A circle on a screen becomes an infinite world. Near the center, tiles appear large; near the boundary, they appear tiny; but a creature living inside this geometry would measure them as equal. What looks like "the edge" of the disk is actually infinitely far away — a horizon that can never be reached.

This is the mathematical metaphor for Torah.

The text of the Written Torah is finite: 304,805 letters. A child can copy it out by hand in a year. And yet the tradition insists that this bounded object contains infinite meaning — that every generation finds interpretations no previous generation had, that the conversation never concludes, that the commentary tradition is not a supplement to the Torah but its natural unfolding, its own form of revelation.

The Poincaré disk says: you can contain infinity in a finite circle. The tiles near the center are legible and obvious — the plain meaning, the peshat. But as you move outward, the tiles multiply and become finer, holding more and more meaning in less and less apparent space. The boundary of the disk is a horizon: always approached, never reached, always receding.

## The Tessellation

The tiling shown here is generated by the hyperbolic reflection group. A fundamental domain — a hyperbolic triangle or polygon — is reflected across its edges to fill the disk. Each reflection is a Möbius transformation, a conformal map that preserves angles while distorting distances.

The seven colors correspond to the seven weeks of Sefirat HaOmer — the forty-nine day count from Pesach to Shavuot — and also to the seven species of the Land of Israel: wheat, barley, grapes, figs, pomegranates, olives, dates. Color assignment ensures that no two adjacent tiles share a color, a proper hyperbolic coloring, reflecting how the seventy faces of Torah do not contradict each other: adjacent interpretations are distinct.

At the center of the disk — the point of highest legibility, the place from which all reflections originate — sits the letter **ת** (Tav), the final letter of the Torah. The tradition teaches that when Moses finished writing the Torah, the last letter he wrote was the Tav of the word *yisrael*. The Tav is also the last letter of the Hebrew alphabet: it marks both conclusion and the threshold of a new beginning. A circle made of letters that starts again. An inexhaustible scroll.

## Shavuot and the Open Text

The Talmud records that when Israel stood at Sinai and said *naaseh v'nishma* — "we will do and we will hear" — they placed action before understanding. They accepted a text they had not yet read. They committed to a tradition that would continue to generate meaning long after any individual reading.

This is the peculiar gift of Shavuot: not a law delivered complete, closed, final — but an open system, a fractal structure in which every generation has to interpret for itself, receiving the Torah newly each time.

*"Her ways are ways of pleasantness, and all her paths are peace. She is a tree of life to those who grasp her, and those who hold her fast are made happy."* — Proverbs 3:17–18

A tree of life. The image is organic, growing, never finished.

The Poincaré disk rotates slowly. This is a Möbius transformation: a map that looks like a rotation but carries points from the center toward the boundary and back again, cycling through all the tiles without ever repeating. The mathematics guarantees that every tile will be visited. The animation is not decorative: it is the motion of interpretation itself — each reading bringing the whole structure into focus, each new angle revealing tiles that were invisible before.

## On the Plurality of Meaning

Rabbi Akiva, the tradition teaches, could derive new teachings from the decorative crowns (tagin) atop each letter of the Torah — marks that were understood to be purely ornamental. Nothing in the Torah is ornamental. Every stroke participates in the seventy faces.

The heresy the Rabbis guarded against was not multiple interpretation. It was the arrogance of insisting there is only one. To claim that the plain meaning exhausts the text is, in this tradition, a kind of idolatry — to reduce the infinite to the finite, the divine voice to a single fixed meaning.

And so the Poincaré disk: infinitely many tiles, all congruent, all valid, all simultaneously present — contained within a single circle that you can hold in your hand and call small.

שבעים פנים לתורה. Seventy faces. And more.
