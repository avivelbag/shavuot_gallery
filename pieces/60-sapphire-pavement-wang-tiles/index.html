לִבְנַת הַסַּפִּיר — The Pavement of Sapphire

וַיִּרְאוּ אֵת אֱלֹהֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל וְתַחַת רַגְלָיו כְּמַעֲשֵׂה לִבְנַת הַסַּפִּיר וּכְעֶצֶם הַשָּׁמַיִם לָטֹהַר "They saw the God of Israel, and under His feet was something like a pavement made of sapphire, clear as the very sky." — Exodus 24:10

The ascent to Sinai described in Exodus 24:9–11 is one of the Torah's most extraordinary passages. Moses, Aaron, Nadab, Abihu, and seventy elders of Israel climb the mountain together to ratify the covenant. They see the God of Israel. And then — with breathtaking ordinariness — the text reports: va-yochlu va-yishtu, "they ate and they drank" (Exodus 24:11). No one was struck down. No one fell in terror. They saw the divine and had lunch. The vision of the sapphire pavement is embedded in this remarkable domesticity: the most transcendent moment in the Torah ends with a meal.

The pavement itself — livnat ha-sapir — is described only by what it was like: something like sapphire tiles, clear as the sky itself. The Torah names no architect, gives no dimensions, offers no further description. It is a floor, not a ceiling. The divine is not above, but below; not distant but underfoot.

Rashi, citing the Midrash, connects the pavement to the memory of Egyptian slavery. During the bondage, Israelite women had given birth on the bricks, and those bricks were bound up in the image of oppression. God kept the sapphire-like image before Him during the plagues as a reminder of Israel's suffering — and now, at the moment of covenant, that memory was transformed into radiance. The pavement of sapphire is the sublimation of clay.

Nachmanides reads differently. For him, the sapphire represents the pure revealed light of the divine intellect — the luminosity of pure being, which the elders perceived directly in that moment of covenant. The floor is not a reminder of slavery but an emanation of the infinite, grounded in the visible world.

Both readings point to the same paradox: this is a floor, something walked upon, yet it is made of the sky. Wang tiles enact this quality. Each tile is a small square with colored edges that must match its neighbors — structured by law, yet producing patterns that never quite repeat. The mosaic is legible but inexhaustible, rule-governed but endlessly surprising. This is what the Torah describes: not chaos but an ordered radiance whose regularity is the regularity of law, and whose beauty is always new.

The empty center — תַּחַת רַגְלָיו, "under His feet" — holds the place of the vision without depicting what stood above it. That is left, deliberately, without image.
