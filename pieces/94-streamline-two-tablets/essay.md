# Between the Tablets, the Law Flows

## The Object

"And He gave to Moses, when He had finished speaking with him on Mount Sinai, the two tablets of the testimony, tablets of stone — written by the finger of God." (Exodus 31:18)

Shavuot commemorates the giving of the Torah at Sinai, and at the center of that giving stand the *luchot habrit* — the tablets of the covenant. Not papyrus, not clay: stone. The choice of material is not incidental. The root ח-ר-ת (*charut*, engraved) and the word *cherut* (freedom) share the same letters; the Sages read them together: "Read not *charut* but *cherut* — the truly free person is one who occupies himself with Torah" (Avot 6:2). Engraving is irreversible. The law and the stone are made one substance.

## Both Sets in the Ark

The story of the tablets is also a story of shattering. When Moses descended from Sinai and saw the golden calf, he threw the tablets to the ground and broke them (Exodus 32:19). The replacement tablets — cut and written during Moses' second forty days on the mountain — descended whole. The Talmud (Bava Batra 14a) records that both sets were placed inside the Ark of the Covenant: the whole tablets of the second giving, and the fragments of the first. The broken pieces were not discarded. They were carried.

The Talmud Yerushalmi (Shekalim 6:1) makes the principle explicit: *שברי לוחות מונחים בארון* — "the fragments of the tablets rest in the Ark." Because the fragments of something holy remain holy. Brokenness does not cancel sanctity. The shattering was not a failure; it was a beginning. In this reading, Moses' act of smashing the tablets is not a moment of despair but a pedagogical one — the broken law travels alongside the whole law because to be a people of the covenant is to carry both.

## The Division of the Law

The majority rabbinic view divides the Ten Commandments five to each tablet. The first five govern the vertical axis — the relationship between the human and the divine. The second five govern the horizontal axis — the relationships among people. The tablets stand beside each other as equal weight, cut from identical stone. The Zohar (II:85b) maps this onto the cosmic structure: the right side corresponds to Hesed (loving-kindness) and the left to Din (judgment). Together they form the complete body of the law.

This topology matters. The two obligations — toward God and toward neighbor — cannot simply be ranked or collapsed. They are co-originating, of equal structural weight. Leviticus 19:18, "love your neighbor as yourself: I am the Lord," appends the divine name to a human commandment, as if to say: these are aspects of one field.

## The Magnetic Dipole Field

The artwork renders the invisible structure of the space between and around the tablets using a precise mathematical model: two magnetic dipoles placed symmetrically — one for each tablet — each with moment pointing in the same direction (upward). The field lines you see are solutions to the differential equation governing how a vector field flows through space when two equal sources drive it.

The integration method is Runge-Kutta of order 4 (RK4), a standard algorithm for tracing curves through vector fields with high accuracy. From seed points arranged in a ring around each pole, the algorithm steps forward along the field direction — sampling the field at four intermediate positions per step to achieve fourth-order accuracy — accumulating the path until it hits a pole or exits the canvas.

These field lines have a fundamental mathematical property: they never cross. At every point in the plane there is exactly one field direction, and so no two streamlines from different seeds can intersect. The law is the same: obligations toward God and toward neighbor do not contradict each other, even though they originate from different sources. The non-crossing property is not a metaphor imposed on the mathematics; it is a theorem.

The color encodes field strength: electric blue and cyan near the sources, where the field is most intense, fading through deep cobalt toward near-black at the far margins. The law is brightest at its origin. It does not vanish at distance — it only dims.

## The Breathing

The seed-point rings rotate slowly — two degrees per second — causing the streamlines to grow, retract, and redistribute in a gentle continuous cycle. Because RK4 integration is deterministic, the same seeds always produce the same lines; the drift simply rotates which lines are visible, so the pattern breathes without chaos. The Talmud (Shabbat 88a) describes the mountain held suspended over Israel at the moment of revelation. The law arrived in the midst of an overwhelming, unresolvable tension. That suspension — present, charged, unresolved — is what the breathing holds.

The stone tablets are visible as outlines behind the field lines. The numerals mark which five commandments live on each stone. But the life of the law is not inside the stone. It is in the space the tablets create between them — the field of mutual influence and attraction that neither pole can produce alone.
