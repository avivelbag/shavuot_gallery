Between the Tablets, the Law Flows

לוּחוֹת הַבְּרִית — The Tablets of the Covenant

וַיִּתֵּן אֶל-מֹשֶׁה, כְּכַלֹּתוֹ לְדַבֵּר אִתּוֹ בְּהַר סִינַי, שְׁנֵי לֻחֹת הָעֵדֻת, לֻחֹת אֶבֶן—כְּתֻבִים בְּאֶצְבַּע אֱלֹהִים.
"And He gave to Moses, when He had finished speaking with him on Mount Sinai, the two tablets of the testimony, tablets of stone — written by the finger of God."
Exodus 31:18 (שמות לא:יח)

Shavuot commemorates the giving of the Torah and, at its center, the luchot habrit — the tablets of the covenant. Two slabs of stone descended from Sinai bearing the Ten Commandments, one half of the relationship between the human and the divine made literal and portable.

Both Sets in the Ark

The Talmud (Bava Batra 14a) records something remarkable: when Moses descended from Sinai the second time carrying the replacement tablets (Exodus 34:28), the fragments of the first — the ones he had shattered at the sight of the golden calf — were placed inside the Ark alongside the whole ones. Broken law and whole law, carried together.

וַיְהִי, כַּאֲשֶׁר קָרַב אֶל-הַמַּחֲנֶה, וַיַּרְא אֶת-הָעֵגֶל וּמְחֹלֹת; וַיִּחַר-אַף מֹשֶׁה, וַיַּשְׁלֵךְ מִיָּדָו אֶת-הַלֻּחֹת, וַיְשַׁבֵּר אֹתָם תַּחַת הָהָר.
"And it came to pass, as he drew near the camp, that he saw the calf and the dancing; and Moses' anger burned hot, and he threw the tablets from his hands, and broke them beneath the mountain."
Exodus 32:19 (שמות לב:יט)

The Talmud Yerushalmi (Shekalim 6:1) draws the conclusion that the Sages found in this act not a disaster but a teaching: שברי לוחות מונחים בארון — "the fragments of the tablets rest in the Ark." Because the fragments of something holy remain holy. The shattering was not a failure; it was a beginning. The broken pieces belong.

Five and Five

The majority rabbinic view divides the commandments five to each tablet. The first five govern the relationship between the human and the divine; the second five govern the relationships among people. The two tablets are not merely duplicates — they encode a topology. One half of the law faces inward toward God, the other outward toward neighbor. They stand beside each other as equal weight, which is why they are cut from stone of identical size and shape.

The Zohar (II:85b) reads this division cosmically: the right side (divine commandments) corresponds to the sefirah of Hesed — loving-kindness — and the left side (interpersonal commandments) to Din — judgment. The tablets together are the human body, heart and hand, soul and society.

The Field Between

The artwork renders the invisible structure of the space between and around the two tablets using a precise mathematical model: two magnetic dipoles, one at each stone, both oriented upward. The field lines you see are not decoration — they are the solution to the differential equation that describes how a vector field flows through space when two equal sources generate it.

These lines have a fundamental property: they never cross. Two field lines from different streamlines cannot intersect because at every point in space there is exactly one field direction. The law, similarly, does not contradict itself: the obligation toward God and the obligation toward neighbor cannot cross or cancel, even though they arise from different sources. The Sifra (Kedoshim 2:4) finds the same non-contradiction encoded in Leviticus 19:18 — "love your neighbor as yourself: I am the Lord" — the divine signature appended to the human commandment, as if to say they are aspects of a single field.

The lines arch between the two poles in curves that recall the shape of a Torah scroll crown (keter Torah), and fan outward in rays that suggest the divine light descending on Sinai. The electric-blue palette — intense near the sources, fading to near-black at the margins — maps field strength to luminosity: the law is brightest at its source and dims only at distance, never vanishing entirely.

The Breathing

The seed-point ring rotates two degrees per second, causing the streamlines to grow, retract, and shift in an endless slow cycle — as if the field is breathing, or as if the tablets are still alive with whatever charged the air of Sinai. The Talmud (Shabbat 88a) says the mountain was held suspended over Israel; the law itself remained suspended between stone and air, between the written word and the space it creates around itself. That breathing is what this animation holds.

The law is not contained in the stone. It lives in the space the tablets create between them.
