# As One Person, With One Heart

## The Singular Verb at Sinai

When the Israelites arrived at Mount Sinai, the Torah deploys a grammatical anomaly. Throughout the desert wanderings the people travel in the plural — *va-yisu*, "they journeyed"; *va-yachanu*, "they camped." But at the moment of arrival, Exodus 19:2 suddenly uses the singular:

> **וַיִּסְעוּ מֵרְפִידִים וַיָּבֹאוּ מִדְבַּר סִינַי וַיַּחֲנוּ בַּמִּדְבָּר וַיִּחַן-שָׁם יִשְׂרָאֵל נֶגֶד הָהָר.**
>
> "They journeyed from Rephidim, and they came to the wilderness of Sinai and they camped in the wilderness — and Israel camped there, opposite the mountain." — Exodus 19:2

The closing verb, *va-yichan*, is singular: "and *he* camped." Rashi, following the Mechilta d'Rabbi Ishmael, reads this as the Torah's mark that Israel arrived at Sinai כְּאִישׁ אֶחָד בְּלֵב אֶחָד — *k'ish echad b'lev echad*, "as one person with one heart." Twelve tribes, each with a distinct genealogy, banner, and encampment direction, achieved momentary perfect unity at the foot of the mountain.

## Twelve Pillars at the Mountain

> **וַיִּכְתֹּב מֹשֶׁה אֵת כָּל-דִּבְרֵי יְהוָה, וַיַּשְׁכֵּם בַּבֹּקֶר, וַיִּבֶן מִזְבֵּחַ תַּחַת הָהָר, וּשְׁתֵּים עֶשְׂרֵה מַצֵּבָה לִשְׁנֵים עָשָׂר שִׁבְטֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל.**
>
> "Moses then wrote down all the words of the LORD. Early in the morning he arose and built an altar at the foot of the mountain, with twelve pillars for the twelve tribes of Israel." — Exodus 24:4

Twelve distinct pillars stood together at the mountain's base — not merged into one, but each present, each oriented toward the same center. Unity here was not uniformity.

## The Arrangement in the Wilderness

Numbers 2 specifies the camp geometry. Judah's three-tribe division faced east, leading every march. Reuben's camp anchored the south. Ephraim commanded the west. Dan's division closed the rear to the north. The Levites and the Tabernacle occupied the exact center. The wilderness camp was not a crowd but an ordered cosmos: each tribe in its place, all twelve faces turned inward toward a shared center of gravity.

## The Cellular Potts Model

François Graner and James Glazier developed the cellular Potts model in 1992 to simulate cell sorting in biological tissues — the process by which embryonic cells of different types spontaneously segregate into coherent layers. Unlike the Ising model, which has only two states (spin up or spin down), the Potts model supports any number of distinguishable cell types. An energy function penalizes unlike-neighbor boundaries, and the Metropolis algorithm drives sorting: at each step, propose copying a neighbor's type into a cell; accept if energy decreases, or with probability exp(−ΔE/T) otherwise. As temperature T decreases, the system freezes: each type consolidates into a large coherent domain, cross-domain boundaries become energetically costly to traverse, and the territory map stabilizes.

The energy penalty structure encodes theology as physics. Two cells of the same tribe touch at zero cost — no tension within a tribe. A tribal cell bordering the void (the Tabernacle courtyard) pays a moderate cost of 2 — the sacred center imposes a modest boundary. Two cells from different tribes touching each other pay the highest cost of 5 — the most urgent pressure to sort, to find one's territory, to stop living intermingled with strangers.

## The Non-Obvious Insight

The twelve tribes did not achieve *k'ish echad b'lev echad* by dissolving into each other. Each kept its color, its banner, its genealogy. The Potts model explains why this is not a contradiction: sorting into coherent domains *is* the mechanism that makes genuine unity possible. A randomly mixed lattice has no spatial structure — no tribe knows where it stands relative to the others, or relative to the center. Only when each tribe consolidates into its own territory, touching adjacent tribes at clean boundaries, does the entire camp become oriented around the Tabernacle. The moment of unity is the moment the domains form and hold — each tribe present, distinct, and in its place.

The simulation begins in maximum disorder — twelve colors scattered randomly across 40,000 cells. Temperature falls. Domains coalesce. The tribal names emerge over each territory. When the system finally freezes, the twelve distinct colored regions radiate outward from the dark central void. *K'ish echad b'lev echad*: as one person, with one heart.
