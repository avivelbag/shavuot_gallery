# Where All Paths Converge

## Given in the Wilderness

The Mechilta d'Rabbi Yishmael (Yitro 1) asks a pointed question: why was the Torah given
specifically in the wilderness of Sinai, and not in the Land of Israel?

> דָּבָר אַחֵר: לָמָּה נִתְּנָה תּוֹרָה בַּמִּדְבָּר? לְפִי שֶׁנִּתְּנָה בִּמְקוֹם הֶפְקֵר
> בְּפַרְהֶסְיָא — כָּל הָרוֹצֶה לְקַבֵּל יָבוֹא וְיִקַּבֵּל.

"Another interpretation: Why was the Torah given in the wilderness? Because it was given in ownerless,
public space — let anyone who wishes to receive it come and receive it."

If Torah had been given in any tribe's territory, that tribe would have claimed it. The wilderness —
*hefker*, ownerless — was the only space large enough to belong to everyone. And so the entire people
assembled at Sinai: as one.

Exodus 19:2 records their arrival:

> וַיַּחֲנוּ בַמִּדְבָּר וַיִּחַן-שָׁם יִשְׂרָאֵל נֶגֶד הָהָר׃

"They encamped in the wilderness, and Israel encamped there, opposite the mountain."

The verb *vayichan* — "he encamped" — appears in the singular, though hundreds of thousands had gathered.
Rashi hears in this a teaching: *ke-ish echad be-lev echad*, "like one person, with one heart." Every
soul present at the same moment, facing the same mountain, beginning from the same starting point.

## The Sinai Billiard

In 1970, the Russian-American mathematician Yakov Grigoryevich Sinai proved a theorem about a
deceptively simple system: a square room with a circular obstacle at its center. He showed that
the billiard trajectories inside this room are *ergodic* — over infinite time, almost every trajectory
visits every region of the room with equal frequency, touching every corner, filling all available
space — and *hyperbolic* — nearby trajectories diverge from each other exponentially fast. The system
bears his name: the Sinai billiard.

The double pun was not lost on those who encountered the theorem.

Ergodicity is a promise: given enough time, every soul reaches every corner of Torah. No region
of the room is permanently avoided. The wilderness belongs to everyone, and over the infinite span
of study and transmission, every path fills the entire space.

But the short-term tells a different story. Two particles launched from positions only a millimeter
apart — same velocity, same room, same obstacle — trace completely different paths. The circular
obstacle acts as a focusing lens: any two trajectories incident on opposite sides of the circle
are reflected outward in opposite directions, amplifying every initial difference exponentially with
each collision. By the hundredth bounce, the two particles are on opposite sides of the room.

## Each Person Heard Differently

Shemot Rabbah 5:9 preserves the teaching:

> כֵּיוָן שֶׁנִּתְּנָה תּוֹרָה לְיִשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁנֶּחֱלַק הַקּוֹל לְשִׁבְעִים לָשׁוֹן,
> וְכָל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד שָׁמַע לְפִי כֹּחוֹ.

"When the Torah was given to Israel, the voice divided into seventy languages, and each and every
person heard according to their strength."

They were all present at once — *ke-ish echad* — yet each soul was a distinct starting point, a
slightly different position within the cluster. The same revelation, passing through ten different
people, produces ten different trajectories through the same room.

This is what this piece makes visible. Ten particles begin from a single tight cluster — nearly
identical starting positions, the same velocity, the same room, the same central obstacle. Within a
few dozen collisions, they scatter across the entire table. Their trails, accumulating over time,
fill the room with equal density — just as ergodic theory predicts. But no two trails are alike.
The crimson particle has not gone where the gold one went. The sapphire has not bounced where the
violet has.

They are ten souls who stood together at the same mountain, at the same moment, heard the same
voice — and each went somewhere different.

The room, over time, fills completely. Every corner is reached. The wilderness belongs to all of
them equally. But the paths that got them there were their own.
