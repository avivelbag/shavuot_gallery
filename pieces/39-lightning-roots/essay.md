# Lightning Roots — Lichtenberg at Sinai

**Exodus 19:16–19**

> *וַיְהִי בַיּוֹם הַשְּׁלִישִׁי בִּהְיֹת הַבֹּקֶר וַיְהִי קֹלֹת וּבְרָקִים וְעָנָן כָּבֵד עַל הָהָר וְקֹל שֹׁפָר חָזָק מְאֹד וַיֶּחֱרַד כָּל הָעָם אֲשֶׁר בַּמַּחֲנֶה*
>
> "And it came to pass on the third day, when it was morning, that there were thunders and lightnings and a thick cloud upon the mount, and the voice of the shofar exceeding loud; and all the people that were in the camp trembled."
>
> *וְקוֹל הַשֹּׁפָר הוֹלֵךְ וְחָזֵק מְאֹד*
>
> "And the voice of the shofar grew louder and louder exceedingly."

On the morning of the third day, the mountain was wrapped in cloud and fire and the sound of a shofar growing, impossibly, louder with each passing moment. Natural thunder begins with a crack and fades. The shofar at Sinai inverted this law: it grew. Rashi, commenting on *הוֹלֵךְ וְחָזֵק*, notes that this was one of the signs distinguishing the revelation from every earthly phenomenon — the sound increased where it should have diminished, announcing that its source was not physical resonance but divine speech.

The Mechilta de-Rabbi Ishmael on this verse counts the voices — *kolot* — not as overlapping noise but as distinct utterances, each one a separate divine communication. "Every single commandment that issued from the mouth of the Holy One, blessed be He, split into seventy voices, and each voice went out to seventy nations in their languages." The single overwhelming sound at Sinai was simultaneously seventy communications, infinitely branched, reaching every people in the form suited to it. What the Israelites experienced as thunder was structured: a tree of voice, each branch a complete message.

## The Branching Geometry of Revelation

The Dielectric Breakdown Model, developed by Niemeyer, Pietronero, and Wiesmann in 1984, simulates the growth of electric discharge through an insulating medium. The algorithm maintains a grid of potential values — high at one pole, low at another — and models lightning not as a single straight bolt but as a tree that grows probabilistically. At each step, a candidate cell on the boundary of the existing discharge tree is selected with probability proportional to its local electric potential raised to a power η. Higher η produces more linear, spindly trees; lower η produces broader, more diffuse branching. The resulting forms — Lichtenberg figures — are the exact shapes of real lightning struck into acrylic or sand, and of the dendritic growths found in snowflakes, river deltas, and lung bronchi.

The Lichtenberg figure is a fractal: zoom into any branch and the same pattern of subdivision repeats. The trunk divides; the major branches subdivide; each twig ends in the same way the trunk began. The geometry encodes its own description at every scale.

The Maharal of Prague, in *Tiferet Yisrael* (chapter 25), describes Torah as a *tree of life* in which every root law contains all its derivative applications already present in seed form. When a scholar draws out a *chiddush* — a new legal insight — they are not adding to Torah but revealing what was already implicit in the structure. The tree of Torah law branches by the same self-similar logic as the electric discharge: each subdivision follows the same rule as the one that produced it.

The verse from Proverbs — *עֵץ חַיִּים הִיא לַמַּחֲזִיקִים בָּהּ* — "It is a tree of life to those who hold fast to it" (Proverbs 3:18) — is sung each time the Torah is returned to the ark. The image is not metaphorical decoration. It is a structural claim: the Torah organizes its content the way a tree organizes its growth, the way a lightning bolt organizes its discharge.

## Sinai and the Lichtenberg Figure

The lightning in this piece grows downward, rooting into the mountain rather than striking from a cloud. This reversal is intentional. The revelation at Sinai was a discharge from above into the ground — divine speech entering the material world through a mountain, through stone tablets, through letters engraved into rock. The tree grows from heaven downward and roots itself in the peak of Sinai.

Each growth cycle is seeded fresh. The DBM algorithm is stochastic: the same parameters produce a different tree each time, because the probabilistic branching at each step accumulates differently. No two Lichtenberg figures are identical. In the same way, the Mechilta teaches that though the Torah was given once, each Jew at Sinai heard it according to the capacity of their soul — the same revelation branching differently into seventy nations, into six hundred thousand Israelite souls, into every generation that was present in potential. The randomness in the branching is the point: the revelation was both single and infinitely individuated.

When the tree completes — when it roots into the mountain — the image holds briefly, then fades. A new cycle begins. There is no final Lichtenberg figure. There is only the act of branching.
