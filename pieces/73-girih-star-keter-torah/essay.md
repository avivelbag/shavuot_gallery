# The Crown Woven in Geometry

## Pirkei Avot 4:13 — The Three Crowns

Rabbi Shimon taught: there are three crowns — the crown of Torah, the crown of priesthood, and the crown of royalty. But the crown of a good name (*Keter Shem Tov*) surpasses them all.

The crown of royalty belongs to one dynasty. The crown of priesthood belongs to one tribe. But the crown of Torah, the Rabbis teach, is available to anyone who reaches out and takes it. And yet even Torah's crown is surpassed by the crown of a good name — because a good name is only earned through how one *uses* the Torah one has received.

## Keter Torah: The Tagin

What is the crown of Torah, concretely? Look at a Torah scroll. The sofer — the scribe — does not merely copy letters. On certain letters, he draws *tagin*: tiny, three-pronged coronets that sit atop the letter like a miniature crown. Shin has three tagin; gimel, zayin, ayin, tzadi, nun, and tet each have one. These are not ornamental. They are commanded.

The Talmud (*Menachot* 29b) tells this story: when Moses ascended Mount Sinai to receive the Torah, he found the Holy One drawing these very crowns on the letters. Moses asked: "Master of the universe, who delays You?" God answered: "There is a man who will arise in many generations, Akiva ben Yosef by name, who will derive mountains of laws from each and every tag." Moses asked to see him. He was transported to Rabbi Akiva's study hall and sat in the back row, unable to follow the discussion. He grew faint. Then a student asked Rabbi Akiva: "From where do you derive this?" And Akiva answered: "*Halacha l'Moshe miSinai* — it is a law given to Moses at Sinai." And Moses was comforted.

Every crown drawn on every letter contains teaching that has not yet been unpacked. The Torah was given once; it is still being received.

## Geometry That Encodes the Infinite

The girih star pattern works the same way. Medieval craftsmen — in the mosques and madrasas of Iran, Afghanistan, and Morocco, from the 11th century onward — produced floor tiles and wall panels using the five girih tiles: the decagon, the elongated hexagon, the bowtie, the rhombus, and the regular pentagon. They fit together without gaps. The resulting patterns cover the plane in a way that never exactly repeats — they are *aperiodic*, what mathematicians today call a quasiperiodic tiling, structurally related to the Penrose tiling discovered in the West only in 1974.

The medieval craftsmen did not have this vocabulary. They discovered the geometry empirically, pattern by patient pattern, testing tile against tile by hand. Centuries later, mathematical physicists needed supercomputers to prove what those craftsmen had *found* — that a finite set of shapes encodes an infinite, non-repeating order.

This is the structure of the tagin. The crowns on the letters are finite marks. But Rabbi Akiva found infinite law hidden in each one. The letter *Shin* is three strokes. Its crown is three more. The pattern is simple; the meaning is inexhaustible.

## Shavuot and the Endless Crown

Shavuot is when the Torah — crowns, tagin, and all — was given. The sages teach that at Sinai, God placed two crowns on the head of each Israelite: one for *naaseh* (we will do) and one for *nishma* (we will hear). The Torah was not given to a priesthood or a monarchy; it was given to a people who accepted it entirely, in advance, before understanding what they were accepting.

The central star of this piece — the bright gold decagon bearing the letter *כ*, Kaf, for *Keter* — is the crown at the center of an infinite pattern. Every surrounding star is the same star, repeated across an aperiodic lattice that covers the plane without end. There is no center in the mathematical tiling — any point can be the center, and the pattern looks the same from everywhere. But we place a crown at the center because that is what we do: we orient ourselves toward the Torah as toward a center, even as its meaning fans out in every direction forever.
