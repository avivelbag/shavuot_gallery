# Moses Received and Transmitted — The Möbius Chain of Tradition

**Pirkei Avot 1:1**

> *מֹשֶׁה קִבֵּל תּוֹרָה מִסִּינַי, וּמְסָרָהּ לִיהוֹשֻׁעַ, וִיהוֹשֻׁעַ לִזְקֵנִים, וּזְקֵנִים לִנְבִיאִים, וּנְבִיאִים מְסָרוּהָ לְאַנְשֵׁי כְנֶסֶת הַגְּדוֹלָה*

"Moses received the Torah from Sinai and transmitted it to Joshua; Joshua to the Elders; the Elders to the Prophets; and the Prophets transmitted it to the Men of the Great Assembly."

These are the first words of Pirkei Avot, the tractate of rabbinic wisdom that every community has read on the Shabbatot between Pesach and Shavuot — one chapter per week — since the early medieval period. The timing is not incidental. By the time Shavuot arrives, recalling the moment of original revelation at Sinai, one has just spent six weeks rehearsing the chain that stretches from that moment to the present. Avot 1:1 is the opening of that rehearsal: here is where it all began; here is the first link.

The chain names five links explicitly: Moses, Joshua, the Elders, the Prophets, the Men of the Great Assembly. The Men of the Great Assembly — the *Anshei Knesset HaGedolah* — represent the period of Ezra and Nehemiah, the return from Babylonian exile, the reconstitution of Jewish communal life in the Land. They receive the tradition and, in Avot's account, hand it on to specific named individuals: Shimon HaTzaddik, then Antigonus of Socho, then the pairs (*zugot*). From there the chain continues unbroken into the Mishnaic period, through the Talmudic period, down to the present.

---

## The Epistemological Claim

The claim of Avot 1:1 is not merely historical. It is epistemological. The chain asserts that the Oral Torah — the tradition of interpretation, application, and extension that was never written down at Sinai — carries the same authority as the Written Torah because both derive from the same moment of revelation. Moses received *Torah*, not merely the written text; he received instruction in its meaning, application, and unfolding. This fuller reception is what he transmitted to Joshua.

This claim was deeply contested. The Sadducees rejected the authority of oral tradition entirely, insisting only on the written text. Later, the Karaites made the same argument. Avot opens with the chain of transmission precisely to establish the warrant: if you can trace an unbroken line from the present back to Sinai, the authority is original, not derived. The chain *is* the argument. Shavuot celebrates not merely the gift of the written text but the far more fragile and precious gift of oral tradition — the living interpretation that makes the text inhabitable across every change of circumstance.

## Möbius Transformations and the Schottky Group

A Möbius transformation is a map of the form *f(z) = (az + b) / (cz + d)* on the Riemann sphere of complex numbers, with *ad − bc ≠ 0*. These are the conformal maps of the sphere: they preserve angles, map circles to circles (or circles to lines, which are circles through infinity), and compose to form a group. Three points determine a Möbius transformation uniquely, just as three points determine a circle.

The Schottky group construction begins with a set of circles whose interiors are pairwise disjoint. For each circle, one defines a Möbius transformation that maps the exterior of that circle to the interior of a paired circle. Iterating all transformations and their inverses generates an infinite cascade of image circles: each original circle maps to a copy, each copy maps to further copies, the copies of copies shrink and accumulate toward limit points on the boundary of the fractal limit set.

The depth of the cascade corresponds to the generation count: the seed circles are generation 0, their first images are generation 1, and so on. At each generation, the circles are smaller and more numerous. They converge toward the *limit set* of the Schottky group — the set of points that remain bounded under all iterations, the fractal boundary where circles shrink to points.

## Transmission as Conformal Mapping

Each generation in the chain of tradition is a conformal copy of what preceded it. The shape is preserved — the angles of interpretation, the structure of argument, the methods of derivation — but the position shifts: a new time, a new place, a new community. Moses' reception at Sinai is the seed circle. Joshua's reception from Moses is its first Möbius image: conformally equivalent, positionally displaced. The Elders receive from Joshua; the Prophets from the Elders; the Men of the Great Assembly from the Prophets. Each generation is mapped to the next by a transformation that preserves form.

The limit points of the Schottky group — where circles shrink to dust — are the horizon of history: the generations too distant to name individually, the anonymous transmitters who carried the tradition through centuries of persecution, exile, and dispersion. They are not absent from the chain; they are its limiting structure, the boundary toward which the named links converge.
