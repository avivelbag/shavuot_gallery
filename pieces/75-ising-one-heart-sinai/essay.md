# כְּאִישׁ אֶחָד בְּלֵב אֶחָד — As One Person, With One Heart

## Rashi at the Mountain

"And they journeyed from Rephidim and came to the wilderness of Sinai, and they encamped in the wilderness; and Israel encamped there before the mountain." (Exodus 19:2)

Rashi, citing the Mechilta, pauses at the grammar of the last clause. The Hebrew switches from plural to singular — *va-yichan* — "and he encamped," not "and they encamped." This is not incidental. Rashi writes: *ke-ish echad be-lev echad* — "as one person with one heart." He continues: "But all their other encampments were in a spirit of complaint and division." Sinai was different. Something happened to the people before they arrived at the foot of the mountain. They became, in some sense, a single being.

Midrash Tanchuma (Yitro 9) elaborates: only when Israel became as one were they worthy to receive the Torah. This is a strong claim. Unity was not a pleasant side-note to the revelation — it was its *prerequisite*. The Torah could not descend until the people had risen into something collectively new.

## The Snap That Physics Discovered

In the twentieth century, statistical mechanics discovered something strange about matter: phase transitions do not happen gradually. You cannot edge slowly into a ferromagnet. Below a precise critical temperature — for the two-dimensional Ising model, T_c = 2/ln(1+√2) ≈ 2.269 — something *snaps*. Spins that were randomly oriented in every direction, pointing this way and that like individual people pursuing individual desires, suddenly align. Large coherent domains crystallize out of noise. The disorder does not smoothly dissolve; it crosses a threshold and collapses.

This is the non-obvious teaching of statistical physics: *individual components cannot produce collective order by themselves.* There is a critical threshold, and either you are above it (disorder, independence, fragmentation) or below it (unity, coherence, a single emergent direction). There is no gradual middle path.

## The Mode of Reception

Shavuot celebrates not merely the content of the Torah — the commandments, the narratives, the legislation — but the *mode of its reception*. Exodus 24:7 records the moment: *naaseh v'nishma* — "we will do and we will hear," spoken, according to the Talmud (Shabbat 88a), as a single voice. Not as individuals negotiating terms. As one.

The Ising phase transition is a mathematical model of the moment Rashi describes. At T=4.5, the spins are noise — a salt-and-pepper image of individual, uncorrelated choices. As temperature descends through the critical point, fractal domains of aligned spins emerge: regions of gold and blue, tentative at first, then sweeping, then overwhelming. At T=1.5, nearly all spins point in one direction. The people have become one.

The model does not tell them which direction to align. Gold or blue — it does not matter. What matters is the alignment itself. That, the Rabbis teach, is what made the Torah possible.
