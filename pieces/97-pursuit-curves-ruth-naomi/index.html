Wherever You Go

עַמֵּךְ עַמִּי וֵאלֹהַיִךְ אֱלֹהָי
"Do not urge me to leave you or to turn back from following you; for where you go, I will go, and where you lodge, I will lodge. Your people shall be my people, and your God my God." — Ruth 1:16

At the crossroads of Moab, after the death of their husbands, Naomi releases her two daughters-in-law and turns back toward Bethlehem. Orpah weeps, kisses her, and goes home. Ruth clings — davkah, the same root used in Genesis for a man who cleaves to his wife. This is not sentiment. It is a covenant act.

Ruth's declaration is one of the few voluntary conversions recorded in all of Tanakh. The Rabbis in Yevamot 47b derive from this passage the template for receiving converts: a prospective convert must be informed of the obligations, pressed three times to reconsider, and only then accepted. Ruth enacts every element spontaneously. She does not wait to be asked.

The Mathematics of Pursuit

In the classic "mice problem," N mice stand at the vertices of a regular polygon. At each moment, every mouse turns directly toward the next and takes a small step. The paths they trace are equiangular spirals — not because anyone planned it, but because always heading straight toward a moving target produces a curve that rotates while converging.

This is Ruth's path to Bethlehem. She does not steer around obstacles or calculate the destination. She simply faces Naomi and walks. The path curves — through the fields of Boaz, through gleaning and threshing floors, through a redemption story that neither woman could have predicted — and yet the destination was always Bethlehem.

The Three Spirals

Ruth (gold) always turns toward Naomi (ivory), who is the fixed destination. Orpah (terracotta) begins the same pursuit but at the crossroads deflects — her direction slowly rotates outward, she fades, and vanishes beyond the edge of the canvas. Ruth's spiral completes itself, converging at Naomi.

The forty-nine four-agent pursuit systems behind the main story are the Omer harvest season: the same convergence pattern repeated forty-nine times, each day bringing the harvest — and Ruth — closer to Bethlehem.
