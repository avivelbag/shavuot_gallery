# Wherever You Go — Pursuit Curves of Ruth and Naomi

> "Do not urge me to leave you or to turn back from following you; for where you
> go, I will go, and where you lodge, I will lodge. Your people shall be my
> people, and your God my God. Where you die I will die, and there will I be
> buried." — Ruth 1:16–17

At the crossroads of Moab, after the death of their husbands, Naomi releases
her two daughters-in-law and turns back toward Bethlehem. Orpah weeps, kisses
her, and goes home. Ruth clings. The Hebrew word is *davkah* — the same root
as the verb used in Genesis for a man who cleaves to his wife. This is not
sentiment. It is a covenant act.

Ruth's declaration is one of the few voluntary conversions recorded in all of
Tanakh. The Rabbis in Yevamot 47b derive from this passage the template for
receiving converts: a prospective convert must be informed of the obligations,
pressed three times to reconsider, and only then accepted. Ruth's vow enacts
every element spontaneously. She does not wait to be asked; she announces it.
She adopts Naomi's people, Naomi's God, Naomi's burial place. The completeness
is the point.

## The Mathematics of Pursuit

In the classic "mice problem," N mice stand at the vertices of a regular
polygon. At each moment, every mouse turns directly toward the next mouse and
takes a small step. The paths they trace are equiangular spirals — not because
anyone planned it, but because always heading straight toward your target, when
your target is also moving, produces a curve that rotates while converging.

This is Ruth's path to Bethlehem. She does not steer around obstacles or
calculate the destination. She simply faces Naomi and walks. The path curves —
through the fields of Boaz, through gleaning and threshing floors, through a
redemption story that neither woman could have predicted — and yet the
destination was always Bethlehem.

## The Three Spirals

This artwork shows three agents: Ruth (warm gold) always turns toward Naomi
(ivory). Naomi is the fixed point, the destination around whom the spirals
form. Orpah (terracotta) begins the same pursuit, but at the crossroads she
deflects — her direction slowly rotates outward, she fades, and she vanishes
beyond the edge of the canvas. Ruth's spiral completes itself, converging at
Naomi's position.

Behind the main story, forty-nine four-agent pursuit systems fill the canvas
in a 7×7 grid. Each runs its own continuous loop: a square of four pursuers
spiraling inward, converging, then resetting. These are the forty-nine days of
the Omer — the harvest season through which Ruth worked in Boaz's fields,
gleaning behind the reapers, day after day. The mathematical repetition of the
pursuit pattern mirrors the liturgical repetition of the Omer count: the same
action, forty-nine times, each time bringing the harvest closer.

The convergence of Ruth and Naomi is not a meeting of equals traveling the
same road. It is one soul in pursuit of another, spiraling inward on a path
that is always direct but never straight — until they arrive together at the
same place, which was always the only destination.
