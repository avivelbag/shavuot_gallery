# Let My Teaching Drop as Dew

*Deuteronomy 32:2 — יַעֲרֹף כַּמָּטָר לִקְחִי, תִּזַּל כַּטַּל אִמְרָתִי — "Let my
teaching drop like rain, my speech distill as dew."*

The opening verse of Moses's farewell poem, Ha'azinu, makes a double comparison
that the rabbis refused to collapse into a single image. Rain and dew are not
the same thing, and Moses is deliberate: his *teaching* (likhi) will be like
rain, but his *speech* (imrati) — his most intimate utterance — will be like
dew. The distinction is crucial, and the Talmud presses it hard in **Eruvin 54b**.

**Eruvin 54b** asks why, if Torah can be compared to rain, the tradition leans
so heavily on dew. The Talmud's answer emerges from observation. Rain can damage.
It floods fields, erodes soil, destroys what is already planted. Dew never
damages. It arrives without force, absorbed silently into whatever it touches,
leaving nothing broken. Torah, therefore, must be like dew: it falls on the
learned and the unlearned, the strong vessel and the broken one, those who seek
it and those who have not thought to look. The Talmud states this plainly: just
as dew is available to everyone, so Torah words must be available to everyone.
The absence of exclusivity is the point. This is a radical claim: most spiritual
goods require initiation or preparation. But Torah as dew does not require
any special vessel. It falls.

**Rashi on Eruvin 54b** adds the observation that transforms the image entirely.
Rain announces itself. Clouds gather, thunder rolls, the air changes. You can
watch rain coming and prepare. Dew does not announce itself. It forms in the
night, in silence, and you discover it only in the morning when everything is
already wet. This is why Moses chose dew specifically for *imrati* — his
*speech*, the direct utterance, the most unmediated form of teaching. Torah
absorbed as dew is absorbed without performance, without the act of receiving
being made visible, without ceremony. It enters quietly.

This silence is theologically significant. The opposite of dew is not rain —
it is a fanfare. The scholar who takes up Torah with great display, announcing
the beginning of learning and the difficulty of learning and the prestige of
learning, has made it rain. The scholar who simply learns — who reads in the
night, who thinks while walking, who returns to the same text as naturally as
one returns to morning — has let it fall as dew. Torah absorbed this way becomes
part of the ground it touches. It cannot be extracted. The ground is changed.

Why did Moses choose this image in his final speech? Because Ha'azinu is the
penultimate utterance of his life, spoken by a man who knows he will not enter
the land. He is not teaching for himself. He is teaching so that what he
transmits will remain after he is gone. And dew is precisely the image of
transmission that does not require the original source to be present. Rain
requires clouds. Dew forms from the air itself, from moisture already present
in the world. Once Moses has spoken, Torah continues to form — from the existing
conditions of the community, the tradition, the accumulated words. He will be
gone. The teaching will still arrive, nightly, silently.

In this piece, glowing droplets fall from the deep indigo sky and accumulate on
an implied surface. Each drop falls without drama — no announcement, no
violence. At the ground they settle, and when enough have gathered, smaller
drops are absorbed into larger ones: the pool grows. Over time, the resting
positions of the settled drops are gently biased, and the accumulation traces
the shape of the word **תּוֹרָה** — not by force, but by the patient addition
of what arrives each morning to what was already there. The letter does not
appear suddenly. It emerges. That is the only way dew makes something.
